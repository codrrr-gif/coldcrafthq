## The Core Principle (Ries & Trout)

"Positioning is not what you do to a product. Positioning is what you do to the mind of the prospect." You don't create something new and different. You manipulate what's already in the mind, retying the connections that already exist.

### The Overcommunicated Society
The average person is bombarded with thousands of messages daily. The mind, as a defense mechanism, screens and rejects most information. The only way to cut through is with an oversimplified, sharply focused message.

**The mind accepts only what matches prior knowledge or experience.** It rejects everything else. You cannot change minds with more information — you can only work with what's already there.

### Mental Ladders
The mind organizes information in hierarchical ladders — one ladder per product category. Each rung holds a brand. The top rung is the leader. Most ladders have no more than seven rungs (Miller's Law).

**Implications**:
- If you're not on the ladder, you don't exist in the prospect's mind
- You cannot dislodge the brand above you by claiming you're better — the mind resists
- You must find an open rung, or create a new ladder (new category)

### The Power of Being First
"The easy way to get into a person's mind is to be first." First brand in a category gets disproportionate mindshare:
- First cola: Coca-Cola
- First copier: Xerox
- First rental car: Hertz
- First computer: IBM

Being first in the mind is not the same as being first in the market. What matters is being first to establish the position in the prospect's mind.

## Positioning Strategies by Market Position

### Leader Strategy
Leaders own the category in the prospect's mind. Key strategies:

1. **Cover all bets**: When a new concept emerges, the leader should immediately embrace it. Coca-Cola introducing Diet Coke rather than letting Diet Pepsi own the space.
2. **Multi-brand strategy**: Use different brand names for different positions. P&G with Tide, Cheer, Bold, Dash — each owns a different position in the detergent ladder.
3. **Broaden the category**: Instead of fighting competitors, expand the total market. The leader gains more from growing the pie than protecting their slice.
4. **Never say "We're #1"**: The prospect already knows. Saying it makes you look insecure. Instead, reinforce the original concept that made you first.

**The leader trap**: The biggest mistake leaders make is believing their success comes from marketing skill rather than from being first. This leads them to extend that "skill" into new categories where they have no position. Xerox in computers. IBM in copiers.

### Follower Strategy: Find the Creneau
A "creneau" is a hole or gap in the prospect's mind. Find it and fill it:

| Creneau Type | Strategy | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Size** | Go small when leaders are big, or vice versa | Volkswagen Beetle ("Think Small") |
| **Price** | Own the high end or low end | Michelob (high-price beer) |
| **Sex/Demographics** | Target an underserved identity | Marlboro (masculine cigarette) |
| **Time of day** | Own a specific occasion | Nyquil (nighttime cold medicine) |
| **Distribution** | Own a specific channel | L'eggs (pantyhose in supermarkets) |

**Critical rule**: The creneau must exist in the MIND, not just in the market. A gap in the factory is useless if there's no gap in the mind.

**The F.W.M.T.S. Trap**: "Forgot What Made Them Successful." Volkswagen owned the small-car position, then tried to sell big, fast, sporty cars — destroying their position entirely.

### Repositioning the Competition
When there's no creneau to fill, you must reposition the competition — change the way the prospect perceives the competitor:

- Tylenol repositioned aspirin: "If your stomach is sensitive... ask your doctor about Tylenol"
- Stolichnaya repositioned American vodkas: "Most American vodkas look Russian... Stolichnaya is different. It IS Russian"
- Scope repositioned Listerine: Exploited "medicine breath"

**Ethics of repositioning**: You must base repositioning on fact. You're not attacking the competitor unfairly — you're giving the prospect a new way to evaluate the existing products. The repositioning must be true, or it won't stick.

### The Line Extension Trap
"If you have clear positioning, resist the urge to extend it." Line extension is the most violated law in positioning. It occurs when a company takes a well-known brand name and puts it on a new product.

**Why it's tempting**: The existing name has awareness and goodwill. Why not leverage it?

**Why it destroys positioning**: The name is a rubber band. The more products you attach to it, the weaker it gets. When someone says "Heineken," you think beer. When someone says "Scott," do you think toilet paper, paper towels, napkins, or facial tissue? The name becomes meaningless.

**The bartender test**: If you asked a bartender for a "Budweiser," what would you get? A beer. If you asked for a "Michelob"? A beer. But if a brand means ten things, asking for it by name produces confusion, not clarity.

**When line extension works**: Only when the product is low-volume, has no competitors, has no advertising support, and lives in an undifferentiated commodity space. The moment competition arrives, the line extension becomes a liability.

**The reverse principle**: Narrow the focus to broaden the market. The more focused your position, the larger your share of the category.

## The Power of the Name

### Names That Work
- **Descriptive names** tell the prospect what the product does: Spray 'n Wash, DieHard, Head & Shoulders
- **The name is the hook** that hangs your brand on the prospect's product ladder
- **Generic names** are the strongest when you're first: Band-Aid, Kleenex, Scotch Tape. They become synonymous with the category

### Names That Fail
- **Coined/meaningless names** require massive spending to fill with meaning: Qyx, Xyience
- **Initials**: Eastern Airlines → Eastern (fine). But GE, IBM work ONLY because the full name was famous first. Starting with initials is building on quicksand
- **Inside-out names**: JCPenney batteries fail because the prospect thinks in product terms, not company terms. DieHard owns the battery rung; JCPenney doesn't

### Negative Names Can Be Positive
Margarine would have fared better as "soy butter." The name connotes imitation. Products that position against the category (unsweetened, unleaded, sugar-free) use negatives effectively because they reposition the entire category.

## Dunford's 10-Step Positioning Process

April Dunford's process provides the operational methodology for executing positioning. Unlike Ries/Trout's descriptive laws, Dunford gives you a step-by-step recipe.

**Core insight**: "Positioning is the act of deliberately defining how you are the best at something that a defined market cares a lot about."

**The Joshua Bell experiment**: A world-class violinist plays in a DC subway station. Almost nobody stops. Context (positioning) determines how people perceive even extraordinary quality. Without the right context, people don't know how to evaluate you.

### The 10 Steps (In Strict Order)

The order matters because each step builds on the previous:

**Step 1: Understand Who Loves Your Product (Best-Fit Customers)**
Start with your happiest, most successful customers. Don't start with the total addressable market — start with the customers who already get the most value. These are your best-fit customers.

**Step 2: Form a Positioning Team**
Positioning is a cross-functional exercise. Include founders, product, marketing, sales, and customer success. Diverse perspectives prevent blind spots.

**Step 3: Align on Vocabulary and Let Go of Baggage**
Your team carries assumptions from past positioning attempts. Explicitly acknowledge these and set them aside. Agree on terminology before proceeding.

**Step 4: List Your True Competitive Alternatives**
Ask best-fit customers: "What would you use if our product didn't exist?" These are your real competitive alternatives — not what you think they are from the inside. They might include "do nothing," "use a spreadsheet," or "hire an intern."

**Step 5: Isolate Your Unique Attributes**
What features or capabilities do you have that the alternatives lack? Be honest and specific. Don't list generic strengths — list things that are true ONLY of you versus the specific alternatives identified in Step 4.

**Step 6: Map Attributes to Value Themes**
Attributes are features. Value is what customers GET from those features. Cluster related value into themes. "Fast processing + real-time updates + instant alerts" might cluster into the value theme "Never miss a critical moment."

**Step 7: Determine Who Cares Most**
What characteristics make a customer care deeply about your differentiated value? These characteristics define your target segments — they're not demographics but rather situations, pain points, or workflows that make your unique value indispensable.

**Step 8: Pick a Market Frame of Reference**
Three positioning styles available:

| Style | When to Use | Risk |
|---|---|---|
| **Head to Head** | You can win on acknowledged criteria in an existing category | Must be demonstrably superior |
| **Big Fish, Small Pond** | You dominate a subsegment of a larger market | Market may be too small |
| **Create a New Game** | Your product is genuinely different from anything that exists | Must educate the market on the problem — expensive and slow |

**Head to Head**: Position in an existing category and compete on known criteria. Works when you have clear advantages on what the market already values.

**Big Fish, Small Pond**: Carve out a subsegment where your specific strengths are dominant. "CRM for investment banks" instead of "CRM." You inherit the category's buying criteria but win because you're purpose-built for that segment.

**Create a New Game**: Category creation. You must educate the market that the problem exists, that it's worth solving, and that your approach is the right one. Requires deep pockets and patience. The reward: you define the category criteria to match your strengths. The risk: you build the market and a well-funded fast follower steals leadership.

**Step 9: Layer On a Trend (Optional)**
If a current trend (AI, sustainability, remote work) naturally reinforces your positioning, use it. But trends must link clearly to your product AND your market — never trend for trend's sake. "Sharing economy for pets" tells prospects nothing; "marketplace for pet services" does.

**Warning**: A trend without a clear market category creates confusion. Always establish what you ARE before explaining why you're timely.

**Step 10: Capture Positioning in a Shareable Document**
Create a positioning canvas that maps: Product → Market Category → Competitive Alternatives → Unique Attributes → Value → Target Segments. This becomes the source of truth for all downstream messaging, sales stories, marketing, and product decisions.

## Positioning Style Decision Matrix

### Decision Criteria Table

Use this matrix to determine which of Dunford's three positioning styles fits your situation. Score each criterion, then follow the weight of evidence — not any single factor.

| Decision Criterion | Head to Head | Big Fish, Small Pond | Create a New Game |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Market maturity** | Mature, well-defined category with established buying criteria | Mature category but underserved segments exist | Nascent or nonexistent category; buyers don't have a mental model yet |
| **Competitive density** | Moderate — you can differentiate on 2-3 acknowledged criteria | High in the broad market, low in your target niche | Low overall, but incumbents may pivot fast once you educate the market |
| **Budget available** | Moderate — you need enough to compete visibly but not to educate | Lower — niche marketing is more capital-efficient | High — expect 3-5x the spend of category positioning to educate + capture |
| **Timeline to revenue** | 3-6 months — buyers already have budget and buying process | 1-3 months — niche buyers convert faster because the fit is obvious | 12-24 months — you must build awareness of the problem before you can sell the solution |
| **Risk tolerance** | Medium — you might lose on criteria you don't control | Low — smaller upside but higher probability of winning | High — you could build the market for someone else, or the market may never materialize |
| **Sales cycle** | Standard for the category — prospects know what they're buying | Shorter than category average — "built for us" accelerates trust | Longer — requires consultative education before evaluation begins |
| **Defensibility** | Low — if you win on features, competitors will copy | Medium — niche expertise and reputation create switching costs | High if you win — category creators define the rules, but only if you capture leadership |

### Real-World Examples by Style

**Head to Head**

1. **Pepsi vs. Coca-Cola (B2C)**: Pepsi never tried to create a new category. It positioned head-to-head on taste ("The Pepsi Challenge"), accepting Coke's category definition while claiming superiority on one criterion. It worked because taste is subjective and testable — they had a demonstrable claim.
2. **Figma vs. Sketch (B2B)**: Figma entered the UI design tool market and competed on acknowledged criteria (speed, collaboration, browser-based access). They didn't redefine design tools — they were better at what designers already valued.
3. **AMD Ryzen vs. Intel Core (B2C/B2B)**: AMD re-entered the CPU market head-to-head on performance-per-dollar, the criterion buyers already used. They didn't try to create a new processor category — they beat Intel on Intel's own scoreboard.

**Big Fish, Small Pond**

1. **ConvertKit vs. Mailchimp (B2B)**: ConvertKit didn't position as "better email marketing." It positioned as "email marketing for professional creators." The feature set was narrower, but every feature mapped to creator workflows — landing pages, sequences, paid newsletters. Creators felt seen.
2. **Liquid Death vs. water brands (B2C)**: Positioned not as "premium water" but as water for the counter-culture / punk / comedy audience. Same product (mountain water in a can), radically different segment. The niche was identity-driven, not feature-driven.
3. **Veeam vs. enterprise backup vendors (B2B)**: Entered the backup market positioned exclusively for virtualized environments (VMware). While Symantec and CommVault covered everything, Veeam owned the VMware backup rung and expanded from there.

**Create a New Game**

1. **HubSpot — "Inbound Marketing" (B2B)**: HubSpot didn't position as better marketing automation. They created the category "inbound marketing," defined its principles (attract vs. interrupt), wrote the book, built the conference, then sold the software to execute it. Took 5+ years and hundreds of millions in content investment to establish.
2. **Salesforce — "No Software" (B2B)**: Salesforce didn't position as better CRM. They created the "cloud CRM" category (symbolized by the "No Software" logo). They had to educate the market that software-as-a-service was safe, reliable, and preferable. The education phase lasted years.
3. **Red Bull — "Energy Drink" (B2C)**: The energy drink category didn't exist in Western markets. Red Bull had to educate consumers on what an energy drink was, when to drink it, and why they needed it. They spent years on experiential marketing (extreme sports, nightlife) before the category tipped into mainstream. Category creation cost was enormous, but they defined every criterion.

### Common Mistakes by Style

**Head to Head Mistakes**
- *Competing on criteria the leader defined and dominates.* If Salesforce defines "CRM" and you compete head-to-head on CRM criteria, you will lose because Salesforce wrote the scorecard. Only go head-to-head when your advantage is on criteria the market already values independently of the leader.
- *Claiming superiority without proof.* Head-to-head demands demonstrable evidence — benchmarks, side-by-side comparisons, third-party validation. "We're better" without receipts triggers the mind's rejection reflex.
- *Underestimating the leader's response.* Leaders have budgets, distribution, and brand equity. If your head-to-head advantage is copyable in 6-12 months, you'll win a battle and lose the war.

**Big Fish, Small Pond Mistakes**
- *Choosing a pond that's too small to sustain a business.* "CRM for left-handed orthodontists" is not a market. The niche must be large enough to build a real business AND adjacent to larger segments you can expand into later.
- *Niching on demographics instead of needs.* "Software for women" is not a position. "Software for freelance designers who manage 5-15 clients" is — because it describes a workflow with specific needs. Niche on the situation that makes your value indispensable, not on who the person is.
- *Failing to plan the expansion path.* Big Fish, Small Pond is a starting strategy, not a forever strategy. You must have a plan for which adjacent ponds to enter next. ConvertKit started with creators, then expanded to "creator economy" broadly. Without an expansion path, you cap your growth.

**Create a New Game Mistakes**
- *Category creation without the budget to educate.* If you cannot sustain 12-24 months of market education before meaningful revenue, do not attempt category creation. The graveyard is full of companies that were right about the category but ran out of money before the market caught up.
- *Naming the category after your product.* The category name must be bigger than you — it must describe a market that multiple vendors could serve. "Inbound marketing" worked because it described a movement. "HubSpot Marketing" would have failed because prospects don't want to join a vendor's category.
- *Educating without capturing.* You build awareness of the problem, the market agrees the problem exists, and then a well-funded competitor enters with a better product and captures the category you created. Mitigation: build community, set the evaluation criteria, and establish thought leadership so deeply that you ARE the category in the prospect's mind.

### Hybrid Approaches

Pure positioning styles are the exception. Most successful companies use hybrids that evolve over time.

**Big Fish → Head to Head (The Expansion Play)**: Start as Big Fish in a subsegment, prove dominance, then expand into the broader category head-to-head. Figma started as "collaborative design tool" (niche: teams that needed real-time collaboration) and expanded to challenge the entire UI design category. This is the most common and lowest-risk path to market leadership.

**Category Creation → Big Fish (The Beachhead Play)**: Create a new category but target a specific subsegment first to prove the concept. Salesforce created "cloud CRM" but initially targeted SMBs who couldn't afford on-premise Siebel. They proved the model in the small pond before going head-to-head upstream.

**Head to Head → Big Fish (The Retreat Play)**: If head-to-head competition fails or a stronger competitor enters, retreat to a defensible niche. This is not defeat — it's strategic repositioning. BlackBerry should have retreated to "secure enterprise mobile" when iPhone redefined consumer smartphones. They tried to compete head-to-head and lost everything.

**When to combine**: Use a hybrid when (a) your current resources don't match your long-term ambition — start in the pond you can win, then expand; or (b) you're creating a category but need revenue now — pick the subsegment that will adopt fastest and use them as proof for the broader market.

## Category Creation Deep Dive

### When Category Creation Is the Right Move

Do not attempt category creation unless you can check at least 5 of these 7 conditions:

- [ ] **No existing category fits.** When prospects evaluate you, they consistently struggle to compare you to anything. "It's like X meets Y" is a signal — they're trying to build a mental model and failing.
- [ ] **Existing categories actively hurt you.** Being placed in the current closest category triggers wrong expectations, wrong buying criteria, and wrong price anchors. If being called "CRM" makes prospects evaluate you on Salesforce's criteria and you lose, the category is the problem.
- [ ] **You have 18-24 months of runway minimum.** Category creation requires sustained investment before revenue scales. If you need revenue in 6 months, use Big Fish, Small Pond instead.
- [ ] **You have (or can build) a credible thought leadership platform.** Books, conferences, podcasts, research reports, frameworks. You need to be seen as the definitive expert on the problem, not just the vendor of a solution.
- [ ] **The problem is real and growing.** Category creation fails when the problem is niche or shrinking. You need secular tailwinds — a problem that is getting worse for more people over time.
- [ ] **You can name the category in 2-3 words that are self-explanatory.** "Inbound marketing," "revenue intelligence," "product-led growth." If the name requires explanation, it's not a category name — it's a tagline.
- [ ] **You're willing to let competitors into the category.** A category with one vendor is not a category — it's a product. You need others to validate the space. The goal is to be the leader of a recognized category, not the only inhabitant of an unrecognized one.

### The 3-Phase Process: Define, Educate, Dominate

**Phase 1: Define (Months 1-6)**

The goal is to name the category, articulate the problem it solves, and establish the evaluation criteria.

- Name the category. Test candidate names with prospects — the right name produces an immediate "oh, I need that" or "yes, that's a real problem." Wrong names produce "what does that mean?"
- Write the category manifesto. A 2,000-5,000 word document (or a book) that defines the problem, explains why existing solutions fail, describes the new approach, and outlines the criteria for evaluating solutions in this category. This is NOT a product pitch — it's a market thesis.
- Establish the evaluation criteria. The criteria must be ones your product wins on (obviously), but they must also be genuine — criteria that an honest analyst would agree matter. If your criteria feel rigged, the category loses credibility.
- Build the advisory circle. Recruit 5-10 industry practitioners who agree the problem is real. They become your validators, co-authors, and early evangelists.

**Phase 2: Educate (Months 6-18)**

The goal is to get prospects, analysts, and media to use your category name and agree the problem is real.

- Content engine: Produce research reports, case studies, benchmark data, and "state of the category" publications. Quantity matters here — you're filling a void in the market's mental model.
- Events: Host a category-specific conference or summit. HubSpot's INBOUND conference made "inbound marketing" feel like a movement, not a product pitch. Gainsight's Pulse did the same for "customer success."
- Analyst relations: Brief industry analysts. When Gartner or Forrester creates a Magic Quadrant or Wave for your category, the category is real. This is the single most powerful validation event for B2B category creation.
- Community: Build a community of practitioners (not users — practitioners of the discipline your category represents). Slack groups, forums, certification programs. The community becomes self-sustaining and generates content you don't have to create.

**Phase 3: Dominate (Months 18+)**

The goal is to be the default choice when a buyer decides they need a solution in your category.

- Capture the "first rung" on the new mental ladder. Per Ries/Trout, the first brand to establish position in a new category gets disproportionate mindshare. Your Phase 1-2 investment was to build the ladder. Phase 3 is ensuring you're on the top rung.
- Welcome competitors. When competitors enter, celebrate publicly. "The market is validating what we've been saying." More vendors = more legitimacy = bigger category = bigger opportunity for the leader.
- Set the narrative for the category's evolution. Publish the roadmap for where the category is going next. The category creator who defines the future criteria maintains leadership even as competitors catch up on current criteria.

### Budget and Timeline Expectations

Category creation costs 3-5x more than positioning within an existing category. Here is a realistic breakdown:

| Investment Area | Head to Head / Big Fish | Category Creation |
|---|---|---|
| Content marketing (annual) | $50K-$200K | $200K-$1M+ |
| Events | $10K-$50K (attend + sponsor) | $100K-$500K (create your own) |
| Analyst relations | $20K-$50K (briefings) | $50K-$200K (research sponsorship + briefings) |
| Thought leadership (books, keynotes) | Optional | Required — $50K-$150K |
| Community building | Nice-to-have | Must-have — $50K-$200K/year in tooling + moderation |
| Timeline to measurable pipeline impact | 3-6 months | 12-24 months |
| Total first-year investment premium | Baseline | 3-5x baseline |

These are B2B SaaS benchmarks. B2C category creation (like Red Bull creating "energy drinks") can cost 10-50x more due to mass-market education requirements.

### Successful and Failed Category Creations

**Successful: Gainsight and "Customer Success"**

- *Why it worked*: The problem (SaaS churn) was real, growing, and painful. Every SaaS CEO felt it. Gainsight named the discipline, wrote the book ("Customer Success" by Mehta, Steinman, Murphy), created the conference (Pulse), and built the community. By the time competitors entered, Gainsight WAS customer success in the buyer's mind.
- *Key lesson*: They invested in the discipline, not just the product. They trained customer success managers, published frameworks, and built certification programs. The category became bigger than the product.

**Successful: Drift and "Conversational Marketing"**

- *Why it worked*: David Cancel and Elias Torres bet that "forms are dead" and coined "conversational marketing" to describe real-time buyer engagement via chatbots. They published the book, built the community, and got analysts to recognize the category.
- *Key lesson*: They explicitly burned the boats — removing all forms from their own website as proof of conviction. The boldness attracted attention and forced prospects to engage with the new mental model.

**Failed: Google+ and "Social Layers"**

- *Why it failed*: Google tried to create a category ("social layer across Google products") when the market already had a clear mental model: "social network = Facebook." Prospects couldn't understand what category Google+ belonged to, and Google couldn't explain it in 2-3 words. The name meant nothing. The positioning was inside-out (what Google wanted) rather than outside-in (what users needed).
- *Key lesson*: Category creation fails when the problem isn't felt acutely enough to motivate learning a new mental model. People didn't feel pain with existing social networks sufficient to adopt a new paradigm.

**Failed: Quibi and "Quick Bites"**

- *Why it failed*: Quibi tried to create the "short-form premium mobile video" category with $1.75 billion in funding. The problem (boredom during short idle moments) was real but already solved by YouTube, TikTok, and Instagram. The category name required explanation. The budget was massive but the education never landed because prospects didn't feel the gap between existing solutions and what Quibi offered.
- *Key lesson*: Budget cannot substitute for a genuine gap in the market's mental model. If existing solutions are "good enough" in the prospect's mind, no amount of spending will create a new ladder.

### Connection to Ries/Trout's "Be First" Principle

Category creation is the ultimate expression of Ries/Trout's "be first" law. You are not being first in an existing category — you are creating the category so that you are, by definition, first. This is the strongest possible positioning move because:

1. **You define the ladder.** The evaluation criteria are yours to set.
2. **You occupy the top rung by default.** Every competitor who enters is, by definition, a follower.
3. **You get the "generic brand" advantage.** Just as Xerox = copier and Kleenex = tissue, the category creator often becomes synonymous with the category: HubSpot = inbound marketing, Salesforce = cloud CRM.

The risk is the inverse of the reward: if you fail to capture the top rung of the ladder you built, you've spent 3-5x more than a follower would have, and a better-funded or better-executing competitor takes the position you created.

## Naming Strategy Framework

### Name Evaluation Rubric

Score each candidate name 1-5 on each criterion. Minimum viable score is 24/35 (no individual score below 3).

| Criterion | 1 (Fail) | 3 (Acceptable) | 5 (Excellent) | Weight |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Memorability** | Requires multiple exposures to recall; sounds like competitors | Distinctive enough to remember after 2-3 exposures | Instantly memorable after one exposure; "sticky" sound or image | 1.5x |
| **Pronunciation** | Ambiguous pronunciation; people avoid saying it aloud | One obvious pronunciation; no stumbling | Rolls off the tongue; people enjoy saying it | 1x |
| **Domain availability** | Exact .com taken by an active business; alternatives are clunky | Exact .com available at premium price; or strong .io/.co available | Exact .com available or acquirable at reasonable cost | 1x |
| **Legal clearance** | Direct conflict with active trademark in same class | Minor conflicts in adjacent classes; manageable with counsel | No conflicts in relevant classes; clean TESS/TMview search | 1x |
| **Semantic associations** | Negative or confusing connotations in primary market | Neutral — no negative associations, no strong positive ones | Positive associations that reinforce the positioning | 1.5x |
| **Cross-cultural check** | Offensive or negative meaning in major market language | No negative meanings found in top 10 market languages | Positive or neutral across all target market languages | 1x |
| **Visual/typographic quality** | Awkward letter combinations; poor logo potential | Clean enough for professional design | Strong visual presence; distinctive letterforms; good logo potential | 0.5x |

### Name Types Ranked by Positioning Style

Different positioning styles favor different name types. This ranking reflects effectiveness, not absolute rules.

**For Head to Head positioning:**
1. **Descriptive names** — Tell the prospect exactly what you do. Reduces cognitive load in a crowded category where the buyer is comparison-shopping. Examples: Booking.com, General Electric, PayPal.
2. **Suggestive names** — Hint at the benefit without stating it literally. Creates slight differentiation while staying in the category frame. Examples: Slack (suggests ease), Pinterest (pin + interest).
3. **Coined names** — Only if you have massive ad spend to fill the name with meaning. Risky head-to-head because the name doesn't help the prospect place you on the existing mental ladder. Example: Accenture (succeeded due to $100M+ rebranding budget).

**For Big Fish, Small Pond positioning:**
1. **Segment-signaling names** — Names that tell the niche "this is for you." Examples: Shopify (shop + simplify), ConvertKit (conversion + toolkit for creators).
2. **Suggestive names** — Work well because the niche is small enough that word-of-mouth carries meaning. The name hints at the benefit; the community fills in the rest.
3. **Descriptive names** — Can work but risk sounding too generic for the niche. "Investment Bank CRM" is clear but not ownable.

**For Create a New Game positioning:**
1. **Coined/invented names** — You're building a new mental ladder. A coined name becomes synonymous with the category (Xerox, Google, Uber). The name IS the category.
2. **Evocative names** — Names that evoke the feeling or transformation of the new category. Examples: Salesforce (force for sales teams — evoked power), HubSpot (hub = center of activity).
3. **Descriptive names** — Dangerous for category creation because they anchor to existing mental models. If you're creating something new, a descriptive name pulls the prospect back to old categories.

### Tagline + Name Combination Strategies

The tagline's job depends on what the name does not do.

| Name Type | Tagline Job | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Coined name** (meaning-empty) | Tagline must convey positioning — it carries the entire message burden | "Accenture: High Performance Delivered" |
| **Descriptive name** (meaning-full) | Tagline adds emotional resonance or differentiator — the name already conveys category | "Booking.com: Booking.yeah" (adds excitement to a functional name) |
| **Suggestive name** (meaning-partial) | Tagline completes the picture — bridges from the hint to the full positioning | "Slack: Where Work Happens" (name suggests ease; tagline claims the workspace) |
| **Segment-signaling name** | Tagline broadens aspiration or articulates the unique value beyond the segment | "Shopify: Making Commerce Better for Everyone" (name signals merchants; tagline signals ambition) |

**Rules for name-tagline pairing:**
- Never let the tagline repeat what the name already says. Redundancy wastes a scarce resource (prospect attention).
- The name and tagline together must answer: "What is it?" and "Why should I care?" — in under 4 seconds of cognitive processing.
- Test the combination by asking: "If someone saw only the name and tagline on a billboard at highway speed, would they understand the core positioning?" If no, simplify.

### When to Rename vs. When to Reposition Under the Existing Name

**Rename when:**
- The current name actively contradicts the new positioning. Research In Motion → BlackBerry (the product name was stronger than the corporate name). Andersen Consulting → Accenture (had to legally separate from Arthur Andersen).
- The name carries unrecoverable negative associations. Phillip Morris → Altria. ValuJet → AirTran (after the Everglades crash).
- You're entering a new market where the name has no equity and creates wrong expectations.
- The name is legally contested or causes domain/trademark problems in expansion markets.

**Reposition under the existing name when:**
- The name has significant brand equity (unaided recall above 30% in your target market). Destroying that equity and rebuilding it is almost always more expensive than repositioning.
- The name is neutral — it doesn't help the new positioning, but it doesn't hurt it either. Apple went from computers to consumer electronics without renaming (they did drop "Computer" from the legal name).
- Your audience is B2B and relationships are name-attached. Enterprise buyers have vendor relationships, procurement records, and institutional memory tied to your name. Renaming creates friction in every active deal.
- You have fewer than 18 months of runway. Renaming is expensive (legal, design, announcement, SEO recovery, confused customers). If cash is tight, reposition under the existing name and rename later from a position of strength.

**The cost of renaming (B2B SaaS benchmark):**
- Legal (trademark filing, clearance): $15K-$50K
- Brand identity (logo, visual system): $30K-$150K
- Website migration + SEO recovery: $20K-$100K (expect 3-6 months of organic traffic dip)
- Announcement campaign: $25K-$100K
- Internal rollout (training, collateral, email signatures, everything): $10K-$50K
- **Total: $100K-$450K+, with 3-6 months of market confusion**

## Positioning Audit Template

Use this template to assess and strengthen an existing brand's positioning. Work through each section in order.

### Step 1: Current Position Assessment

**Customer perception survey questions** (deploy to 20-50 current customers minimum, split between best-fit and average-fit):

1. "When you describe [product/company] to a colleague, what do you say?" (Open text — captures actual positioning language in the customer's voice)
2. "If [product/company] didn't exist, what would you use instead?" (Reveals true competitive alternatives — per Dunford Step 4)
3. "What is the single biggest reason you chose [product/company] over alternatives?" (Surfaces the value that actually drives the decision, not what you think drives it)
4. "What is [product/company] best at?" (Reveals perceived unique strength — compare to your intended positioning)
5. "What is [product/company] worst at?" (Reveals positioning boundaries — what you should NOT claim)
6. "Which category would you place [product/company] in? What other products are in that category?" (Reveals market frame of reference and competitive set in the customer's mind)
7. "On a scale of 1-10, how easy is it to explain what [product/company] does to someone who has never heard of it?" (Score below 7 = positioning clarity problem)

**Analysis method**: Look for clustering in responses to Q1, Q3, and Q4. If 60%+ of respondents cluster around the same answer, your positioning is landing. If responses are scattered, you have a positioning problem — the mind has no clear rung for you.

### Step 2: Competitive Position Map (2x2 Perceptual Map)

**How to select the right axes:**

The two axes must represent: (a) the buying criteria that matter most to your target segment, and (b) the dimensions on which competitors are most differentiated from each other.

**Process:**
1. List all buying criteria from your VOC research (use Research & Market Analysis skill output).
2. Rank by importance to best-fit customers (from survey Q3 above).
3. Take the top 5 criteria. For each pair of criteria, plot competitors. The pair that produces the most spread (competitors occupy different quadrants) is your map.
4. If all competitors cluster in one quadrant, that axis pair doesn't differentiate — try another pair.

**Common axis pairs by market:**
- B2B SaaS: Ease of use ↔ Power/depth, and Vertical-specific ↔ Horizontal
- Professional services: Specialized ↔ Full-service, and Premium ↔ Accessible
- Consumer products: Premium ↔ Value, and Traditional ↔ Innovative
- B2B services: Strategic ↔ Tactical, and Bespoke ↔ Productized

**Plotting the map:**
- Plot competitors based on customer perception (not your internal view). Use survey data or review mining.
- Plot your brand twice: once for your intended position, once for the perceived position (from survey data). The gap between these two points is your positioning gap.
- Identify empty quadrants. An empty quadrant is a potential creneau — but verify it's empty because of opportunity, not because the market doesn't value that combination.

### Step 3: Position Strength Scorecard

Rate your current positioning 1-5 on each criterion. Be honest — inflate scores and the audit is worthless.

| Criterion | 1 (Critical weakness) | 3 (Adequate) | 5 (Best-in-class) | Your Score |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Clarity** — Can a new prospect understand your position in under 10 seconds? | Requires extended explanation; prospects frequently misunderstand category | Clear after a brief conversation; website communicates it reasonably | Instantly clear from name + tagline + first 50 words of website; prospects self-select accurately | ___ |
| **Distinctiveness** — Is your position meaningfully different from competitors? | Interchangeable with 3+ competitors; prospects see no difference | Different on 1-2 dimensions but similar overall feel | Occupies a position no competitor can claim; prospects spontaneously describe the difference | ___ |
| **Credibility** — Does the market believe your claim? | Claims exceed delivery; trust gap evident in reviews and churn | Claims match delivery for core features; some stretch in messaging | Claims understated relative to delivery; customers actively evangelize | ___ |
| **Relevance** — Does your position address what the target market cares about right now? | Position anchored to a problem that is declining in urgency | Position addresses a current need but not the most urgent one | Position hits the #1 pain point or aspiration for the target segment | ___ |
| **Consistency** — Is the position reinforced across all touchpoints? | Website says one thing, sales says another, product experience says a third | Core messaging is aligned; some touchpoints are off-message | Every touchpoint — website, sales pitch, onboarding, support, invoicing — reinforces the same position | ___ |
| **Defensibility** — Can competitors easily copy your position? | Position based on features that competitors can replicate in 6 months | Position based on a combination of features + expertise + brand that would take 1-2 years to replicate | Position based on structural advantages (network effects, data moats, category ownership) that are nearly impossible to replicate | ___ |

**Scoring interpretation:**
- **26-30**: Strong positioning. Focus on consistency and defense.
- **20-25**: Functional positioning. Identify the weakest criterion and address it specifically.
- **15-19**: Positioning needs significant work. Revisit Dunford's 10 steps from scratch.
- **Below 15**: Positioning is a liability. It is actively costing you deals. Treat as urgent.

### Step 4: Gap Analysis — Intended vs. Perceived Position

Create a two-column comparison using data from Steps 1-3.

| Dimension | Intended Position (what you want) | Perceived Position (what customers say) |
|---|---|---|
| Category | How you define your market category | What category customers place you in |
| Primary value | The #1 value you believe you deliver | The #1 value customers cite (survey Q3) |
| Competitive alternative | Who you think you compete with | Who customers say they'd use instead (survey Q2) |
| Target segment | Who you're trying to reach | Who is actually buying and succeeding |
| Key differentiator | What you believe makes you unique | What customers say you're best at (survey Q4) |

**Interpreting the gaps:**
- **Category gap**: If customers place you in a different category than intended, your market frame of reference is not landing. This is the most dangerous gap — it means prospects are evaluating you on the wrong criteria.
- **Value gap**: If the #1 value customers cite is different from what you lead with, you're either under-messaging your true strength or over-claiming a benefit you don't deliver.
- **Competitive gap**: If customers cite different alternatives than you expect, your real competitive battle is elsewhere. This directly affects differentiation strategy.
- **Segment gap**: If your best customers don't match your target segment, either your targeting is wrong or your product has evolved beyond your original positioning.
- **Differentiator gap**: If customers cite a different strength than you claim, you may be leaving your strongest card unplayed.

### Step 5: Repositioning Decision Framework

**When to reposition:**
- Gap analysis reveals 3+ significant gaps (intended and perceived positions diverge on most dimensions)
- Position strength scorecard is below 20
- Win rates have declined 15%+ over two quarters with no product or pricing changes
- A major competitor has entered your position and has more resources to hold it
- Your product has evolved significantly and the current position constrains growth

**When to double down on current positioning:**
- Gap analysis shows alignment (1 or fewer significant gaps)
- Position strength scorecard is 20+
- Win rates are stable or increasing
- Customer retention is strong among the target segment
- The position is defensible and no credible competitor is challenging it

**The repositioning cost test**: Estimate the cost of repositioning (new messaging, website redesign, sales retraining, market re-education, potential customer confusion) and compare it to the cost of staying (lost deals from poor positioning, inability to enter new segments, competitive vulnerability). Reposition only when the cost of staying exceeds the cost of moving.

## Positioning Effectiveness Metrics

### Unaided Brand Recall Benchmarks

Unaided recall = "When you think of [category], what brands come to mind?" — measured without any prompting.

| Market Context | Good | Strong | Dominant |
|---|---|---|---|
| **B2B SaaS, niche category** (<500 vendors) | 5-10% of target market | 15-25% | 30%+ |
| **B2B SaaS, broad category** (500+ vendors) | 2-5% | 8-15% | 20%+ |
| **B2C, niche** | 10-20% | 25-40% | 50%+ |
| **B2C, mass market** | 5-10% | 15-30% | 40%+ |
| **Professional services, local market** | 15-25% | 30-50% | 60%+ |

**How to measure**: Survey 100+ prospects in your target segment (not customers — prospects who have not bought from you). Ask: "When you think of [your category], what companies come to mind? List up to 5." Your brand's mention rate is your unaided recall.

**The "first mention" metric**: Being mentioned at all is good. Being mentioned first is 3-5x more valuable — first mention correlates with being on the top rung of the mental ladder.

### Message Association Testing

Test whether your intended positioning message has actually reached the market's mind.

**Method:**
1. Survey 50+ target prospects (mix of customers and non-customers).
2. Show them your brand name (logo + name, no tagline or description).
3. Ask: "What 3 words or phrases come to mind when you see this brand?"
4. Code responses into your intended positioning themes and non-intended themes.

**Scoring:**
- **60%+ responses match intended positioning**: Strong association. Positioning is landing.
- **40-60% match**: Moderate. Positioning is partially landing — identify which touchpoints are diluting the message.
- **Below 40%**: Weak. The market has a different mental model of your brand than you intend. Revisit positioning execution (not necessarily the positioning itself — it may be right but poorly communicated).

**Advanced method — competitive message association:**
Show respondents 4-5 brand names (yours + competitors) and a list of 8-10 positioning statements. Ask them to match each statement to the brand it best describes. This reveals not just your association strength but whether competitors are stealing your intended position.

### Win/Loss Analysis Framework (Positioning-Specific)

Add these questions to your standard sales debrief for every closed-won and closed-lost opportunity:

**For closed-won:**
1. "What category did the buyer place us in during their initial evaluation?" (Validates market frame of reference)
2. "Which alternatives did they seriously consider?" (Validates competitive alternatives from Dunford Step 4)
3. "What was the #1 reason they chose us?" (Validates whether your differentiated value is the actual deciding factor)
4. "Was there a moment in the sales process where our positioning 'clicked' — where they suddenly understood why we were different?" (Identifies the most effective positioning touchpoint)
5. "Did they use our language or their own language to describe why they chose us?" (If they use your language, positioning has been internalized; if their own, capture it — it may be better)

**For closed-lost:**
1. "What category did they place us in?" (Did wrong categorization lead to wrong evaluation criteria?)
2. "Who did they choose instead, and why?" (Is the loss a positioning failure or a product/price failure?)
3. "At what stage did we lose? Early (not shortlisted), mid (evaluated but not chosen), or late (chosen then reversed)?" (Early loss = awareness or positioning problem; mid loss = differentiation problem; late loss = proof or pricing problem)
4. "Did they express confusion about what we do or how we're different?" (Direct signal of positioning failure)
5. "Did they compare us to competitors we don't consider competitive?" (Reveals a market frame of reference mismatch)

**Analysis cadence**: Review win/loss data quarterly. Look for patterns:
- If closed-lost consistently shows category confusion → reposition market frame of reference
- If closed-lost consistently shows "chose competitor X on criterion Y" → either strengthen that criterion or reposition to change the evaluation criteria
- If closed-won consistently cites a different reason than your lead messaging → consider leading with what actually wins

### Positioning Decay Indicators

Positioning is not permanent. Watch for these signals that indicate it is time to review:

| Signal | What It Means | Urgency |
|---|---|---|
| **Win rate drops 10%+ over two quarters** (no product/price change) | Competitive positioning has shifted — someone is taking your rung or the criteria have changed | High — audit within 30 days |
| **Sales cycle lengthens 20%+** | Prospects are struggling to understand or evaluate you, suggesting positioning is not doing its job of simplifying the decision | Medium — audit within 60 days |
| **Inbound leads increasingly misfit** (wrong segment, wrong use case) | Your positioning is attracting the wrong mental association — the market is categorizing you incorrectly | High — this means your marketing is spending money to acquire leads that can't convert |
| **Best-fit customers start churning** | If your core segment is leaving, the positioning-product alignment may have broken — either the product evolved away from the position, or the segment's needs shifted | Critical — this is an existential threat to the positioning foundation |
| **"What do you do?" question increases** | When you hear this more often from prospects who should know, your positioning clarity is eroding | Medium — often caused by inconsistent messaging across touchpoints |
| **Competitor messaging converges on your position** | When 2+ competitors are claiming the same position, the position loses distinctiveness and becomes table stakes | High — reposition to a more defensible claim or find a new dimension of differentiation |
| **Your team can't articulate the position consistently** | If your own people can't say it the same way twice, the market definitely can't | Critical — fix internal alignment before any external positioning work |

## Positioning Over Time

Positioning is not permanent. Review every 6 months or when:
- A major competitor enters or exits the market
- Technology shifts change what's possible
- Customer attitudes shift (regulatory, economic, cultural)
- Your product capabilities have fundamentally changed

**But resist change for its own sake.** The landscape is littered with companies that abandoned working positions to chase trends. Maytag selling reliability, Disney selling fantasy, Avon calling — consistency wins. "The programs of those who kept at what they did best and held their ground have been immensely successful."

## The Six Questions Every Position Must Answer

1. **What position do you own today?** (Not what you want — what the prospect actually thinks)
2. **What position do you WANT to own?** (Pick the one position you can own exclusively)
3. **Who must you outgun?** (Who owns or contests the position you want?)
4. **Do you have the resources?** (The marketing budget to establish and maintain the position?)
5. **Can you stick with it?** (Positioning requires long-term consistency — not campaign cycles)
6. **Does your creative match your position?** (Does every communication reinforce the same position?)
