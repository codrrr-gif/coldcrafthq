## Why Story Works (Miller)

The human brain is wired for story. Story is a sense-making device — it organizes information into a pattern the brain can process and remember. When a brand communicates without story structure, it creates noise. When it communicates with story structure, it creates music.

**"If you confuse, you'll lose."** The number one reason companies fail at marketing is not bad products or bad advertising — it's unclear messaging. Clarity beats cleverness every time.

**The survival mechanism**: The brain's primary job is helping us survive and thrive. Every piece of information the brain processes gets filtered through two questions:
1. Can this help me survive and thrive?
2. How much energy (calories) does it cost to process this?

If a message requires too much cognitive energy to decode, the brain ignores it — regardless of how good the underlying product is. Story reduces the cognitive load because the brain already knows the pattern.

### The Two Critical Mistakes
1. **Positioning the brand as the hero**: When a brand talks about itself — its founding story, its values, its journey — it positions itself as the hero. But the customer doesn't care about the brand's journey. The customer is the hero of THEIR OWN story and is looking for a GUIDE.

2. **Failing to define what you offer in terms of survival**: If people can't figure out how your product helps them survive (save time, money, build social connections, gain status, accumulate resources, find meaning), they'll ignore you.

## The SB7 Framework

### Element 1: A CHARACTER (The Customer)
Every story starts with a character who wants something. The brand's job is to define what the customer wants — clearly, simply, in one sentence.

**Rules for defining the want**:
- It must be relevant to their survival (broadly defined: financial, relational, physical, spiritual)
- It should create a story question: "Will the customer get what they want?"
- It must be a SINGLE desire — multiple desires split the story and confuse the audience
- The desire should be a "desire of the flesh" — concrete and tangible, not abstract

**Examples**:
- Financial advisor: "Retire with enough money to enjoy it"
- Software company: "A hassle-free way to manage your team"
- Restaurant: "A meal everybody at the table will love"

### Element 2: HAS A PROBLEM
Stories don't really get interesting until the character encounters a problem. Problems are the hook — they create the tension that keeps the audience engaged.

**Three Levels of Problem** (this is the critical insight):

| Level | Definition | Example (Car Buying) |
|---|---|---|
| **External** | The tangible, surface-level problem | "I need a reliable car" |
| **Internal** | How the external problem makes the customer FEEL | "I feel overwhelmed and intimidated by the car-buying process" |
| **Philosophical** | Why this situation is fundamentally WRONG | "Buying a car shouldn't be this confusing. I deserve to be treated with respect" |

**"Companies tend to sell solutions to external problems, but customers buy solutions to INTERNAL problems."** This is the single most important insight in StoryBrand. The external problem is the reason customers START looking. The internal problem is the reason they BUY.

CarMax succeeds not because it sells better cars (external) but because it eliminates the anxiety and distrust of the car-buying experience (internal). The philosophical problem ("Car buying should be fair and transparent") gives the purchase a sense of meaning.

**The Villain**: Every problem has a source — the villain. The villain should be:
- A root cause (not a symptom)
- Relatable (customers immediately recognize it)
- Singular (one clear villain, not a committee of problems)
- Real (must be a genuine antagonist, not manufactured)

### Element 3: AND MEETS A GUIDE (The Brand)
**The brand is the guide, not the hero.** This is the fundamental role shift. The guide is the character in every story who helps the hero win: Yoda to Luke, Haymitch to Katniss, Gandalf to Frodo.

Two qualities define a guide:

**1. Empathy**: The guide understands the hero's pain. They've been there. They get it.
- Express empathetic statements: "We understand how frustrating it is when..."
- Demonstrate that you've felt what they feel
- Never skip empathy and jump straight to competence — it feels cold and salesy

**2. Authority**: The guide has the competence to actually help. Four ways to demonstrate authority:
- **Testimonials**: Other heroes who succeeded with this guide
- **Statistics**: Data that proves competence ("10,000 customers served")
- **Awards/Credentials**: Third-party validation
- **Logos**: Brands you've worked with (social proof)

**The empathy-authority balance**: Too much empathy without authority = you seem like a sympathetic friend, not a capable guide. Too much authority without empathy = you seem arrogant and self-focused (the brand-as-hero mistake). Always lead with empathy, then back it with authority.

**The Jay Z trap**: Jay Z launched Tidal by gathering famous musicians on stage to talk about how Tidal would help THEM (the artists). Nobody cared because artists aren't the customer's hero — the customer is. Brands that talk about themselves are positioning themselves as the hero.

### Element 4: WHO GIVES THEM A PLAN
Customers won't take action until they see a clear path. The plan is the bridge between the problem and the solution. It reduces perceived risk.

**Two types of plans**:

**Process Plan** (Steps to Follow):
- Give the customer 3-6 clear steps to engage with you
- Steps reduce confusion and increase confidence
- Example: "1. Schedule a call → 2. Get a custom plan → 3. Start seeing results"
- Even if the actual process is complex, simplify it to 3-6 steps for communication

**Agreement Plan** (Promises You Make):
- A list of agreements that address the customer's fears
- Example: CarMax's "no-haggle pricing" is an agreement plan — it promises the customer won't be taken advantage of
- Agreement plans work because they directly counter the internal problem

**Naming your plan** adds credibility: "The Easy Installation Plan" or "Our Quality Guarantee." Named plans feel more intentional and trustworthy than generic steps.

### Element 5: AND CALLS THEM TO ACTION
Characters in stories don't take action on their own — they must be challenged by the guide. Your brand must clearly challenge the customer to take action.

**Two types of calls to action**:

**Direct CTA**: Ask for the sale. Buy Now. Schedule a Call. Register Today.
- Must be bold, clear, and obvious
- Should appear multiple times on every page
- Never bury it, never make it subtle
- There should be ONE primary direct CTA — not three competing options

**Transitional CTA**: Offer value in exchange for engagement. Free PDF. Assessment. Webinar.
- Deepens the relationship without asking for the sale
- Positions you as the guide (you're helping before asking)
- Collects contact information for nurturing
- Can be as simple as a free PDF that addresses their internal problem

**The passivity problem**: Most brands are too passive with their CTAs. They put a small "Learn More" button in the corner and wonder why nobody clicks. The guide must be direct. "Nobody will be offended if you ask for the sale. People are used to being asked."

### Element 6: THAT HELPS THEM AVOID FAILURE
Stories must have stakes. If nothing bad can happen, there's no tension, no urgency, no reason to act.

**Loss aversion (Kahneman & Tversky)**: People are 2-3x more motivated to avoid loss than to achieve gain. Brands that don't communicate potential failure lose the most powerful motivational tool available.

**How to use failure stakes**:
- Name what the customer stands to LOSE if they don't act
- Be specific but not overwhelming — "a little salt in the recipe"
- Don't dwell on failure (that's fear-mongering) — hint at it, then pivot to success
- Examples: "Don't let another satisfying retirement year slip away." "Stop wasting money on marketing that doesn't work."

**The Allstate "Mayhem" approach**: Personify the failure as a character. Make the stakes vivid and concrete. Then position your product as the path away from that failure.

### Element 7: AND ENDS IN A SUCCESS
Paint a vivid picture of what life looks like AFTER the customer uses your product. Never assume customers can imagine the resolution — you must tell them explicitly.

**Three categories of successful resolution**:

**1. Status**: The customer gains status, power, or position
- "Join 10,000 industry leaders who..."
- Premium packaging, exclusive access, social proof
- Completion of something (a badge, certification, membership)

**2. Completeness**: Something lacking is now whole
- Reduced anxiety, reduced workload, time reclaimed
- "Finally, a ___ that actually works"
- Before/after transformations

**3. Self-Acceptance / Self-Realization**: The ultimate transformation
- "Become the leader you were meant to be"
- Aspirational identity (Dove's Real Beauty, Under Armour's "I Will What I Want")
- The hero transforms — they're not who they were at the start of the story

**Show the contrast**: The most powerful success endings juxtapose the before (the problem world) with the after (the resolution world). Make both vivid.

## Brand Participation in Transformation

The most powerful brands don't just sell products — they participate in the customer's identity transformation. They help the customer become someone NEW.

**The identity transformation formula**:
- FROM: [Who the customer is before] → TO: [Who the customer becomes after]
- Example: "From overwhelmed founder → To confident CEO with a clear strategy"
- Example: "From anxious investor → To savvy portfolio manager"

This maps to a deeper human need: we all want to become a better version of ourselves. Brands that facilitate this transformation create lifelong loyalty.

## The StoryBrand BrandScript

The BrandScript is the tool for capturing your SB7 Framework. It's a single page with seven sections:

```
CHARACTER: [Who is the customer and what do they want?]
PROBLEM:
  External: [Tangible problem]
  Internal: [How it makes them feel]
  Philosophical: [Why it's wrong]
  Villain: [Source of the problem]
GUIDE:
  Empathy: [Statement showing you understand]
  Authority: [Evidence of competence]
PLAN:
  Process: [3-6 steps]
  Agreement: [Promises that counter fears]
CALL TO ACTION:
  Direct: [Primary ask]
  Transitional: [Value-first offer]
FAILURE: [What the customer loses if they don't act]
SUCCESS: [Vivid picture of life after transformation]
IDENTITY TRANSFORMATION: [From ___ to ___]
```

## Complete BrandScript Worked Example

**Brand**: FitShift — a fitness app for busy professionals who want to stay in shape without living at the gym.

### The Completed BrandScript

```
CHARACTER:
A busy professional (manager, founder, consultant) aged 30-50 who wants to stay
fit and energized without sacrificing career performance or family time.

  Single desire: "Get back in shape without rearranging my entire life."

PROBLEM:
  Villain: The "all-or-nothing" fitness culture that says you need 90-minute gym
  sessions, strict meal prep, and 5am wake-ups to be healthy — a standard that
  makes busy people feel like failures before they even start.

  External: "I don't have time for a real workout program. My schedule is
  unpredictable — meetings run over, travel comes up, kids get sick."

  Internal: "I feel guilty and embarrassed. I used to be in shape. I know what
  I should be doing, but I can't seem to make it stick. Every failed attempt
  makes me feel less disciplined, less capable — like I'm losing a battle
  with myself."

  Philosophical: "Staying healthy shouldn't require quitting your career or
  becoming a full-time athlete. You shouldn't have to choose between being
  successful and being healthy."

GUIDE:
  Empathy: "We've been the person who buys the gym membership in January,
  goes three times, and then watches it auto-renew for six months while the
  guilt compounds. We built FitShift because we were tired of fitness programs
  designed for people who already have time to work out."

  Authority:
  - 47,000+ professionals currently active on the platform
  - Average user completes 4.2 workouts per week (vs. 1.8 industry average
    for fitness apps after 90 days)
  - 91% retention rate at 6 months (industry average: 29%)
  - Featured in Fast Company, Wired, Men's Health
  - Built by certified trainers with corporate performance backgrounds

PLAN:
  Process Plan ("The 3-Step Reset"):
    1. Take the 5-minute Fitness Profile — tell us your schedule, equipment
       access, and goals
    2. Get your adaptive weekly plan — workouts that flex around your real
       calendar (15-30 min each, adjusts when meetings shift)
    3. Track your streak, not your six-pack — we measure consistency, energy
       levels, and recovery, not just reps

  Agreement Plan ("The FitShift Promise"):
    - No workout longer than 30 minutes. Ever.
    - Your plan adapts to schedule changes in real time — miss a slot,
      and we reschedule, not guilt-trip
    - Cancel anytime. No contracts, no friction, no "talk to retention" calls
    - You'll never see "you missed a workout!" shame notifications

CALL TO ACTION:
  Direct: "Start Your Free Week"
  Transitional: "Download the Busy Professional's 15-Minute Workout Guide (PDF)"

FAILURE:
  - Another year passes. The annual physical gets worse. The doctor starts
    mentioning medication.
  - Energy keeps dropping. You're the parent who's too tired to play with
    their kids on Saturday.
  - Confidence erodes — you avoid photos, dodge social events, buy bigger
    clothes and tell yourself you'll "start Monday."
  - The gap between who you are and who you see yourself as keeps widening.

SUCCESS:
  - You finish a Tuesday workout in 22 minutes before your 9am standup —
    and you actually feel energized, not drained.
  - Your spouse notices you have more energy. Your kids notice you say "yes"
    to the trampoline park instead of "maybe next time."
  - At the next company offsite, you're the one who suggests the hike.
  - You stop thinking about fitness as a thing you need to "get back to" —
    it's just part of your week now, like brushing your teeth.
  - The annual physical? Numbers are moving in the right direction.

IDENTITY TRANSFORMATION:
  FROM: A busy professional who used to be in shape and feels guilty about
        letting it slip — someone who starts programs but can't sustain them
  TO:   A consistently active professional who stays fit without drama —
        someone who's proven that health and ambition aren't mutually exclusive
```

### BrandScript → Copy Translation

**Homepage headline** (External problem + Success):
> "Get Back in Shape in 30 Minutes a Day — Without Rearranging Your Life"

**Homepage subheadline** (Internal problem):
> "Fitness programs designed for people who actually have jobs, families, and zero interest in becoming gym influencers."

> "The reason your last fitness app collected dust"

**Ad headline** (Villain + Plan):
> "Stop following workout plans built for people with 2 hours to kill. FitShift adapts to YOUR calendar — not the other way around."

## The One-Liner Formula

A distilled version of the BrandScript for elevator pitches, taglines, and bios:

**Formula**: Character + Problem + Plan + Success

**Structure**: "We help [character] who [problem] by [plan] so they can [success]."

**Examples**:
- "Most business leaders don't know how to talk about their company, so we created a framework that helps them simplify their message, create great marketing material, connect with customers, and grow their business."
- "We provide busy moms with a short, meaningful workout they can use to stay healthy and have renewed energy."

**Testing the one-liner**: When you say it to someone, do they:
1. Look interested? (It's working)
2. Ask a follow-up question? (It's good)
3. Ask for your business card? (It's great)

Repeat it until it feels borderline excessive. "Amateurs ramble on; professionals serve their audience." Your one-liner is your hit song — sing it every night.

## The StoryBrand Website Wireframe

Miller provides a specific website structure based on the SB7 Framework:

### Above the Fold (First Screen)
1. **Header image** that shows an aspirational identity (happy customers, success outcome)
2. **Headline** that clearly states what you offer (external problem + success)
3. **Direct CTA button** (prominent, contrasting color)
4. **Transitional CTA** (smaller, secondary)

**The "grunt test"**: Could a caveman look at your website and immediately answer: (1) What do you offer? (2) How will it make my life better? (3) What do I need to do to buy it? If not, revise.

### Below the Fold
5. **Stakes section**: What failure looks like without your product
6. **Value proposition**: 3-4 benefits (tied to internal problems)
7. **Guide section**: Empathy statement + authority proof
8. **Plan section**: 3-step process
9. **Explanatory paragraph**: Your one-liner expanded
10. **More testimonials/proof**
11. **Bottom CTA**: Repeat the direct call to action

## Three-Level Problem Discovery Process

Most BrandScripts fail at the problem level. Brands default to external problems because they're obvious. But external problems are commoditized — every competitor solves the same external problem. Internal and philosophical problems are where differentiation and emotional resonance live.

### Step 1: Interview Questions That Surface Internal Problems

These five questions reliably pull internal-problem language out of customers. Use in customer interviews, survey open-ends, or review mining.

**Question 1: "What was happening in your life right before you started looking for a solution?"**
- Surfaces the emotional trigger, not the logical one. People don't google "project management software" because they love software — they google it because they just dropped the ball on a client deliverable and felt incompetent.

**Question 2: "What had you already tried that didn't work? How did that make you feel?"**
- The "how did that make you feel" follow-up is where internal problems live. Failed solutions create frustration, self-doubt, and distrust — all powerful internal problems.

**Question 3: "If you could describe the frustration in one sentence to a friend over a beer, what would you say?"**
- Forces informal language. Removes the corporate filter. The sentence they give you is often close to usable copy. Listen for emotion words: annoyed, embarrassed, overwhelmed, stupid, behind, stuck.

**Question 4: "What were you most afraid would happen if you didn't solve this?"**
- Reveals the failure stakes AND the internal problem simultaneously. Fear of looking incompetent (internal) is different from fear of losing money (external).

**Question 5: "What would solving this mean for how you see yourself?"**
- Points directly at identity transformation and philosophical territory. The answer usually contains both the internal problem (current negative self-image) and the success state (desired self-image).

### Step 2: Common Internal-Problem Patterns by Industry

| Industry | Typical External Problem | Common Internal Problem (what they actually feel) | Voice-of-Customer Language |
|---|---|---|---|
| **SaaS / B2B Software** | "Our tools are disconnected and processes are slow" | Feeling incompetent or behind — "Every other company seems to have figured this out except us." Anxiety about being the bottleneck. Fear of looking unsophisticated to the board. | "I'm duct-taping everything together." "I feel like I'm constantly firefighting." "My team thinks I don't have a plan." |
| **E-commerce (DTC)** | "I need [product category]" | Decision paralysis — "There are 50 options and I can't tell which one is actually good." Fear of wasting money on the wrong choice. Embarrassment if the purchase doesn't work out. | "I've been burned before." "I don't trust the reviews." "I just want someone to tell me what to buy." |
| **Professional Services** | "I need help with [tax/legal/financial/marketing]" | Feeling out of control — "I don't understand enough to know if I'm being taken advantage of." Vulnerability of handing important decisions to a stranger. Fear of looking stupid for not knowing. | "I don't even know what questions to ask." "Last time I felt talked down to." "I need someone who explains things in plain English." |
| **B2B Services / Agencies** | "We need more leads / better marketing / improved operations" | Feeling like a fraud — "We should be further along by now." Pressure from stakeholders. Fear that hiring help is an admission of failure. Career risk if this doesn't work. | "My boss is asking why we're not growing faster." "I've been promising results for two quarters." "If this doesn't work, it's on me." |

### Step 3: The "Shouldn't" Test for Philosophical Problems

Philosophical problems always contain the word "shouldn't" or "should." They express a moral judgment about how the world OUGHT to work versus how it currently works.

**The formula**: "[Situation] shouldn't be [negative adjective]. People deserve [positive state]."

**Process**:
1. State the external problem plainly
2. Ask: "Why is it WRONG that this problem exists?"
3. Frame the answer as a "should/shouldn't" statement
4. Verify it resonates at a gut level — philosophical problems feel righteous, not logical

**Examples**:

| External Problem | Philosophical Problem (using the "shouldn't" test) |
|---|---|
| Small businesses struggle to afford legal help | Small business owners shouldn't need a $500/hr lawyer to protect their livelihood |
| Managing projects requires juggling 5 tools | Teams shouldn't need an engineering degree to collaborate on a simple project |
| Healthy food is expensive and inconvenient | Feeding your family real food shouldn't require a second income or a personal chef |
| SaaS pricing is confusing and opaque | You shouldn't need a spreadsheet to figure out what software actually costs |
| Hiring a contractor is a gamble | Trusting someone with your home shouldn't feel like rolling the dice |

**When the philosophical problem is strong**: it turns customers into evangelists. People don't evangelize products that solve external problems ("I love my project management tool"). They evangelize brands that align with their values ("Nobody should have to deal with this — you need to try this").

### Step 4: Villain Identification Framework

The villain is the root cause of the three-level problem. It is NOT the customer, and it is NOT a vague concept. It must be specific and nameable.

**Villain identification decision tree**:

```
Start: What is the customer's external problem?
  ↓
Ask: What CAUSES this problem?
  ↓
Is the cause a specific, recognizable force the customer would nod at?
  → YES: That's your villain. Name it.
  → NO: Go one level deeper. What's behind the cause?
      ↓
      Can you personify or name this deeper cause?
        → YES: That's your villain.
        → NO: You're likely too abstract. Return to customer language
              and find what they BLAME.
```

**Villain quality test**:

| Criterion | Good Villain | Bad Villain |
|---|---|---|
| Specificity | "The all-or-nothing fitness culture" | "Being unhealthy" |
| Recognizability | "The 'hustle harder' mentality that burns out founders" | "Challenges in business" |
| Externality | "Confusing insurance jargon designed to keep you in the dark" | "You don't understand insurance" (blames the customer) |
| Rootness | "The outdated hiring process that filters for credentials over ability" | "Bad hires" (that's a symptom) |

### Step 5: Problem Placement Mapping

Once you have all three levels plus the villain, here is where each one goes:

| Problem Level | Primary Copy Location | Why It Goes There |
|---|---|---|
| **External problem** | **Headline / Hero section** | The external problem is the HOOK — it's the search term, the reason they clicked, the thing they think they need. It earns attention. |
| **Internal problem** | **Subheadline / Value propositions / Email body copy** | The internal problem is the SALE — it's why they buy, not just why they look. It creates emotional connection. Place it right after the headline to deepen engagement. |
| **Philosophical problem** | **Brand story / About page / Mission / Long-form content** | The philosophical problem is the LOYALTY — it turns customers into advocates. It's the "why we exist" and goes in deeper brand content. |
| **Villain** | **Opening hook of any content / Lead-in to the problem** | The villain creates instant recognition: "YES, that's the thing I'm fighting." Use it to open emails, ads, and sales pages before naming the three problem levels. |

## Common BrandScript Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Multiple Desires (Split Story)

**The error**: Listing 3-5 things the customer wants. "They want to save time, reduce costs, improve quality, and increase team morale."

**Why it fails**: Multiple desires create multiple story questions. The brain can't track them all, so it tracks none. Story tension requires a SINGLE question: "Will the hero get [this one thing]?"

**The fix**: Choose the ONE desire that, if fulfilled, would implicitly address the others. "Save time" → if they save time, the rest follows. Test: can you build a single story question around it? "Will this busy manager finally get control of their time?" If yes, you have your desire.

**Before**: "Our customers want better analytics, faster reporting, easier collaboration, and fewer meetings."
**After**: "Our customers want to stop drowning in busywork so they can do the work that actually matters."

### Mistake 2: Brand as Hero

**The error**: The BrandScript's Guide section reads like a hero's journey — "We were founded in a garage in 2015 and overcame incredible odds to build..."

**Why it fails**: The customer doesn't care about your journey. They care about their own. Every word you spend on your story is a word you're not spending on theirs.

**The fix**: Rewrite every Guide statement through the customer lens. Replace "We achieved X" with "We achieved X, which means YOU get Y." Better yet, limit the origin story to one sentence that establishes empathy: "We built this because we had the same problem."

**Before**: "Founded by two MIT engineers who spent 10 years in enterprise software, our award-winning platform has been recognized by Gartner and Forrester..."
**After**: "We spent 10 years watching enterprise teams struggle with the same problem you have. So we built the solution we wish had existed."

### Mistake 3: External-Only Problems

**The error**: The Problem section only lists tangible, surface-level problems. "They need faster software." "They need more leads."

**Why it fails**: Every competitor solves the same external problem. External problems are commoditized. Internal problems are where emotional resonance and differentiation live — and internal problems are why people actually BUY.

**The fix**: For every external problem, ask "And how does that make them FEEL?" three times. The third answer is usually the real internal problem.

**Before**: "Our customers' problem is that their data is scattered across multiple tools."
**After**: External — "Data scattered across tools." Internal — "They feel out of control and dread being asked a question they can't answer in real time." Philosophical — "Leaders shouldn't need a data science degree to understand their own business."

### Mistake 4: Vague Villain

**The error**: The villain is an abstract concept. "Complexity." "Inefficiency." "The status quo."

**Why it fails**: Abstract villains don't create emotional recognition. The customer should read the villain and feel "YES — that's EXACTLY what I'm fighting." Vague concepts don't trigger that response.

**The fix**: Make the villain specific and personifiable. Name the behavior, the system, or the cultural norm that causes the problem.

**Before**: "The villain is complexity."
**After**: "The villain is the 'we've always done it this way' mentality that keeps teams duct-taping 15 different tools together instead of using one that works."

### Mistake 5: Wimpy CTAs

**The error**: The direct CTA is buried, vague, or passive. "Learn More." "Get In Touch." "Explore Our Solutions."

**Why it fails**: "Learn More" is not a call to action — it's a suggestion to think about possibly doing something at some unspecified time. The guide must CHALLENGE the hero. Clear, direct, confident.

**The fix**: Use verb-first language that implies immediate value. State what happens NEXT, not what happens EVENTUALLY.

**Before**: "Learn More About Our Platform"
**After**: "Start Your Free Trial" or "Get Your Custom Plan" or "See Your Results"

### Mistake 6: Fear-Mongering in the Failure Section

**The error**: The failure section goes too far — listing catastrophic outcomes, using aggressive scare tactics, or dwelling on negativity for multiple paragraphs.

**Why it fails**: Miller's instruction is "a little salt in the recipe." Too much failure content makes the brand feel manipulative and fear-based. It also creates a negative association with the brand itself.

**The fix**: Limit failure to 2-3 specific, relatable consequences. State them. Then IMMEDIATELY pivot to success. The ratio should be roughly 20% failure, 80% success.

**Before**: "Without our platform, your business will hemorrhage money, your competitors will crush you, your team will quit, and you'll be forced to shut down."
**After**: "Every month without a system, you're leaving decisions to guesswork — and guesswork compounds. Don't let another quarter pass without clarity."

### Mistake 7: Generic Success ("A Better Tomorrow")

**The error**: The success section uses vague, aspirational language. "Achieve your goals." "Take your business to the next level." "Experience the difference."

**Why it fails**: The brain can't visualize abstractions. "Take your business to the next level" creates zero mental imagery. Specificity creates visualization, and visualization creates desire.

**The fix**: Paint a scene. Use concrete, sensory details. Describe a specific moment in the customer's transformed life.

**Before**: "With our platform, you'll achieve better results and grow your business."
**After**: "It's Friday afternoon. Your dashboard shows this month's numbers — all green. You close your laptop at 4pm because there's nothing on fire. Your team handled it. You text your partner: 'Leaving early. Dinner out tonight?'"

## The StoryBrand Marketing Roadmap (5 Steps)

1. **Create a one-liner**: Memorize it. Put it everywhere. Train your entire team.
2. **Create a lead generator**: PDF, e-course, webinar — collect email addresses
3. **Create an automated email drip campaign**: Nurture leads with story-based emails
4. **Collect transformation stories**: Ask customers how they changed. Use before/after format.
5. **Create a referral system**: Turn customers into evangelists with structured incentives

## Applying Story to Company Culture

The SB7 Framework works internally too:
- **Team as hero**: Employees are the heroes of their own story
- **Leadership as guide**: Leadership guides employees toward their own success
- **Mission as the story**: When the customer's story, the company's story, and each team member's personal story align — you get engagement, loyalty, and performance

"Where there's no story, there's no engagement." — This applies to employees as much as customers.
