## 2. Voice vs. Tone

**Brand voice** is your consistent personality across all communication. It's WHO you are. Voice doesn't change based on context.

**Tone** is how your voice adapts to different situations. It's HOW you speak in a specific moment. Tone shifts based on audience, channel, and emotional context.

**Analogy**: A person's personality (voice) stays the same whether they're at a funeral or a party. But their manner of speaking (tone) changes dramatically.

## 3. The 12 Brand Archetypes

Based on Carl Jung's universal character patterns (operationalized by Margaret Mark and Carol Pearson), archetypes provide a shortcut to brand personality. They tap into patterns humans already recognize and respond to emotionally.

### The Archetypes Map

| Archetype | Core Desire | Brand Promise | Voice Character | Risk |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **The Innocent** | Safety, simplicity | Life can be paradise | Optimistic, pure, honest | Naivety, denial |
| **The Explorer** | Freedom, discovery | Don't fence me in | Bold, independent, pioneering | Aimless wandering |
| **The Sage** | Truth, understanding | The truth will set you free | Knowledgeable, analytical, assured | Ivory tower, pedantic |
| **The Hero** | Mastery, courage | Where there's a will, there's a way | Bold, confident, honorable | Arrogance |
| **The Outlaw** | Liberation, revolution | Rules are made to be broken | Disruptive, provocative, radical | Destructiveness |
| **The Magician** | Transformation | Make dreams come true | Visionary, charismatic, imaginative | Manipulation |
| **The Regular Guy/Gal** | Belonging, connection | Everyone's welcome here | Friendly, humble, authentic | Blandness |
| **The Lover** | Intimacy, experience | You're the only one | Passionate, sensual, warm | Superficiality |
| **The Jester** | Joy, fun | If I can't dance, I don't want to be part of your revolution | Playful, irreverent, witty | Frivolity |
| **The Caregiver** | Service, nurturing | Love your neighbor as yourself | Warm, generous, compassionate | Martyrdom |
| **The Creator** | Innovation, expression | If you can imagine it, you can create it | Inventive, artistic, perfectionist | Impracticality |
| **The Ruler** | Control, order | Power isn't everything — it's the only thing | Commanding, refined, responsible | Authoritarianism |

### How to Use Archetypes

**Primary archetype**: Every brand should have ONE dominant archetype. This is the core personality.

**Secondary archetype**: A secondary archetype adds nuance and prevents cliches. Apple is primarily Creator but with Explorer influence. Nike is primarily Hero but with Outlaw undertones.

**Archetype selection criteria**:
1. Does it align with what your best-fit customers aspire to?
2. Does it differentiate you from competitors in your category?
3. Can you authentically embody it? (Don't pick Outlaw if your culture is conservative)
4. Does it reinforce your positioning? (A Sage archetype supports a "trusted expert" position)

**Category norms**: Most categories have a dominant archetype. Banks default to Ruler. Tech companies default to Magician or Creator. Breaking category archetype norms is a powerful way to differentiate — but only if your brand can authentically support it.

### Archetype-to-Voice Translation

| Archetype | Sentence Length | Vocabulary | Punctuation Style | Example Brand |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Hero | Short, punchy | Action verbs, conquest metaphors | Periods. Commands. | Nike |
| Sage | Medium, structured | Precise, technical, evidence-based | Colons, semicolons | The Economist |
| Jester | Varied, surprising | Slang, puns, unexpected juxtaposition | Exclamation marks, ellipses | Old Spice |
| Caregiver | Warm, flowing | Inclusive pronouns, gentle language | Commas, questions | Dove |
| Outlaw | Short, aggressive | Slang, contractions, profanity-adjacent | Dashes, fragments | Harley-Davidson |
| Explorer | Medium, vivid | Sensory language, nature metaphors | Varied, dynamic | Patagonia |
| Creator | Detailed, layered | Design/craft vocabulary, specific | Precise, intentional | Apple |
| Ruler | Long, authoritative | Formal, categorical, definitive | Colons, semicolons | Rolex |

## 4. Archetype Selection Decision Matrix

Selecting an archetype is not a personality quiz. It is a strategic decision with measurable downstream consequences. Use this 4-step process.

### Step 1: Map Customer Aspirations

Your archetype must align with who your customer wants to BECOME, not who they are today. The archetype is the brand's personality, but it resonates because it mirrors the customer's aspirational identity.

**Aspiration Mapping Worksheet**:

| Question | Data Source | What It Reveals |
|---|---|---|
| What does your customer brag about to friends? | Social listening, reviews, NPS verbatims | Their identity-signaling priorities |
| What brands do they already love (outside your category)? | Customer surveys, purchase data | Their existing archetype affinity |
| What content do they share organically? | Social media analysis, email forwards | The tone and worldview they identify with |
| What would they say your product makes them feel? | Post-purchase surveys, JTBD interviews | The emotional territory to occupy |
| What is the transformation they hired you for? | Jobs-to-be-done interviews | The gap between current self and aspirational self |

**Example**: A B2B cybersecurity company discovers through customer interviews that buyers don't aspire to feel "safe" (Caregiver territory). They aspire to feel "in control" and "ahead of threats" (Ruler + Sage territory). The company's voice should make buyers feel commanding and informed — not coddled.

### Step 2: Audit Category Norms

Map every significant competitor's archetype. The goal is to identify the default and decide whether to break it.

**Category Archetype Audit Template**:

| Competitor | Primary Archetype | Secondary | Evidence (3 proof points from their copy) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Competitor A | ? | ? | [headline], [tagline], [about page tone] |
| Competitor B | ? | ? | [headline], [tagline], [about page tone] |
| Competitor C | ? | ? | [headline], [tagline], [about page tone] |

**Decision rule**: If 3+ competitors share the same primary archetype, that is the category norm. Breaking it is an opportunity. Matching it is a risk of commoditization.

**Category-Norm-Breaking Examples with Analysis**:

| Brand | Category Norm | What They Chose | Why It Worked |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Liquid Death** | Innocent/Caregiver (water = purity, health) | Outlaw/Jester | The water category was so earnest and wellness-coded that irreverence became a massive differentiator. Target audience (young males) had zero emotional connection to typical water branding. The dissonance between "water" and "death metal aesthetic" generated free media coverage worth 10x their ad spend. |
| **Mailchimp** | Sage/Ruler (enterprise email = serious, data-driven) | Jester/Creator | B2B email marketing was drowning in dashboards and deliverability jargon. Mailchimp's playful voice (Freddie the chimp, high-five after sends, quirky illustrations) made the product feel approachable for small businesses — the underserved segment that became their growth engine. |
| **Oatly** | Innocent (plant milk = wholesome, gentle) | Outlaw/Jester | Oatly's voice is confrontational, self-aware, and deliberately weird ("Wow, no cow!"). Every plant milk competitor spoke in soft greens and gentle reassurance. Oatly's aggression signaled conviction, which resonated with early adopters who wanted to feel rebellious about their milk choice. |
| **Death Wish Coffee** | Creator/Explorer (specialty coffee = craft, origin stories) | Hero/Outlaw | Specialty coffee was precious and gatekeep-y. Death Wish spoke to the customer who didn't care about tasting notes — they wanted to feel powerful. "The world's strongest coffee" is a Hero claim delivered with Outlaw energy. |

### Step 3: Align with Positioning

Your archetype must make your positioning more believable, not less. Cross-reference the positioning strategy (see `positioning-strategy.md`).

**Alignment Check**:

| Positioning Element | Archetype Must... | Red Flag If... |
|---|---|---|
| Category of one | The archetype should feel natural in the new category you defined | The archetype belongs to the old category you are trying to escape |
| Key differentiator | The archetype's voice character should amplify what makes you different | The archetype is the same one competitors use to communicate a similar differentiator |
| Target belief shift | The archetype should be credible delivering the message that shifts belief | The archetype would undermine the seriousness or playfulness the belief shift requires |
| Price position | The archetype should feel congruent with your price tier | A Jester archetype for ultra-premium, or a Ruler archetype for a budget brand |

### Step 4: Authenticity Check

**The 3-Question Kill Test**:

1. **Can your team write in this voice without cringing?** If the internal team finds the voice forced or embarrassing, it will erode over time. Voice must feel natural to the people producing content daily.
2. **Does your product experience match the voice?** If your voice is Jester but your product UX is sterile and corporate, the dissonance will register as dishonesty. Voice is a promise — the product must deliver on it.
3. **Would this voice hold up in a crisis?** Every brand eventually faces a crisis (outage, PR issue, product failure). If your voice can't credibly address a serious situation, it's a costume, not a character.

### Archetype Compatibility Matrix

Not all archetype pairings work. Some create productive tension; others create incoherence.

| Primary \ Secondary | Innocent | Explorer | Sage | Hero | Outlaw | Magician | Regular | Lover | Jester | Caregiver | Creator | Ruler |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Innocent** | — | OK | OK | Clash | Clash | OK | Strong | OK | OK | Strong | OK | Clash |
| **Explorer** | OK | — | OK | Strong | OK | OK | Clash | OK | OK | Clash | Strong | Clash |
| **Sage** | OK | OK | — | OK | Clash | Strong | Clash | Clash | Clash | OK | Strong | OK |
| **Hero** | Clash | Strong | OK | — | Strong | OK | OK | Clash | Clash | OK | OK | OK |
| **Outlaw** | Clash | OK | Clash | Strong | — | OK | OK | OK | Strong | Clash | OK | Clash |
| **Magician** | OK | OK | Strong | OK | OK | — | Clash | OK | OK | OK | Strong | OK |
| **Regular** | Strong | Clash | Clash | OK | OK | Clash | — | OK | Strong | Strong | Clash | Clash |
| **Lover** | OK | OK | Clash | Clash | OK | OK | OK | — | OK | OK | Strong | OK |

| **Caregiver** | Strong | Clash | OK | OK | Clash | OK | Strong | OK | Clash | — | OK | Clash |
| **Creator** | OK | Strong | Strong | OK | OK | Strong | Clash | OK | OK | OK | — | OK |

**Key**: **Strong** = reinforcing pairing that creates depth. **OK** = workable, no inherent conflict. **Clash** = creates incoherence; avoid unless deliberately subversive.

**Why certain pairings clash**:
- **Innocent + Outlaw**: Purity and rebellion are opposing forces. The audience won't know if you're wholesome or dangerous.
- **Sage + Jester**: Authority through knowledge undermined by irreverence. You can't be the trusted expert AND the class clown. (Exception: brands like Wired that use humor to deliver intellectual content — but this requires exceptional execution.)
- **Ruler + Regular**: Commanding authority and everyman humility are mutually exclusive signals. One will always feel fake.
- **Hero + Lover**: Conquest and intimacy pull the audience in different emotional directions. The Hero asks you to strive; the Lover asks you to surrender.

## 5. Building a Verbal Identity System

### Step 1: Define 3-5 Voice Principles

Each principle should be specific to your brand — not generic descriptors like "friendly" or "professional." Every brand claims those.

**Format**: [Principle Name]: [What it means] / [What it DOESN'T mean]

**Example (for a B2B SaaS)**:
1. **Confident, not cocky**: We state our capabilities clearly and back them with evidence. We never talk down to competitors or oversell.
2. **Human, not casual**: We talk like a smart colleague, not a corporate brochure or a meme account. We use contractions and plain language but maintain substance.
3. **Precise, not pedantic**: We value accuracy and specificity. We don't hedge with weasel words, but we don't lecture either.

### Step 2: Create the Voice/Tone Matrix

Map how voice principles shift across different contexts:

| Context | Confident | Human | Precise |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Homepage** | Bold claims, backed by proof | Conversational headline, warm sub-text | Specific outcomes, not vague promises |
| **Error message** | Reassuring ("we've got this") | Empathetic, brief | Clear next step |
| **Blog post** | Thought leadership stance | Stories, anecdotes, first person | Data, examples, specific citations |
| **Sales email** | Direct ask, strong CTA | Personal, reference their situation | Specific benefit relevant to them |
| **Outage notification** | Honest about impact | Acknowledge frustration | Timeline, updates, what's being done |
| **Social media** | Assertive takes | Casual, responsive | Topical, specific to the moment |

### Step 3: Build the Do/Don't Guide

For each voice principle, provide concrete examples:

**Principle: "Human, not casual"**

| DO | DON'T |
|---|---|
| "Here's how it works" | "Lemme break this down for ya" |
| "We screwed up. Here's what happened." | "Oopsie! Our bad" |
| "This matters because..." | "At the end of the day, it is what it is" |
| "We think this is the right call." | "Per our assessment, the optimal determination..." |

### Step 4: Create Signature Phrases and Banned Words

**Signature phrases**: Words and patterns unique to your brand that customers come to associate with you. These become verbal brand cues.

**Banned words/phrases**: Words that conflict with your voice, that are overused in your industry, or that create the wrong impression.

| Category | Banned | Instead Say |
|---|---|---|
| Corporate jargon | "Leverage," "synergy," "paradigm" | Use the specific verb or noun |
| Weasel words | "Best-in-class," "cutting-edge," "world-class" | Specific proof or omit |
| Passive avoidance | "Mistakes were made" | "We made a mistake" |
| Empty superlatives | "The ultimate solution" | What specifically it does |
| Filler | "In order to," "at this point in time" | "To," "now" |

### Step 5: Define Channel-Specific Tone Adjustments

The same voice, adapted to different contexts:

**Tone Spectrum**: For each channel, place the tone on these spectrums:

```
Formal ←——————→ Casual
Serious ←——————→ Playful
Authoritative ←——————→ Conversational
Technical ←——————→ Accessible
Reserved ←——————→ Enthusiastic
```

**Example mapping**:
- Legal/compliance content: Formal, Serious, Authoritative, Technical, Reserved
- Social media: Casual, Playful, Conversational, Accessible, Enthusiastic
- Product documentation: Mid-formal, Serious, Authoritative, Mid-technical, Reserved
- Marketing emails: Casual, Mid-playful, Conversational, Accessible, Enthusiastic
- Crisis communication: Formal, Serious, Authoritative, Accessible, Reserved

## 6. Advanced Voice Techniques

### The Voice Consistency Test
Pick any three pieces of content your brand has produced — an email, a social post, and a support ticket response. Remove the logo and brand name. Could someone identify all three as coming from the same brand? If not, the voice system isn't working.

### Voice-Level Messaging
Different stakeholders within the same customer organization need different tones:

| Stakeholder | Adaption |
|---|---|
| **C-Suite** | Lead with outcome, keep it concise, use financial language |
| **Practitioner** | Lead with how it works, include technical detail, speak peer-to-peer |
| **End User** | Lead with ease of use, minimize jargon, emphasize daily workflow |
| **Procurement** | Lead with compliance, risk reduction, total cost of ownership |

The voice stays the same. The tone and emphasis shift.

### Writing for Rhythm and Cadence
Great brand voice has rhythm:
- **Vary sentence length.** Short sentences create urgency. Longer sentences allow you to develop a thought, build anticipation, and give the reader a sense of momentum that carries them forward. Then stop short.
- **Use the power of three**: Three examples, three benefits, three steps. The brain finds triads satisfying and memorable.
- **Front-load the value**: Put the most important word or phrase at the start of the sentence. Don't bury the lead.
- **Use parallel structure**: "We build, we test, we ship." Parallel construction creates rhythm that's easy to read and hard to forget.

### The "Brand as a Person" Exercise
If your brand walked into a room:
- How old are they?
- What are they wearing?
- What's the first thing they say?
- What's their sense of humor like?
- What makes them angry?
- What's their favorite book/movie?
- How do they handle conflict?
- What do they NEVER talk about?

This exercise makes abstract voice principles concrete and shared across the team.

## 7. Brand Personality Dimensions (Aaker Model)

Jennifer Aaker's research framework provides five measurable personality dimensions:

| Dimension | Traits | Example Brands |
|---|---|---|
| **Sincerity** | Down-to-earth, honest, wholesome, cheerful | Patagonia, TOMS |
| **Excitement** | Daring, spirited, imaginative, up-to-date | Red Bull, Tesla |
| **Competence** | Reliable, intelligent, successful | Microsoft, McKinsey |
| **Sophistication** | Upper-class, charming, elegant | Chanel, Mercedes |
| **Ruggedness** | Outdoorsy, tough, strong | Jeep, Timberland |

Most brands should emphasize ONE primary dimension and ONE secondary. Three or more creates incoherence.

## 8. Voice Audit Scorecard

A voice system is only as good as its enforcement. This scorecard provides a quantitative method for measuring voice consistency across touchpoints.

### The 10 Audit Criteria

| # | Criterion | What It Measures | Scoring (1-5) | Data Collection Method |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Archetype Fidelity** | Does content reflect the chosen primary archetype? | 1 = No recognizable archetype. 3 = Archetype present but inconsistent. 5 = Unmistakably on-archetype. | Pull 10 random content pieces. Score each independently. Average. |
| 2 | **Principle Adherence** | Does content follow the stated voice principles? | 1 = Violates principles. 3 = Follows some. 5 = All principles evident in every piece. | Score each voice principle separately per piece. Average across principles and pieces. |
| 3 | **Consistency Across Channels** | Does the voice feel like the same brand everywhere? | 1 = Completely different voices per channel. 3 = Recognizable but uneven. 5 = Unmistakably one brand everywhere. | Blind test: remove branding from 5 pieces (different channels). Can 3 independent reviewers match them to the same brand? |
| 4 | **Banned Word Compliance** | Are banned words/phrases absent? | 1 = Banned words appear in >50% of content. 3 = Occasional violations (<20%). 5 = Zero violations. | Automated text search across all content produced in the audit period. |
| 5 | **Signature Phrase Usage** | Are signature phrases/patterns present and natural? | 1 = Never used. 3 = Used but forced. 5 = Used naturally and consistently. | Count occurrences. Qualitative review for naturalness. |
| 6 | **Tone Appropriateness** | Does the tone shift correctly for context? | 1 = Same tone everywhere (no adaptation). 3 = Some adaptation. 5 = Tone perfectly calibrated per channel/context. | Map 5 content pieces to the tone matrix. Score fit for each. |
| 7 | **Rhythm and Cadence** | Does the writing have varied, intentional rhythm? | 1 = Monotone sentence structure. 3 = Some variation. 5 = Deliberate rhythm that matches archetype. | Sentence length analysis (standard deviation of word count per sentence). Compare to archetype benchmarks. |
| 8 | **Stakeholder Adaptation** | Does the voice adapt for different audiences while staying consistent? | 1 = Same message for all audiences. 3 = Some adaptation. 5 = Clear audience-specific tone shifts within consistent voice. | Pull content for each stakeholder segment. Score separately. |
| 9 | **Competitive Distinctiveness** | Is this voice distinguishable from competitors? | 1 = Interchangeable with competitors. 3 = Somewhat distinct. 5 = Unmistakably unique. | Blind test: mix your content with 3 competitors' (logos removed). Can reviewers pick yours out? |
| 10 | **Internal Alignment** | Can the team produce on-voice content without heavy editing? | 1 = Everything requires major rewrites. 3 = Some team members get it. 5 = 80%+ of first drafts are on-voice. | Track editorial revision rate. Survey content creators on voice confidence. |

### Score Interpretation

| Total Score (out of 50) | Rating | Action Required |
|---|---|---|
| **40-50** | Excellent | Maintain. Refine edges. Focus on criteria scoring below 4. |
| **30-39** | Good | Targeted improvements needed. Identify the 2-3 weakest criteria and run focused workshops. |
| **20-29** | Needs Work | Systemic issues. The voice system may be unclear, unenforced, or misunderstood. Revisit voice principles and retrain the team. |
| **Below 20** | Critical | The voice system is not functioning. Rebuild from archetype selection forward. |

### Quarterly Audit Process

1. **Week 1**: Collect 20 content pieces across all channels (proportional to output volume per channel).
2. **Week 2**: 3 independent reviewers score all 10 criteria. Use the data collection method specified for each.
3. **Week 3**: Average scores. Identify the 3 lowest-scoring criteria. Root-cause analysis: is it a guidelines problem (unclear) or an execution problem (clear but not followed)?
4. **Week 4**: Action plan. For each weak criterion, assign one corrective action with a deadline and owner.

### Before/After Scoring Example

**Scenario**: DTC skincare brand. Archetype: Sage/Creator. Voice principles: "Evidence-first," "Elegant simplicity," "Respectfully direct."

**Before (Q1 audit, total score: 24/50)**:

| Criterion | Score | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| Archetype Fidelity | 2 | Homepage reads like a Caregiver. Blog reads like a Sage. Instagram reads like a Jester. No coherent archetype across touchpoints. |
| Principle Adherence | 3 | "Evidence-first" followed in blog but not in ads. Ads use unsubstantiated superlatives ("the best skin of your life"). |
| Consistency | 2 | Support emails are corporate; social is casual and emoji-heavy; website is clinical. Three different brands. |
| Banned Word Compliance | 2 | "Revolutionary," "game-changing," and "synergy" found in 38% of content. |
| Competitive Distinctiveness | 3 | Blog content is distinct. Everything else could be any skincare brand. |

**After (Q3 audit, total score: 41/50, following voice system rebuild)**:

| Criterion | Score | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| Archetype Fidelity | 4 | Sage/Creator evident across all channels. The "explain the science beautifully" approach is consistent. |
| Principle Adherence | 4 | "Evidence-first" now includes citations in ads. Superlatives replaced with specific claims ("73% saw visible improvement in 4 weeks"). |
| Consistency | 4 | Blind test: reviewers matched 4/5 pieces correctly. Support emails still slightly more formal than target. |
| Banned Word Compliance | 5 | Automated scan: zero banned words in Q3 content. |
| Competitive Distinctiveness | 4 | Reviewers correctly identified brand content from competitor mix 80% of the time. |

## 9. Complete Voice System Worked Example

**Brand**: Forge & Fellow — DTC premium coffee brand.
**Positioning**: The coffee for people who build things. Not specialty-coffee-as-personality. Coffee as fuel for meaningful work.
**Target Customer**: Makers, founders, engineers, creatives. Ages 28-45. Value craft but despise pretension. More interested in what they're building than what they're drinking.
**Price Point**: $22-$38 per bag. Premium but not luxury.
**Competitive Landscape**: Specialty coffee is dominated by Creator/Explorer archetypes (origin stories, tasting notes, pour-over rituals). Mass market is Regular Guy/Caregiver (Folgers, Maxwell House).

### Primary Archetype: Creator

**Rationale**: The customer identifies as a creator/builder. The archetype mirrors their self-image. But unlike competitor coffee brands that are Creator about *coffee* (obsessing over the craft of coffee itself), Forge & Fellow is Creator about *what you make with coffee in your system*. The coffee is the tool. The customer's work is the art.

### Secondary Archetype: Hero

**Rationale**: Adds energy and drive that pure Creator can lack. The Hero element injects a sense of purpose and grit — "we're in the trenches with you." This pairing works because both archetypes share a forward-motion orientation: the Creator makes, the Hero acts.

### 4 Voice Principles

**1. Built, Not Bought**
- **What it means**: We talk like people who make things with their hands and minds. Our language is concrete, tactile, and action-oriented. Tools, materials, process.
- **What it doesn't mean**: We don't romanticize hustle culture or glorify overwork.
- **DO**: "We dial in every roast to hit the same profile, bag after bag."
- **DON'T**: "Our artisanal micro-lot coffees are a symphony of flavor."

**2. Respect the Morning**
- **What it means**: We take the morning ritual seriously without being precious about it. Coffee is the bridge between waking up and doing your best work. That matters.
- **What it doesn't mean**: We're not writing poetry about sunrise. We're not lecturing about optimal brew ratios.
- **DO**: "Your first cup sets the tone. Make it count."
- **DON'T**: "Awaken your senses to the glorious possibilities of a new day."

**3. Sharp, Not Slick**
- **What it means**: We're precise and direct. We say what the coffee is, what it tastes like, what it costs. No weasel words. No marketing fluff.
- **What it doesn't mean**: We're not cold or curt. Precision can be warm.
- **DO**: "Dark chocolate, black cherry, a clean finish. This is our benchmark blend."
- **DON'T**: "An extraordinary journey for your palate through the lush highlands of..."

**4. We're In It With You**
- **What it means**: We're fellow makers, not a lifestyle brand looking down from a photo shoot. We use "we" as peers, not as a corporation.
- **What it doesn't mean**: We don't pretend to know the customer's specific work. We don't use forced camaraderie.
- **DO**: "We built this company in a garage with a drum roaster and too much coffee. Figured we should sell some."
- **DON'T**: "We know how hard you hustle, fam! You deserve this!"

### Voice/Tone Matrix

| Context | Built, Not Bought | Respect the Morning | Sharp, Not Slick | We're In It With You |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Homepage** | Product-focused. Materials, process, craft. | Anchor the morning ritual as sacred work prep. | Lead with the coffee, not the lifestyle. | Origin story of the founders as makers. |
| **Email (welcome)** | Brief, functional. "Here's what you need to know." | Acknowledge they've chosen to upgrade their morning. | Specific: what they bought, when it ships, how to brew it. | Introduce the team like colleagues, not a brand. |
| **Error/out-of-stock** | Honest about the problem. "This blend sold out faster than we roasted it." | Don't disrupt their routine — offer an alternative immediately. | Specific timeline: "Back in stock March 15." | "We're on it" energy. |
| **Social media** | Workshop/roastery content. Show the making. | Morning-adjacent content. The workspace at 6 AM. | Short, declarative posts. One image. One sentence. | Reply to customers like a peer, not a brand account. |
| **Packaging** | Minimal copy. Let the product speak. Roast date, origin, tasting notes. | The bag IS the morning ritual. Design reinforces the principle. | No fluff on the bag. Every word earns its place. | Small human touches: roaster's initials, batch number. |
| **Customer support** | Fix the problem. No script. No "I understand your frustration." | Urgency — don't let a coffee issue ruin their morning. | State what happened, what you're doing, when it's resolved. | First person. Name of the person helping. Direct tone. |

### Signature Phrases (5)

1. **"Dial it in."** — Used when talking about precision, customization, or getting something right. From espresso calibration terminology.
2. **"Morning fuel, not morning theater."** — The anti-pretension line. Used in marketing to contrast with specialty coffee performativeness.
3. **"Built for the bench."** — Bench as in workbench, not park bench. Used to describe products designed for real use.
4. **"Ship it."** — Borrowed from maker/dev culture. Used as a CTA and as a product ethos (perfection is the enemy of shipping).
5. **"First cup, best work."** — The brand's core promise compressed into five words.

### Banned Words (10)

| Banned Word/Phrase | Why | Instead Say |
|---|---|---|
| "Artisanal" | Overused to the point of meaninglessness in coffee. | Name the specific craft process. |
| "Curated" | We make things, we don't curate them. | "Selected," "built," "assembled." |
| "Elevate" | Empty aspirational verb. | Name the specific improvement. |
| "Journey" | Customers aren't on a journey. They're drinking coffee. | Remove or use a specific verb. |
| "Passion" / "Passionate" | Every coffee brand claims passion. It means nothing. | Show it through specificity instead of claiming it. |
| "Indulge" | Positions coffee as guilty pleasure. We position it as fuel. | "Enjoy," "make," "brew." |
| "Game-changing" | Unsubstantiated superlative. | Specific claim with evidence. |
| "Bespoke" | Pretentious for a coffee brand at this price point. | "Custom," "made-to-order." |
| "Hack" | Undermines the craft positioning. Coffee isn't a hack. | "Method," "approach," "technique." |
| "Fam" / "Squad" / "Tribe" | Forced community language. We're peers, not a tribe. | Use "you" and "we" naturally. |

## 10. Voice Evolution Framework

Voice is not static. A brand's voice must evolve as the company grows, enters new markets, and serves new audiences. But evolution without discipline is drift.

### Stage-Based Voice Characteristics

| Stage | Typical Characteristics | Voice Risks | Voice Opportunities |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Startup (0-50 employees, pre-PMF to early traction)** | Founder's voice IS the brand voice. Informal, direct, high-energy. Often raw and unpolished. Personal stories, strong opinions. | Over-reliance on founder personality. Voice breaks when other people write. Too casual for enterprise buyers. | Authenticity is automatic. Speed of iteration lets you test voice quickly. Strong emotional connection with early adopters. |
| **Scale-up (50-500 employees, growth phase)** | Multiple writers. Brand guidelines exist but may be thin. Voice starts to wobble between channels. New audiences (enterprise, international) require adaptation. | "Committee voice" — writing by consensus that sands off all edges. Loss of founder edge. Inconsistency as new hires interpret voice differently. | Formalize what works. The 70/30 rule (below). Build voice training into onboarding. Systematize without sterilizing. |
| **Enterprise (500+ employees, mature brand)** | Established voice system. Style guide, voice training, editorial review. Multiple sub-brands or product lines may need their own voice variants. | Voice becomes a compliance checklist instead of a living personality. Innovation in voice slows. Brand sounds "correct" but not compelling. | Voice can become a genuine moat — the recognizable personality that competitors cannot replicate. Invest in voice as brand equity. |

### The 70/30 Rule

When evolving voice, change no more than 30% at a time. The other 70% must remain recognizable.

**How to apply it**:
- Identify the 3-5 most distinctive elements of your current voice (the elements customers would miss if they disappeared).
- Those elements form the 70%. They do not change.
- The 30% is where you experiment: new vocabulary, adjusted tone for new channels, slight shifts in formality or humor.
- Evaluate the 30% over a full quarter before making it permanent.

**Example**: A brand known for dry humor (70% element: keep) enters B2B enterprise sales. The 30% shift: slightly longer sentences, more data in claims, reduced use of slang. But the dry humor stays in every piece of enterprise content — just deployed more selectively.

**What "evolve" does NOT mean**:
- Abandoning your archetype (that is a rebrand, not an evolution)
- Suddenly adding humor if you've never been funny
- Matching the voice of a competitor who's currently winning
- Changing voice because a new CMO has different taste

### Voice Versioning for Sub-Brands

When a company has multiple products, sub-brands, or audience segments, each may need a voice variant. The system:

**1. Define the Master Voice**: The parent brand's voice principles, archetype, and signature elements. This is the "constitutional" layer that all sub-brands inherit.

**2. Define the Variant Layer**: Each sub-brand gets a variant document that specifies ONLY what changes:

| Element | Master Voice (Parent) | Variant (Sub-Brand A) | Variant (Sub-Brand B) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Primary archetype | Creator | Creator (inherited) | Creator (inherited) |
| Secondary archetype | Hero | Sage (sub-brand serves expert audience) | Jester (sub-brand targets younger demo) |
| Vocabulary tier | Mid-formal | Technical, specialized | Casual, contemporary |
| Humor level | Dry, occasional | Minimal | Frequent |
| Signature phrases | "Dial it in" | "Dial it in" (inherited) + "Spec'd for [use case]" | "Dial it in" (inherited) + "Less talk, more [action]" |

**3. Governance Rule**: The master voice principles (the "this, not that" definitions) apply to ALL variants. Only tone, vocabulary, humor level, and secondary archetype may vary.

### Common Voice Evolution Mistakes

| Mistake | What Happens | How to Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| **Reactive evolution** | Brand changes voice in response to a viral competitor or trend. Six months later, the trend dies and the brand sounds dated. | Only evolve voice based on YOUR audience data, not competitors' tactics. |
| **Executive taste override** | New CMO/CEO rewrites voice guidelines to match personal preference. Team loses confidence in the system. | Voice decisions must be grounded in data (audit scores, customer feedback, brand tracking). Personal taste is not strategy. |
| **Evolution without announcement** | Voice shifts gradually. Team doesn't know if the change is intentional or drift. | Document every voice evolution with a version number, date, rationale, and what changed. |
| **Simultaneous multi-change** | Brand changes archetype, vocabulary, tone, and channel strategy all at once. Audience doesn't recognize the brand. | The 70/30 rule. One major shift per quarter maximum. |
| **Premature formalization** | Startup locks in a voice system before finding product-market fit. The voice was built for an audience they end up pivoting away from. | Wait until you have consistent customer feedback before formalizing voice. Until then, iterate fast and keep guidelines light. |

## 11. AI Voice Consistency

AI-generated content is now a significant portion of brand output. Without deliberate voice encoding, AI defaults to generic, pleasant, personality-free prose — the opposite of distinctive brand voice.

### Prompt Engineering for Brand Voice

The goal is to encode your voice system into AI system prompts so that every generated piece starts on-voice, not off-voice.

**System Prompt Structure for Brand Voice**:

```
You are writing as [Brand Name].

## Voice Identity
- Primary archetype: [X]. Secondary: [Y].
- Brand personality in one sentence: [the "brand as a person" summary].

## Voice Principles (follow ALL of these)
1. [Principle 1]: [What it means]. [What it doesn't mean].
   - Example DO: "[example]"
   - Example DON'T: "[example]"
2. [Principle 2]: [repeat format]
3. [Principle 3]: [repeat format]

## Tone for This Context
This content is for [channel/context]. On the tone spectrum:
- Formality: [position]
- Humor: [position]
- Technical depth: [position]

## Vocabulary Rules
- Signature phrases to use naturally (not forced): [list]
- BANNED words and phrases (never use these): [list]
- Vocabulary character: [short/long sentences, active/passive, contractions yes/no]

## Audience for This Piece
- Who they are: [segment]
- What they care about: [priorities]
- What they do NOT want: [anti-patterns]

## Quality Guardrails
- Every claim must be specific and substantiated. No empty superlatives.
- Sentences must vary in length. No monotone rhythm.
- The reader should be able to identify this as [Brand Name] with the logo removed.
```

**Why this structure works**: Most AI voice prompts fail because they provide adjectives ("write in a friendly, professional tone") instead of behavioral rules. Adjectives are ambiguous. Do/don't examples and banned word lists are unambiguous. The AI can follow concrete instructions far more reliably than abstract descriptors.

### Voice Validation Checklist for AI-Generated Content

Before publishing any AI-generated content, run it through this 8-point check:

| # | Check | Pass Criteria | Common AI Failures |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Archetype alignment** | Content feels like it was written by the brand's archetypal character. | AI defaults to Sage/Caregiver blend regardless of target archetype. |
| 2 | **Banned word scan** | Zero banned words or phrases present. | AI loves "elevate," "leverage," "journey," and "game-changing." Add these to every banned list. |
| 3 | **Signature phrase integration** | At least one signature phrase appears naturally (not shoehorned). | AI either ignores signature phrases or jams them into the first sentence. |
| 4 | **Sentence rhythm** | Standard deviation of sentence length > 4 words. No more than 3 consecutive sentences of similar length. | AI produces uniform mid-length sentences. Everything is 12-18 words. No variation. |
| 5 | **Specificity test** | Every claim includes a specific detail, number, or example. | AI defaults to "our product helps you achieve your goals" vagueness. |
| 6 | **Opening line test** | The first sentence would NOT work for a competitor. It is distinctively this brand. | AI opens with generic category statements: "In today's fast-paced world..." |
| 7 | **Human-proof read aloud** | When read aloud, the content sounds like a real person speaking. No awkward constructions. | AI produces grammatically correct but rhythmically dead prose. |
| 8 | **Emotional register** | The emotional tone matches the intended context (per the voice/tone matrix). | AI tends toward relentless positivity. Struggles with nuance: empathy without pity, confidence without arrogance. |

**Scoring**: Each check is pass/fail. Content must pass all 8 to publish without human rewriting. Failing 1-2 checks means targeted editing. Failing 3+ means regenerate with an improved prompt.

### Human-in-the-Loop Workflow

```
┌─────────────┐     ┌──────────────┐     ┌─────────────────┐     ┌──────────────┐
│ AI generates │────>│ Voice check  │────>│ Human editor    │────>│ Publish      │
│ first draft  │     │ (8-point)    │     │ (if needed)     │     │              │
└─────────────┘     └──────────────┘     └─────────────────┘     └──────────────┘
                          │                      │
                          │ Pass all 8           │ Editor logs
                          │ → skip to publish    │ changes for
                          │                      │ prompt refinement
                          │ Fail 3+              │
                          │ → regenerate         │
                          ▼                      ▼
                    ┌──────────────┐     ┌─────────────────┐
                    │ Refine       │     │ Prompt library   │
                    │ prompt       │     │ updated monthly  │
                    └──────────────┘     └─────────────────┘
```

**The feedback loop is critical**: Every human edit to AI content is data. Log what was changed and WHY. Monthly, review the edit log for patterns:
- If the same banned word keeps appearing, add it to the system prompt with stronger emphasis.
- If rhythm is consistently flat, add explicit rhythm instructions ("vary sentence length between 4 and 25 words; include at least one sentence under 6 words per paragraph").
- If the archetype keeps drifting, add more do/don't examples to the system prompt — at least 3 per principle.

**Tiered review based on content risk**:

| Content Type | AI Draft OK? | Human Review Required? | Approval Level |
|---|---|---|---|
| Social media posts | Yes | Spot-check (20% sample) | Content creator |
| Blog posts | Yes | Full review, every piece | Senior editor |
| Email campaigns | Yes | Full review, every piece | Marketing manager |
| Homepage / landing pages | Yes for first draft | Full review + brand lead sign-off | Brand lead |
| Crisis communications | NO — human-written | Full review | Executive + comms lead |
| Legal / compliance content | NO — human-written | Legal review | Legal team |
| Packaging copy | Yes for first draft | Full review + brand lead sign-off | Brand lead |

## 12. Voice System Deliverables

A complete verbal identity system should include:

1. **Voice principles** (3-5, with do/don't for each)
2. **Archetype definition** (primary + secondary)
3. **Tone spectrum** (how voice adapts by channel and context)
4. **Voice/Tone matrix** (principles x contexts with examples)
5. **Signature phrases and banned words**
6. **Stakeholder tone adjustments**
7. **"Brand as a person" profile**
8. **Sample content** in the voice (homepage, email, error message, social post, support response)
9. **Before/after rewrites** of existing content in the new voice
10. **Voice audit scorecard** (quarterly cadence, 10-criterion scoring)
11. **AI voice system prompt template** (for encoding voice into AI tools)
12. **Voice evolution log** (version-controlled document tracking all voice changes over time)
