## The Core Principle

Research is not the task — the RESEARCH DOCUMENT is the deliverable. A pile of
notes is useless. A structured synthesis that a copywriter, strategist, or
marketer can USE to make decisions and write persuasive copy — that's the product.

Georgi: "The Research phase is where 90% of the 'magic' happens. If you do the
research right, the copy practically writes itself. If you skip it, no amount of
clever writing will save you."

Albuquerque: "Before I write a single word, I find the One Belief. Everything else
flows from that."

## Part 1: The RMBC Research Phase (Georgi)

### What RMBC Is

RMBC = Research → Mechanism → Brief → Copy

The Research phase produces a comprehensive document that contains EVERYTHING
the copywriter needs to write. The Mechanism phase identifies the unique
differentiator. The Brief structures the argument. The Copy writes itself.

### The Georgi Research Process

**Step 1: Product/Service Deep Dive**
- What does it actually DO? (Not marketing claims — actual functionality)
- What are all the ingredients/components/features?
- How does it work, mechanistically?
- What makes it different from every other option?
- What's the origin story? Why was it created?
- What are the clinical studies, data, or proof points?
- What are the weaknesses or limitations? (Honesty prevents bad copy)

**Step 2: Customer Deep Dive**
- Who is the ideal customer? (Demographics, psychographics, situation)
- What is their current emotional state?
- What have they tried before? (Failed solutions)
- What do they believe? (About the problem, solutions, their ability)
- What are they afraid of? (Fears, risks, objections)
- What do they desperately want? (Dream outcome)
- What would change in their life if this worked?

**Step 3: Market Deep Dive**
- What else exists in this market? (Competitive landscape)
- What claims have already been made?
- What mechanisms have already been introduced?
- What's the market sophistication level? (Schwartz 1-5)
- What's the audience awareness level? (Schwartz 1-5)
- What's working right now in this market? (Active competitor ads/funnels)

**Step 4: Proof Deep Dive**
- What testimonials exist? (Collect all of them)
- What case studies exist? (Specific, measurable results)
- What data/research supports the claims?
- What expert endorsements or credentials exist?
- What media coverage exists?
- What demonstration or before/after evidence exists?

### The Research Document Structure (Georgi)

After completing the four deep dives, compile everything into:

```
RESEARCH DOCUMENT
=================

SECTION 1: PRODUCT OVERVIEW
- What it is
- How it works (mechanism)
- Key features and benefits (features → so what → meaning)
- Origin story
- Proof points (studies, data, credentials)
- Weaknesses and limitations (for honest copy)

SECTION 2: CUSTOMER PROFILE
- Demographics
- Psychographics
- Emotional state
- Failed solutions history
- Beliefs and worldview
- Desires and dreams
- Fears and objections

SECTION 3: MARKET CONTEXT
- Competitive landscape
- Market sophistication stage
- Audience awareness level
- Claims already made
- Mechanisms already introduced
- What's working now (proven angles)

SECTION 4: PROOF ARSENAL
- Testimonials (organized by theme/benefit)
- Case studies (with specific numbers)
- Research/data citations
- Expert endorsements
- Media coverage
- Before/after evidence

SECTION 5: THE MECHANISM
[See Part 2 below]

SECTION 6: THE ONE BELIEF
[See Part 3 below]
```

## Part 2: The Mechanism (Georgi + Schwartz)

### What Is the Mechanism?

The mechanism is the unique REASON why your product/service works. It's not
the benefit — it's the explanation of HOW the benefit is delivered.

"Lose weight" is a benefit.
"Lose weight through intermittent metabolic switching that resets your fat-burning
hormones" is a mechanism.

The mechanism is what separates your claim from everyone else's identical claim.
In Stage 3+ markets (Schwartz), the mechanism IS the copy.

### Finding the Mechanism

**Step 1: Ask "Why does this work?"**
Don't accept the surface answer. Keep asking "why?" until you reach something
specific, unique, and explainable.

Surface: "Our coaching program helps businesses grow."
Why? → "Because we implement a specific sales system."
Why does that work? → "Because it uses behavioral psychology triggers in a 5-step
sequence that increases close rates."
Why does THAT work? → "Because each step addresses a specific psychological
resistance pattern identified in a 3-year study of 10,000 sales calls."

THAT last answer is the mechanism.

**Step 2: Name the Mechanism**
Give it a proprietary name. This creates ownership and differentiation:
- "The 5-Step Neural Close System"
- "Metabolic Reset Protocol"
- "The Compound Content Method"

The name should be: specific, proprietary-sounding, and memorable.

**Step 3: Validate Against Schwartz's Stages**

| Stage | Mechanism Need | Example |
|-------|---------------|---------|
| Stage 1 | No mechanism needed — claim is enough | "Lose 20 lbs in 30 days" |
| Stage 2 | No mechanism needed — enlarge the claim | "Lose 30 lbs in 14 days — guaranteed" |
| Stage 3 | INTRODUCE the mechanism | "Lose weight with the Metabolic Reset Protocol" |
| Stage 4 | ENLARGE the mechanism | "The Harvard-Validated 3-Phase Metabolic Reset Protocol" |
| Stage 5 | Mechanism is secondary — lead with identity | "For women over 40 who've tried everything..." |

## Part 3: The One Belief (Albuquerque)

### The 16-Word Sales Letter Framework

Evaldo Albuquerque's core insight: Every purchase requires the prospect to hold
ONE SPECIFIC BELIEF. If they hold that belief, they buy. If they don't, they won't.

The 16-Word Sales Letter:
"What is the ONE BELIEF I need my prospect to have in order to buy?"

The entire piece of copy exists to install that one belief. Everything that doesn't
serve that purpose is removed.

### Finding the One Belief

**Step 1: Ask "What must they believe to buy?"**
List every belief required for a purchase:
- They must believe the problem is real and urgent
- They must believe a solution exists
- They must believe THIS solution works
- They must believe it will work for THEM
- They must believe it's worth the price
- They must believe NOW is the time to act
- They must believe they can trust the seller

**Step 2: Identify the ONE that matters most**
Which of these beliefs is the HARDEST to install? Which one, if present, makes
all the others fall into place?

Usually it's one of these:
- "This mechanism actually works" (Stage 3 market)
- "This will work for ME specifically" (Stage 4-5 market)
- "I can actually do this" (high effort/sacrifice offers)
- "This is different from everything I've tried" (high failed-solution history)
- "I can trust this person/company" (low-trust markets)

**Step 3: Build the belief-installation sequence**
Albuquerque's 10 Sequential Questions method: Structure the copy as a sequence
of 10 questions where each answer naturally leads to the next, and the final
answer IS the One Belief.

```
Question 1: [Opens a curiosity loop related to the prospect's world]
Answer 1: [Provides insight that leads to...]
Question 2: [Deepens the understanding]
Answer 2: [Provides evidence that leads to...]
...
Question 10: [The logical conclusion IS the One Belief]
Answer 10: [The prospect now believes what they need to believe to buy]
```

The prospect doesn't feel "sold to" — they feel like they discovered the truth
through their own reasoning. The copy guided them there.

### One Belief Examples

**Weight loss product in Stage 3 market:**
One Belief: "My metabolism has been 'locked' by a specific hormonal pattern,
and THIS protocol is the only thing designed to unlock it."

**B2B SaaS in Solution-Aware market:**
One Belief: "The reason nothing has worked is that all other tools address
symptoms — this is the first tool that addresses the ROOT CAUSE."

**Online course in Stage 5 market:**
One Belief: "This person actually did what they're teaching, has results
unlike any guru, and their method is specifically designed for people like me."

## Part 4: The Research Brief (Pre-Copy Document)

### The Bridge Between Research and Copy

The Research Brief takes ALL findings from the research phase and translates
them into COPY DECISIONS. This is the final document before writing begins.

### The Research Brief Template

```
RESEARCH BRIEF
==============

1. THE PRODUCT
   What it is: [One sentence]
   How it works: [The mechanism — 2-3 sentences]
   Mechanism name: [Proprietary name]
   Why it's different: [One sentence — the core differentiator]
   Origin story: [Brief narrative — why this was created]

2. THE PROSPECT
   Avatar: [Name, situation, key details]
   Awareness level: [Schwartz 1-5]
   Sophistication stage: [Schwartz Stage 1-5]
   Core desire: [What they REALLY want]
   Core emotion: [Dominant feeling right now]
   Core belief to install: [THE ONE BELIEF]
   Primary objection: [The #1 thing that stops them from buying]
   Failed solutions: [What they've tried and why it didn't work]

3. THE MARKET
   Competitive alternatives: [Top 3-5]
   What competitors say: [Dominant claims/angles]
   What competitors DON'T say: [Gaps and opportunities]
   Market sophistication: [Stage 1-5 with evidence]
   Positioning angle: [How we position against alternatives]

4. THE OFFER
   Core offer: [What they get]
   Price: [What they pay]
   Value stack: [All components with individual values]
   Guarantee: [Risk reversal]
   Scarcity/urgency: [Real constraints]
   Bonuses: [Additional incentives]

5. THE PROOF
   Best testimonials: [Top 3-5 with specific results]
   Key data points: [Statistics, studies, numbers]
   Credentials: [Expert endorsements, media, authority markers]
   Before/after: [Transformation evidence]
   Social proof: [Customer count, usage stats, logos]

6. THE COPY DECISIONS
   Lead type: [Direct / Indirect / Story / Curiosity / Problem-Agitation / Secret]
   (Based on awareness level — see copywriting skill)

   Big Promise: [The headline-level promise]
   (Based on core desire + mechanism)

   Unique Mechanism: [How the promise is delivered]
   (Based on mechanism research)

   Emotional Driver: [Primary emotion to amplify]
   (Based on core emotional complex)

   Proof Strategy: [Which proof elements to lead with]
   (Based on sophistication stage and primary objection)

   Offer Presentation: [How to present the offer]
   (Based on price sensitivity and value equation)

   CTA Strategy: [What action to request and how]
   (Based on awareness level and traffic temperature)

7. THE ONE BELIEF
   Statement: [The single belief that makes the sale]
   Current state: [What they currently believe instead]
   Belief gap: [The distance between current belief and target belief]
   Installation sequence: [The logical steps to build this belief]
   Key evidence: [The proof that makes the belief credible]
```

## Part 5: The Halbert/Kennedy Research Checklist

### Halbert's Research Questions

Gary Halbert's approach was brutally simple: before writing, answer these questions:

1. What is the product? (Describe it to a 10-year-old)
2. Who are you selling to? (One specific person)
3. What keeps them up at 3am? (Their deepest fear/frustration)
4. What do they desire more than anything? (Their dream outcome)
5. What is the #1 objection? (Why would they say no?)
6. What proof do you have? (That it actually works)
7. What's the offer? (Exactly what they get and what they pay)
8. Why should they act NOW? (Urgency — real, not manufactured)
9. What's the risk? (And how do you reverse it?)
10. What's the one thing that would make this a no-brainer?

### Kennedy's "Ultimate Sales Letter" Research Process

Dan Kennedy's pre-writing research:

**Step 1: Become the customer.**
- Visit the store/website as a customer
- Call the sales line as a prospect
- Use the product yourself
- Experience the buying process firsthand

**Step 2: Study the "what keeps them up at night" list.**
Build a comprehensive list of:
- Fears
- Frustrations
- Desires
- Daily annoyances related to the problem

**Step 3: Develop the "reason why" story.**
Every claim needs a "reason why" — a logical explanation for why the offer is
so good / the price is so low / the guarantee is so strong. People are
suspicious of anything that seems too good to be true unless there's a
plausible explanation.

## Part 6: Research Quality Checklist

### Before Declaring Research "Done"

Run this checklist. If any item is missing, the research is incomplete:

**Product Knowledge:**
- [ ] Can you explain what the product does to a complete novice in 30 seconds?
- [ ] Can you explain the mechanism (HOW it works) in a way that's unique and specific?
- [ ] Do you know the origin story (WHY it was created)?
- [ ] Do you know the limitations and weaknesses?

**Customer Knowledge:**
- [ ] Can you write the avatar narrative from memory?
- [ ] Do you know the Core Emotional Complex (desire, emotion, belief, identity)?
- [ ] Do you know the One Belief that makes the sale?
- [ ] Do you know 5+ failed solutions they've tried?
- [ ] Do you have 20+ verbatim VOC quotes in their exact language?

**Market Knowledge:**
- [ ] Can you name the top 3-5 competitive alternatives?
- [ ] Do you know the market sophistication stage with evidence?
- [ ] Do you know the dominant awareness level?
- [ ] Have you seen the active competitor ads and landing pages?
- [ ] Do you know what claims have already been made?

**Proof Knowledge:**
- [ ] Do you have 3+ testimonials with specific, measurable results?
- [ ] Do you have at least one credentialed proof point (study, expert, media)?
- [ ] Do you have before/after or transformation evidence?

**Strategic Knowledge:**
- [ ] Can you write the positioning statement (Dunford/Moore format)?
- [ ] Can you articulate why THIS product for THIS customer right NOW?
- [ ] Can you identify the primary objection and how to overcome it?
- [ ] Do you know the offer structure (value stack, price, guarantee)?

If all boxes are checked, the research is done. If not, go back and fill the gaps.

## Part 7: Worked Research Document — Email Marketing Course for Ecommerce

### Context

This is a complete, filled-out Research Document for a real-world scenario:
an online course teaching email marketing specifically for ecommerce brands
doing $1M-$10M/year in revenue. The course is called "Revenue Engine" and is
sold by a founder who scaled her own DTC brand from $800K to $4.2M using email
as the primary growth lever.

This example demonstrates how a copywriter would USE the Research Document to
make every copy decision. Each section includes annotations showing the
copywriting implications.

---

### SECTION 1: PRODUCT OVERVIEW

**What it is:**
Revenue Engine is a 6-week cohort-based course that teaches ecommerce brand
owners (and their marketing leads) how to build a complete email marketing
system — flows, campaigns, segmentation, and deliverability — using Klaviyo.

**How it works (mechanism):**
The course is structured around the "Revenue Layer Stacking" method: instead
of treating email as a channel you "do campaigns on," students build 7
automated revenue layers that compound. Each layer is a self-contained system
(welcome, browse abandon, cart abandon, post-purchase, winback, VIP, and
sunset) that gets installed, tested, and optimized sequentially. The stacking
effect means each new layer amplifies the ones before it because of
cross-layer segmentation logic — e.g., the VIP layer changes the behavior of
the winback layer because VIP-tagged customers get different winback timing
and offers.

**Key features and benefits:**
| Feature | So What | Meaning |
|---------|---------|---------|
| 7 pre-built Klaviyo flow templates | Don't start from scratch | Save 40+ hours of setup |
| Weekly live implementation calls | Get stuck = get unstuck in < 7 days | No stalling or guessing |
| Revenue attribution dashboard template | See exactly which emails make money | Kill the ones that don't, double down on the ones that do |
| Deliverability audit checklist | Stop landing in spam/promotions | Every email actually reaches the inbox |
| Segmentation logic library (47 segments) | Stop blasting your whole list | Send fewer emails, make more money |
| Private Slack community | Peer accountability + shared learnings | Reduce isolation, steal ideas |
| 12-month campaign calendar with subject lines | Never wonder "what should I send?" | Consistency without creative burnout |

**Origin story:**
Founder Sarah Chen ran a DTC skincare brand. In 2021, post-iOS 14.5, her
Facebook CAC doubled from $28 to $61. She couldn't profitably acquire
customers anymore. Out of desperation, she went deep on email — reading
everything, hiring consultants, testing relentlessly. In 14 months, email went
from 12% to 41% of total revenue. Her brand went from $800K to $4.2M.
Other DTC founders in her mastermind started asking how. She taught 6 founders
informally. All 6 saw email revenue increase by 80%+ within 90 days. She
realized the method was replicable and built the course.

**Proof points:**
- Sarah's own brand: $800K → $4.2M, email = 41% of revenue
- 6 beta cohort students: average 84% increase in email-attributed revenue within 90 days
- 3 cohorts completed (187 students total), NPS = 72
- 23 detailed case studies with before/after revenue screenshots
- Featured in Shopify Plus blog, 2x Bites podcast, Klaviyo Partner Summit speaker
- Average student results: +$38K/month in email-attributed revenue within 90 days
  (based on Cohort 1-3 self-reported data, n=141 respondents out of 187)

**Weaknesses and limitations:**
- Klaviyo-only. If you use Mailchimp, Drip, or Omnisend, you'll need to translate.
- Requires minimum 5,000 email subscribers to see meaningful results from segmentation.
- Cohort-based = starts on fixed dates. Can't start immediately.
- $2,497 price point. Not for bootstrapped pre-revenue brands.
- Sarah's experience is DTC consumer products. B2B ecommerce or marketplace sellers may find 20% of the content less applicable.

> **COPYWRITING IMPLICATION:** Weaknesses are usable. "Requires 5,000+ subscribers"
> becomes a qualification filter that INCREASES perceived value. "Klaviyo-only"
> becomes specificity proof — "this isn't generic email advice, it's built for
> one platform so every template works out of the box." The $2,497 price point
> and minimum subscriber count together signal "this is for serious operators."

### SECTION 2: CUSTOMER PROFILE

**Demographics:**
- Ecommerce brand owner or Head of Marketing / Email Marketing Manager
- Brand doing $1M-$10M/year in revenue
- 5,000 - 200,000 email subscribers
- Age 28-45, gender split ~60/40 female/male (DTC skew)
- US/Canada/UK/Australia (English-speaking Shopify markets)
- Typically running on Shopify or Shopify Plus
- 1-15 employees

**Psychographics:**
- Operator identity. They see themselves as builders, not marketers.
- Time-starved. Wearing 5 hats. Email is "that thing I know I should do better."
- Data-aware but not data-sophisticated. They look at Klaviyo dashboards but don't
  know what actions to take from the data.
- Community-oriented. Already in 1-2 paid masterminds or Slack groups.
- Paid-ads-traumatized. iOS 14.5 / rising Meta CPMs burned them. Owned channels = safety.
- Growth-pressure. Investors, partners, or personal financial goals demand scale.

**Emotional state:**
Primary: Frustrated overwhelm. They KNOW email should be doing more but don't
have time to figure it out, and every "quick tip" they try doesn't move the
needle. They feel like they're leaving money on the table every single day.

Secondary: Fear of dependency. Dependent on Meta/Google for traffic. One algorithm
change could tank their business. Email = the escape hatch they can't quite build.

Tertiary: Impostor syndrome on email specifically. They're good at product, good at
brand, maybe good at ads — but email feels like a foreign language. Segments?
Flows? Deliverability? SPF records? It feels technical in a way that makes them
feel stupid.

**Failed solutions history:**
1. Hired a "Klaviyo expert" freelancer ($2K-$5K/month). Got basic flows set up but
   no strategic thinking. Flows were generic templates. Revenue barely moved.
2. Took a generic email marketing course (not ecommerce-specific). Learned theory
   but couldn't apply it to Shopify/Klaviyo. Felt like a waste of time.
3. Tried to DIY from YouTube and blog posts. Information overload. Conflicting advice.
   Implemented random tactics. Inconsistent results.
4. Hired an email marketing agency ($3K-$8K/month). Got locked into a contract. Agency
   sent campaigns on a calendar but didn't build systems. When they left, revenue
   from email dropped back to baseline within 60 days.
5. Bought Klaviyo's own templates/resources. Too basic. Designed for beginners with
   100 subscribers, not operators with 30K+ subscribers and complex product catalogs.

> **COPYWRITING IMPLICATION:** Five failed solutions = Stage 4-5 market behavior.
> The prospect doesn't need to be convinced email marketing works. They need to be
> convinced THIS approach is fundamentally different from what they've tried. Every
> failed solution becomes a "repositioning foil" in the copy — "Unlike a freelancer
> who sets up cookie-cutter flows..." "Unlike an agency that disappears and takes
> your email revenue with them..."

**Beliefs and worldview:**
- "Email works for other brands. I just haven't cracked the code yet."
- "I need a system, not more random tips."
- "If I could just find someone who's actually DONE this at my scale, I could follow their playbook."
- "Agencies are expensive and you can't control quality."
- "I don't have time to become an email marketing expert. I just need it to WORK."

**Desires and dreams:**
- Email generating 30-40% of total revenue on autopilot (not manual campaign blasts)
- Reducing dependence on paid ads to under 40% of revenue mix
- Having a system that works even when they're not touching it
- Feeling confident that their email program is "best in class"
- Being able to hire and manage an email person effectively (knowing what good looks like)

**Fears and objections:**
- "I've wasted money on courses before. How is this different?"
- "$2,497 is a lot for a course. Can I just hire someone for that?"
- "I don't have time for a 6-week program. I'm running a business."
- "My brand is different / my products are different. Will this work for me?"
- "What if I fall behind the cohort?"

**Core Emotional Complex (avatar-profiling.md format):**
- Desire: Predictable, scalable email revenue that I own and control
- Emotion: Frustrated overwhelm + fear of continued ad dependency
- Belief: "There's a system out there that works — I just haven't found it yet"
- Identity: "I'm a smart operator who just needs the right blueprint"

**20 Verbatim VOC Quotes (from Reddit, Slack groups, customer interviews):**

6. "iOS 14 broke our acquisition model. We HAVE to figure out retention or we're done."
7. "I literally have anxiety every time I open our Klaviyo dashboard because I know it should be doing more."
8. "My flows are 'fine' but I know fine isn't good enough. I just don't know what great looks like."
9. "The freelancer we hired just copy-pasted templates. Nothing was customized for our brand."
10. "I'd rather learn to fish than keep paying agencies that fish for me badly."
11. "My abandoned cart flow has a 2% conversion rate. Is that good? Bad? I genuinely don't know."
12. "We blast our whole list twice a week and our unsubscribe rate is climbing. Something's wrong."
13. "I spend 2 hours a week on email and I know that's not enough but I literally don't have more time."

### SECTION 3: MARKET CONTEXT

**Competitive landscape:**

| Competitor | Price | Format | Positioning | Weakness We Exploit |
|-----------|-------|--------|-------------|-------------------|
| Klaviyo Academy | Free | Self-paced videos | "Learn Klaviyo" (tool training) | Platform-neutral, no strategy, no ecommerce-specific revenue tactics |
| Chase Dimond's course | $497 | Self-paced | "Email marketing for ecommerce" | Broad, no cohort/accountability, copy-focused not systems-focused |
| Retention.com + various agencies | $3K-$8K/mo | Done-for-you | "We'll do it for you" | Expensive, no knowledge transfer, revenue drops when you leave |
| Ezra Firestone's Smart Marketer | $997 | Self-paced | "Ecommerce marketing" (email is one module) | Not email-specific. Jack of all trades. |
| Random Udemy/Skillshare courses | $15-$50 | Self-paced | "Learn email marketing" | Not ecommerce-specific, beginner-level, no implementation support |

**Market sophistication stage: STAGE 4**
Evidence: Multiple courses exist. "Email marketing for ecommerce" is a known
category. Claims like "increase email revenue" and "build automated flows" have
been made hundreds of times. Mechanisms like "segmentation" and "flows" are
already familiar. We need an ENLARGED mechanism — not just "build flows" but a
specific, named, proprietary system that sounds different from everything else.

## Part 8: Mechanism Naming Framework

### The Naming Formula

A strong mechanism name follows this structure:

```
[Specificity Marker] + [Action/Process Word] + [Domain Word]

Where:
- Specificity Marker = number, scientific term, origin reference, or qualifier
- Action/Process Word = what it DOES (stacking, switching, cycling, compounding, etc.)
- Domain Word = anchors it in the relevant field (protocol, method, system, framework, engine)
```

### The Mechanism Name Quality Test

Score each name 1-5 on these criteria. Minimum score of 15/25 to use:

| Criteria | 1 (Weak) | 5 (Strong) |
|----------|----------|------------|
| **Specificity** | Vague, could apply to anything | Clearly about ONE thing |
| **Proprietary feel** | Sounds generic | Sounds like you invented/own it |
| **Memorability** | Forgettable, too many syllables | Sticks in the mind after one read |
| **Curiosity** | Self-explanatory, no reason to learn more | Creates a "what is that?" response |
| **Credibility** | Sounds made up / hype-y | Sounds grounded, real, and serious |

### Mechanism Credibility Hierarchy

Not all mechanisms carry equal weight. Credibility depends on the evidence
supporting the mechanism, not just the name. Here's the hierarchy from
strongest to weakest:

| Level | Credibility Source | Example | Persuasion Power |
|-------|-------------------|---------|-----------------|
| 1 (Strongest) | Peer-reviewed science naming the exact pathway | "AMPK enzyme activation — published in Nature Metabolism" | Near-irrefutable. The prospect can verify it. |
| 2 | Proprietary data from the creator's own operation | "Tested across 187 ecommerce brands with documented results" | Very strong. Real data from real usage. |
| 3 | Named, specific, verifiable process | "7 layers installed in a specific order with cross-layer logic" | Strong. Specificity implies rigor. |
| 4 | Logical framework with a plausible chain of reasoning | "If A, then B, therefore C — which is why the method works" | Moderate. Persuasive but not provable. |
| 5 | Analogy or metaphor dressed up as a mechanism | "It's like compound interest for your email list" | Weak as a standalone mechanism. Fine as an explanation layer on top of a real one. |
| 6 (Weakest) | Arbitrary name with no underlying substance | "The Power Launch System" — sounds proprietary but means nothing specific | Empty. Sophisticated buyers see through this immediately. |

**Rule:** Your mechanism should operate at Level 3 or above. If you can only get
to Level 4-5, you need more research — go back to the product deep dive.

### When NOT to Use a Mechanism

A named mechanism hurts more than it helps in these situations:

**Stage 1 markets.** If nobody else is making the claim, just make the claim.
"Lose 20 lbs in 30 days" is more powerful than "Lose 20 lbs in 30 days with the
Metabolic Reset Protocol" when there are no competing claims. The mechanism
adds complexity where none is needed. Save it for when the market matures.

**When the mechanism is weaker than the result.** If you have a jaw-dropping
testimonial ("I made $1.2M in my first year"), leading with the mechanism
buries the lead. Let the result do the work. The mechanism becomes supporting
evidence, not the headline.

**When the audience is Unaware or Problem-Aware.** If they don't know the
problem exists or don't know solutions exist, a mechanism is premature. You
must first establish the problem's reality and urgency before explaining HOW
something solves it.

**When the mechanism name sounds like every other mechanism name.** If your
best name is "The 5-Step Success System" or "The Growth Blueprint," you
haven't actually found a mechanism — you've slapped a name on a generic
promise. The naming test above (minimum 15/25) prevents this, but the
deeper issue is that the research hasn't gone deep enough.

**When simplicity IS the differentiator.** Some products win because they're
dead simple. Adding a mechanism name can make simple things sound complicated.
A product whose value prop is "one click and it's done" gets worse when you add
"powered by the Automated Simplicity Engine."

```
"I don't have time" → resolves only itself

"My brand is unique" → resolves only itself

"I should hire someone" → resolves only itself

"Is it worth $2,497?" → resolves only itself
```

The objection with the most downstream effects is the belief you need to install.
"I've tried courses before" collapses 3 other objections. That's your One Belief
territory.

### Belief Installation Techniques by Media Type

The One Belief is the same regardless of medium. The INSTALLATION METHOD changes
based on the format:

**Long-form sales page (2,000-8,000 words):**
- Use the full 10-question sequence as the body copy structure
- Each Q→A pair becomes a section with a subheadline (the question) and 1-3 paragraphs (the answer)
- Embed proof at the moments of highest skepticism (typically after Q4-Q5 and Q8-Q9)
- Total installation time: 12-25 minutes of reading

**Video sales letter (15-45 minutes):**
- Use the 10-question sequence as the script spine
- Questions become "hooks" that maintain attention ("But here's what surprised me...")
- Answers become storytelling + data moments
- Visual aids (charts, screenshots, before/after) at proof moments
- Total installation time: 20-40 minutes

**Email sequence (5-12 emails over 7-21 days):**
- Each email installs ONE Q→A pair
- Subject lines are drawn from the questions
- Each email opens a loop that the next email closes
- The final email presents the full One Belief and the offer
- Installation happens across multiple sessions — requires stronger hooks per email

**Webinar (45-90 minutes):**
- First 10 minutes: Q1-Q3 (establish the gap, name the problem differently)
- Middle 20 minutes: Q4-Q7 (build the case, introduce the mechanism)
- Final 10 minutes: Q8-Q10 (prove it works, land the One Belief, present the offer)
- The "teaching" portion IS the belief installation — not separate from it

**Short-form ad (30s-3min video or < 300 words text):**
- Cannot do full 10-question sequence
- Pick the ONE Q→A pair that creates the biggest shift (usually Q5-Q6, the "aha moment")
- Use that as the entire ad, with a CTA to a page/video that does the full installation
- The ad's job: create enough belief shift to earn the click

**Podcast/interview (30-60 minutes):**
- Tell the origin story as the installation vehicle
- Structure: "I used to believe X. Then I discovered Y. Here's what happened."
- The interviewer's questions naturally create the Q→A structure
- Proof = the story itself (results, before/after, specifics)

### Common One Beliefs by Market Type

These are the MOST FREQUENT One Beliefs in each market category, based on
recurring patterns across thousands of sales letters, VSLs, and funnels.
Use as a starting hypothesis — then validate with actual research.

**1. Health/Fitness/Weight Loss**
"There's a specific [physiological mechanism] that's been blocking my results,
and addressing THAT (not diet/exercise willpower) is the real solution."
Pattern: Externalize blame from the person to a mechanism. The prospect isn't
lazy — their [hormones / gut bacteria / inflammation / enzyme] is the problem.

**2. Make Money Online / Biz Opp**
"There's a specific method that works right now, and the reason I haven't
succeeded is that I was using the WRONG method — not that I'm incapable."
Pattern: Past failure = wrong vehicle, not wrong driver. This belief simultaneously
validates the prospect and invalidates competitors.

**3. B2B SaaS**
"The reason my current tools/processes don't work is that they address symptoms,
not the root cause — and this product addresses the root cause."
Pattern: Reframe from tool problem to architecture problem. Same structure as
the Revenue Engine example.

**4. Online Courses (Skill-Based)**
"This instructor has a specific SYSTEM that makes this skill learnable and
implementable, unlike the theory-heavy content I've consumed before."
Pattern: System > information. The prospect has consumed plenty of information.
They need to believe this is structured implementation, not more content.

**5. Financial Services / Investing**
"There's a specific market condition / strategy that the mainstream doesn't
know about (or deliberately hides), and accessing it is simpler than I thought."
Pattern: Insider knowledge + simplicity. The belief combines "hidden opportunity"
with "I can actually do this."

**6. Relationships / Dating**
"The reason my relationships fail isn't bad luck or personal deficiency — it's
a specific communication/attachment pattern that I can learn to change."
Pattern: Externalize to a changeable pattern. Not "you're broken" — "you have
a pattern, and patterns are fixable."

**7. Parenting**
"My child's behavior is a response to a specific pattern in our dynamic, and
changing MY behavior (not theirs) is the fastest path to change."
Pattern: Locus of control shift. The parent (the buyer) must believe THEY
can create the change, which empowers them and makes the product actionable.

**8. Productivity / Time Management**
"I don't need more discipline — I need a better SYSTEM, because willpower is
finite and systems are infinite."
Pattern: System > willpower. Removes shame ("you're not lazy") and introduces
hope ("the right system fixes this").

**9. Creative Skills (Writing, Design, Music)**
"Talent is learnable. What looks like natural ability is actually a set of
specific, practicable techniques — and this method teaches the techniques
the 'naturals' use unconsciously."
Pattern: Demystify talent. The One Belief must overcome "I'm not naturally
gifted enough" with "gifted people just learned this earlier."

**10. Agency / Consulting / Freelancing**
"The reason I'm stuck at [revenue level] isn't that I need more clients — it's
that my business MODEL is capped, and restructuring the model unlocks the
next level without working more hours."
Pattern: Model > effort. The belief shift is from "work harder / get more
clients" to "restructure how you deliver and price."

**11. Software / Tech Tools (Consumer)**
"The tools I've been using are designed for a different use case, and this tool
is designed specifically for MY use case — which is why it works and they don't."
Pattern: Specificity of fit. "Designed for you" is more powerful than "better
than alternatives" because it implies the alternatives were never meant for
them in the first place.

**12. Personal Development / Self-Help**
"I'm not broken and I don't need fixing — I need to understand the ONE pattern
that's driving all my unwanted behaviors, and once I see it, I can change it."
Pattern: ONE root cause. Personal development markets are overwhelmed with
"10 things to fix." The One Belief is that there's actually only ONE thing,
and it unlocks everything else.

## Part 10: Research Quality Audit — 15-Item Pass/Fail Checklist

### Purpose

Part 6 is a "completeness" checklist — do you HAVE the information. This section
is a QUALITY checklist — is the information GOOD ENOUGH to write effective copy.
Every item has a minimum evidence standard. Anything below the standard is
"research debt" — it will show up as weak copy, vague claims, or poor conversion.

### The Concept of Research Debt

Research debt is the gap between what you KNOW and what you NEED to know to
write copy that converts. Like technical debt in software, research debt
compounds: a weak mechanism leads to a weak headline, which leads to low
click-through, which leads to low conversion, which leads to "the copy didn't
work" — when the real problem was never the copy.

**Research debt manifests as:**
- Headlines that are generic ("Discover the secret to...")
- Body copy that stays abstract instead of getting specific
- Proof sections that rely on vague testimonials ("Great product!")
- Mechanisms that are just renamed benefits ("The Weight Loss Accelerator")
- CTAs that resort to false urgency because the argument wasn't persuasive enough

### The 15-Item Quality Audit

Score each item PASS or FAIL. No partial credit. Minimum passing score: 12/15.
Below 12 = go back and fill the gaps before writing.

**PRODUCT EVIDENCE**

| # | Item | Pass Standard | Fail Examples |
|---|------|---------------|---------------|
| 1 | **Mechanism specificity** | You can explain the mechanism in 3 sentences with no jargon, and a skeptic would say "that makes sense." The explanation includes a SPECIFIC causal chain, not just a name. | "It uses a proprietary method" (no causal chain). "It works because of science" (no specificity). The name exists but you can't explain what it actually means. |
| 2 | **Origin story with conflict** | The origin story has a PROBLEM that drove the creation. It's not "I had an idea" — it's "I had THIS problem, tried X/Y/Z, they failed, so I built this." The story must be true and verifiable. | "We started because we saw an opportunity in the market" (no conflict). Story is generic enough to apply to any product. No specific details (dates, numbers, names). |
| 3 | **Honest limitation documentation** | You can name 3+ real limitations/weaknesses AND you have a positioning strategy for each (either disqualify, reframe, or acknowledge-and-bridge). | You can't name any weaknesses, or you have weaknesses but no strategy for addressing them in copy. This means you'll either lie or get blindsided by objections. |

**CUSTOMER EVIDENCE**

| # | Item | Pass Standard | Fail Examples |
|---|------|---------------|---------------|
| 4 | **Verbatim VOC (minimum 20 quotes)** | 20+ quotes from real prospects/customers in their EXACT language, sourced from reviews, forums, calls, or surveys. Each quote is attributed to a source type. | Paraphrased quotes ("customers often say..."). Fewer than 20. All from the same source. Quotes that sound like marketing copy, not real human speech. |
| 5 | **Failed solutions inventory (minimum 5)** | 5+ specific things the prospect has tried before, with WHY each failed from the prospect's perspective. Not "they tried other products" but "they tried [specific product/approach] and it failed because [specific reason]." | Generic list ("they tried diets, exercise, supplements"). No failure reasons. Fewer than 5. Reasons are your spin, not the prospect's actual experience. |
| 6 | **Core Emotional Complex documented** | All four elements (desire, emotion, belief, identity) are filled out with specific, non-generic language. The desire is specific enough to use as a headline element. The emotion is specific enough to evoke in copy. | Desire = "they want to succeed" (too vague). Emotion = "frustrated" (not specific enough — frustrated about WHAT, in WHAT context, triggered by WHAT?). Identity is missing. Belief is a guess, not sourced from VOC. |
| 7 | **One Belief identified with evidence** | The One Belief is stated, the current belief is stated, the gap between them is described, AND you have evidence for WHY this is the hardest belief to install (from survey, sales calls, or objection mapping — not guessing). | One Belief is asserted without evidence. Multiple "one beliefs" listed (there can only be one). The belief is actually a benefit ("they must believe they'll lose weight") instead of a mechanism belief ("they must believe their hormones are the root cause"). |

**MARKET EVIDENCE**

| # | Item | Pass Standard | Fail Examples |
|---|------|---------------|---------------|
| 8 | **Sophistication stage with EVIDENCE** | Stage is declared (1-5) AND supported by 3+ pieces of evidence: specific competitor claims, number of competitors, type of claims being made, market maturity indicators. | Stage is guessed. No evidence cited. Evidence contradicts the stage declaration (e.g., "Stage 2" but you list 8 competitors with mechanism-based claims). |
| 9 | **Competitor claims inventory** | Top 5 competitors listed with their ACTUAL claims (headlines, primary promises, mechanism names if applicable). Not paraphrased — actual copy documented. | "Competitors generally claim..." (not specific). Fewer than 3 competitors. Claims are paraphrased or assumed, not directly observed. |
| 10 | **Positioning gap identified** | One specific thing competitors DON'T say or DON'T do that represents a genuine opportunity. The gap must be validated (prospects actually care about it) not just observed (it exists but doesn't matter). | Gap is trivial ("no one uses purple in their branding"). Gap is assumed important but never validated with prospect data. Gap doesn't translate to a copy angle. |

**PROOF EVIDENCE**

| # | Item | Pass Standard | Fail Examples |
|---|------|---------------|---------------|
| 11 | **Testimonials with specifics (minimum 3)** | 3+ testimonials that include SPECIFIC, MEASURABLE results (numbers, timeframes, before/after). Each overcomes a different objection. Attributed to real, named people. | Vague testimonials ("Great program!"). No numbers. All testimonials say the same thing (no objection diversity). Anonymous or first-name-only with no identifiers. |
| 12 | **Credibility proof (minimum 1 strong)** | At least 1 credibility element from the top 3 tiers: peer-reviewed research, proprietary data from the creator's operation, or named/specific third-party validation (media, expert endorsement, institutional partnership). | Only self-reported data. "As seen on" logos without actual articles. Credentials that don't relate to the specific claim being made. |

**STRATEGIC READINESS**

| # | Item | Pass Standard | Fail Examples |
|---|------|---------------|---------------|
| 13 | **Lead type selected with rationale** | Lead type (direct, indirect, story, curiosity, problem-agitation, secret) is chosen AND the rationale ties directly to awareness level + sophistication stage. You can explain WHY this lead type and not the others. | Lead type chosen by preference ("I like story leads"). No rationale. Rationale contradicts the awareness/sophistication data (e.g., curiosity lead for a Most-Aware audience). |
| 14 | **10-Question belief installation sequence drafted** | All 10 questions written out. Each flows logically from the previous answer. The final answer IS the One Belief. A non-expert reader would follow the logic without confusion. | Fewer than 10 questions. Questions don't flow (logical jumps between Q→A pairs). The sequence requires domain knowledge the prospect doesn't have. The final answer is the product pitch, not the belief. |
| 15 | **Offer math documented** | The value equation is explicit: Dream Outcome x Perceived Likelihood / Time Delay x Effort & Sacrifice = Value. Each variable is filled in with specific numbers or qualitative ratings. Price is anchored against at least 2 alternatives. | Price stated without context. No value stack. No anchoring. "It's a good deal" without showing the math. Guarantee not documented. |

### Research Debt Decision Matrix

When you fail items, use this to decide whether to proceed or go back:

| Items Failed | Severity | Decision |
|-------------|----------|----------|
| 0-1 | Low | Proceed with writing. Note the gap and fill opportunistically. |
| 2-3 | Moderate | Proceed ONLY if the failed items are in different categories. If 2+ failures are in the same category (e.g., both Customer Evidence items), go back. |
| 4-5 | High | Do not write. The research has structural gaps that will produce weak copy. Fill the gaps first. |
| 6+ | Critical | The "research" is actually just notes. Start the structured research process from scratch using the Georgi 4-step framework. |

### Tracking Research Debt Over Time

For teams producing copy regularly, track research debt per project:

```
PROJECT RESEARCH DEBT LOG
=========================
Project: [Name]
Date: [Date]
Audit Score: [X/15]
Items Failed: [List]
Debt Impact: [Where the weak copy showed up — low CTR, weak body, vague CTA, etc.]
Resolution: [What research was done post-launch to fill the gap]
Time to Resolve: [Hours spent filling the gap after the fact]

Pattern Analysis (quarterly):
- Most commonly failed item: [#]
- Research phase causing most debt: [Product / Customer / Market / Proof / Strategy]
- Average resolution time: [Hours]
- Estimated revenue impact: [If measurable]
```

This turns research quality from a subjective judgment ("I think I did enough research")
into a measurable, improvable process.
