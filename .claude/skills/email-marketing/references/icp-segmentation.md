# ICP Definition, Micro-Segmentation & List Strategy

The single highest-leverage variable in cold email. A world-class email to the wrong person
produces nothing. A decent email to the exact right person at the exact right moment produces
a meeting. List strategy is not a pre-campaign task — it is continuous, evolving, and directly
tied to every performance metric that matters.

> "Segmented campaigns generate up to 760% more revenue than non-segmented blasts."
> The top quartile achieves 15%+ reply rates through micro-segmentation alone —
> before changing a single word of copy.

## 1. ICP Definition — The Foundation

An ICP is not a demographic profile. It is a description of the company and person who
experiences the specific pain you solve most acutely, has the authority and budget to act,
and gets the most transformational value from what you offer.

**The ICP Definition Framework**:

### Firmographic Layer (company characteristics)

| Dimension | What to Define | Why It Matters | Data Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| Industry/vertical | Specific sub-vertical (not "SaaS" — "B2B SaaS selling to enterprise, 50-200 employees") | Determines pain language, proof relevance, deal cycle | Apollo, ZoomInfo, LinkedIn |
| Company size | Revenue band AND headcount range | Budget indicator + complexity signal | Clearbit, ZoomInfo, Apollo |
| Growth stage | Pre-seed, Seed, Series A/B/C, bootstrapped, PE-backed, public | Determines urgency, budget, decision speed | Crunchbase, PitchBook |
| Geographic market | Where they sell (not just HQ location) | Affects compliance, timezone, cultural context | Company website, LinkedIn |
| Tech stack | Specific tools they use | Reveals sophistication, budget, pain points, integration needs | BuiltWith, Clearbit, job postings |
| Business model | ACV, sales motion (PLG, sales-led, hybrid), who their customer is | Determines how they buy and what they value | Company website, G2, press |

### Persona Layer (individual characteristics)

| Dimension | What to Define | Why It Matters | Impact on Response Rate |
|---|---|---|---|
| Title and function | Precise title (VP Sales vs. Head of Sales vs. Sales Director) | Different titles = different conversations, different authority | VP-level: 4-7% reply rate; Director: 3-5%; Manager: 2-4% |
| Seniority / authority | Economic buyer vs. champion vs. end user | Determines CTA type and deal velocity | Economic buyers book 2.3x more meetings from cold email |
| Reporting structure | Who they report to | Reveals their KPIs and priorities | — |
| Years in role | New (<6 months) vs. established (2+ years) | New in role = 1.8x higher response rate (motivated to prove themselves) | New: 6-9%; Established: 3-5% |
| Company tenure | New to company (<12 months) vs. tenured | New = evaluating existing processes = active purchase window | New hire: 5-8% vs. tenured: 2-4% |

### Situation Layer (the most underused dimension)

This is where most operators leave pipeline on the table. The situation layer answers:
- What specific situation makes this person feel their pain most acutely **right now**?
- What recent event has made solving this problem more urgent?
- What is their current "good enough" solution and why is it insufficient?

**Situation triggers that create purchase windows**:

| Trigger | Why It Creates Urgency | Response Rate Multiplier | Optimal Contact Window |
|---|---|---|---|
| New funding (Seed-Series B) | Budget unlocked, pressure to grow fast | 3-5x vs. no trigger | 2-6 weeks post-announcement |
| Leadership change (relevant role) | New exec evaluating vendors, wants quick wins | 2-3x | 2-4 weeks into tenure |
| Rapid headcount growth (10%+ in 90 days) | Scaling pains, systematization needed | 2-4x | Within 30 days of growth spike |
| Competitive loss / market shift | Pain amplified by external pressure | 2-3x | Within 2 weeks of event |
| Tech stack change (relevant tool) | Integration gaps, process redesign | 2-3x | 30-60 days post-implementation |
| M&A activity | Integration chaos = tool evaluation | 2-4x | 60-90 days post-announcement |

### The ICP Validation Test

Can you describe your ideal customer in a single sentence that a researcher could use to
find 1,000 specific people? If not, the ICP is too broad.

**Example of weak ICP**: "Marketing leaders at B2B companies"
**Example of strong ICP**: "VP of Demand Generation at B2B SaaS companies, 50-500 employees,
Series A or B, who have recently hired SDRs or are actively hiring them (indicating they're
building or scaling outbound)"

### The ICP Scoring Matrix

Score each potential ICP against these dimensions to prioritize which segments to build first:

```
ICP Priority Score (0-100)

Pain Intensity (30 points max):
  + 15 pts: Problem is urgent (costs them money/time weekly)
  + 10 pts: They're actively searching for solutions (intent signals visible)
  + 5 pts: Clear trigger events are trackable

Accessibility (30 points max):
  + 10 pts: Can find 500+ contacts matching criteria
  + 10 pts: Email addresses obtainable with >95% accuracy
  + 10 pts: Enrichment data available (triggers, tech stack, news)

Revenue Potential (20 points max):
  + 10 pts: ACV supports cold email CAC ($5K+ ACV)
  + 10 pts: Deal cycle <90 days from first meeting

Proof Availability (20 points max):
  + 10 pts: Have a directly comparable case study
  + 10 pts: Can reference specific, verifiable results for this ICP
```

**Score interpretation**:
- 80-100: Primary ICP — build first, allocate most resources
- 60-79: Secondary ICP — build after primary is producing meetings
- 40-59: Tertiary ICP — test with a small campaign before investing
- Below 40: Deprioritize — insufficient evidence or accessibility

## 2. The Five Segmentation Layers

Layer your segmentation from broadest to most precise. Each layer narrows the list and
increases relevance. Not all five are always applicable — use the layers that genuinely
change your message.

### Layer 1: Firmographic Segmentation (The Base)
Industry, company size, location, revenue, headcount. This is where most operators stop.
It's necessary but insufficient.

**What it tells you**: Who this company is
**What it doesn't tell you**: Whether they're in a buying moment or care about your specific angle

**Performance baseline**: Firmographic-only segmentation typically produces 2-4% reply rates.
Adding even one additional layer typically doubles this.

### Layer 2: Technographic Segmentation (The Stack)
What tools do they use? Tech stack reveals:
- Budget and sophistication level (Salesforce + Marketo + Gong = enterprise with budget)
- Current solution (using HubSpot CRM = can position against HubSpot's limitations)
- Adjacent pain (using Outreach but not Gong = discovery gap you could fill)
- Integration requirements (they need to connect with what they have)

**Sources**: BuiltWith, Clearbit, ZoomInfo, Apollo enrichment, job postings (tech mentioned in JDs)

**Technographic segmentation impact on performance**:

| Approach | Typical Reply Rate | Example |
|---|---|---|
| No tech filter | 2-3% | "VP Sales at SaaS companies" |
| Basic tech filter | 4-6% | "VP Sales at SaaS companies using Salesforce" |
| Competitive tech filter | 6-10% | "VP Sales using Salesforce + Outreach, no Gong" |
| Tech + trigger | 8-15%+ | "VP Sales using Salesforce + Outreach, no Gong, recently hired RevOps" |

See `references/ai-personalization.md` for Clay-based waterfall enrichment that automates
technographic data collection at scale.

### Layer 3: Behavioral Segmentation (The Intent)
What are they doing right now that signals a buying moment?
- Recent job postings (SDR roles = building outbound; RevOps = systematizing)
- LinkedIn content (commenting on pain-related posts = engaged with the problem)
- Content downloads (your content or competitor's)
- Website visits (for warm audiences)
- Review site activity (G2 page visits = active evaluation)

### Layer 4: Trigger-Based Segmentation (The Moment)
External events that create purchase windows:

| Trigger | Why It Matters | How to Find It | Optimal Timing | Decay Rate |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| New funding (Seed-Series B) | Budget unlocked, pressure to grow fast | Crunchbase, PitchBook, LinkedIn news | 2-6 weeks post | Signal decays 80% by week 10 |
| Leadership change | New exec evaluating vendors, wants wins | LinkedIn "Started new position" | 2-4 weeks into tenure | Decays 70% by month 3 |
| Headcount growth (10%+ in 90 days) | Scaling pains, systematization needed | LinkedIn, Bombora | Within 30 days | Decays 60% by month 2 |
| New product launch | Need for new GTM motion | Press releases, ProductHunt | 1-4 weeks post | Decays 75% by month 2 |
| Competitor weakness/outage | Pain amplified by alternative failure | G2 reviews, Twitter/X | Within 1-2 weeks | Decays 90% by month 1 |
| Regulatory change | Compliance urgency | Industry publications | Pre-deadline rush | Spikes near deadline |
| Recent M&A | Integration chaos = tool evaluation | Crunchbase, press | 60-90 days post | Long tail (6+ months) |

**The trigger principle**: A message arriving the week after funding closes converts 3-5x
better than the same message 3 months later. Timing is relevance.

### Layer 5: Psychographic Segmentation (The Mind)
Values, beliefs, and decision-making style of the individual:

| Psychographic Profile | Indicators | Copy Adaptation | Proof Type |
|---|---|---|---|
| Data-driven | LinkedIn posts cite metrics, analytical role titles | Lead with numbers, ROI | Quantitative results, benchmarks |
| Relationship-driven | Community-focused content, people-first language | Lead with team impact | Testimonials, referrals |
| Risk-averse | Enterprise, regulated industry, long tenure | Lead with safety, compliance | Category leaders, large logos |
| Growth-oriented | Startup, recently promoted, "scaling" language | Lead with speed, competitive edge | Fast results, startup case studies |
| Technical | Engineering background, technical content | Lead with mechanism, how it works | Architecture diagrams, integrations |

Derive from: LinkedIn content style, company culture signals, job descriptions they post,
industry they operate in.

See `references/ai-personalization.md` for using Claygent to automatically classify
psychographic profiles from LinkedIn data at scale.

## 3. Intent Signals — The Elite Edge

Intent signals separate "fits our ICP" from "is in a buying moment right now." The best cold
email programs don't just find fits — they find fits at the exact moment they're most likely
to buy. This is the move from cold email to warm-feeling cold email.

### Intent Signal Hierarchy

| Signal Type | Strength | Conversion Lift | Source | Shelf Life |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Visited your pricing page (2x in 7 days) | Very High | 8-12x vs. cold | Your analytics, RB2B | 48-72 hours |
| Downloaded BOF content (case study, ROI calc) | High | 5-8x | Your CMS, marketing automation | 1-2 weeks |
| G2 category page visit | High | 4-7x | Bombora, G2 Buyer Intent | 1-2 weeks |
| Competitor review activity | High | 4-6x | G2, TrustRadius | 2-3 weeks |
| Job posting for relevant role | Medium-High | 3-5x | LinkedIn, Indeed | 30-60 days |
| LinkedIn engagement on pain-related content | Medium | 2-4x | LinkedIn Sales Navigator | 1-2 weeks |
| Recent funding announcement | Medium | 3-5x | Crunchbase, PitchBook | 2-8 weeks |
| New executive in relevant role | Medium | 2-3x | LinkedIn | 2-8 weeks |
| Webinar attendance (>50% watched) | Medium | 3-5x | Your platform | 1-2 weeks |
| Previous "not now" reply | Medium | 3-5x (at re-engagement) | Instantly CRM | 90+ days wait |

### First-Party Intent (Strongest)
Behavior on your own properties:
- Visited your pricing page (2+ times in 7 days = hot)
- Downloaded bottom-of-funnel assets (case study, ROI calculator, comparison guide)
- Attended your webinar or watched >50% of a video
- Engaged repeatedly with your LinkedIn content
- Replied to a previous campaign with "not now" (they're aware — re-engage at trigger)

**Instantly integration**: Use webhooks + n8n/Make to push first-party intent signals into
Instantly campaigns. A pricing page visitor who matches your ICP can enter a dedicated
"high-intent" campaign automatically within minutes of their visit.

See `references/multichannel-orchestration.md` for cross-channel intent signal aggregation.

### Third-Party Intent (Strong)
Behavior on external platforms:
- G2 category page visits (Bombora, G2 Buyer Intent)
- Competitor review page activity
- Relevant industry publication engagement
- Job postings for roles that signal your pain (hiring 5 SDRs = outbound investment)
- LinkedIn posts about the problem you solve

**Sources**: Bombora, G2 Buyer Intent, 6sense, Demandbase

### Trigger-Based Intent (Contextual)
Events that create urgency:
- New funding (6 weeks post-announcement is the sweet spot — before the chaos settles)
- New executive hire in relevant role (2-4 weeks into tenure — still evaluating everything)
- Company milestone announcements (product launch, expansion, partnership)

### The Intent-Matched Sequence

Build separate campaigns for each intent tier:

```
Campaign A: High-Intent (pricing page + ICP match)
  → Faster cadence (Day 1, Day 2, Day 5)
  → Direct offer hook ("noticed you were looking at X...")
  → Shorter sequence (3-4 steps)
  → Immediate CTA: book a call
  → Expected reply rate: 15-30%
  → Expected positive reply rate: 60-75% of replies

Campaign B: Trigger-Matched (funding + ICP match)
  → Standard cadence (Day 1, Day 3, Day 7)
  → Timing hook ("congrats on the Series B, typically when teams...")
  → 4-6 step sequence
  → CTA: low-friction reply question
  → Expected reply rate: 8-15%
  → Expected positive reply rate: 50-65% of replies

Campaign C: Fit-Only (ICP match, no strong signal)
  → Slower cadence (Day 1, Day 4, Day 9)
  → Problem hook or social proof hook
  → 5-7 step sequence
  → CTA: curiosity question or resource offer
  → Expected reply rate: 3-7%
  → Expected positive reply rate: 35-50% of replies
```

## 4. List Building — Sources and Methods

### The Source Hierarchy

| Tier | Sources | Expected Data Quality | Verification Rate | Cost/Lead |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Tier 1 — Highest** | Manual LinkedIn + Sales Nav, trigger-based lists, Bombora/G2 intent, SuperSearch + waterfall | 90-95% email accuracy pre-verification | 95-98% post-verification | $0.15-0.50 |
| **Tier 2 — Good** | Apollo (tight filters + verify), ZoomInfo/Clearbit exports with enrichment, Hunter.io domain targeting | 80-90% accuracy pre-verification | 92-96% post-verification | $0.05-0.20 |
| **Tier 3 — Caution** | Raw Sales Nav exports (no enrichment), database dumps, purchased lists | 60-75% accuracy | 75-85% post-verification | $0.01-0.10 |

**The cost-quality tradeoff**: Tier 1 lists at $0.30/lead producing 10% reply rates cost
$3/reply. Tier 3 lists at $0.03/lead producing 2% reply rates cost $1.50/reply — but the
deliverability damage from higher bounce rates makes Tier 3 more expensive in reality when
you factor in domain rehabilitation costs.

### SuperSearch + Waterfall Enrichment (Instantly Native)
Instantly's SuperSearch queries 450M+ B2B contacts through multiple providers sequentially:
- Filters: job title, company size, industry, revenue, tech stack, funding status, location
- Natural language queries: "Founders of B2B SaaS companies in NYC with 50-200 employees"
- Waterfall enrichment: queries 5+ data providers sequentially if primary source fails
- Saves searches for evergreen campaigns (auto-populates new leads matching criteria)

**Best practice**: Use SuperSearch for initial list generation, then enrich with specific
trigger data (recent funding, job changes) from Crunchbase/LinkedIn before import.

See `references/ai-personalization.md` for Clay-based waterfall enrichment that extends
SuperSearch with 75+ data providers and AI-powered research.

### List Building Protocols

**The research-before-build approach** (for high-value segments, ACV > $25K):
1. Define the specific pain and the trigger that makes it acute
2. Find 50-100 accounts that match both before building a contact list
3. For each account, identify the right contact (title, tenure, decision authority)
4. Manually verify 10-20 before scaling list build
5. Enrich with personalization data (recent news, LinkedIn post, specific pain signal)
6. Verify emails (target <1% bounce rate before sending)
7. Expected output: 200-500 contacts/week at 8-15% reply rate

**The automated build approach** (for scale, ACV < $15K):
1. Define tight filters in SuperSearch or Apollo
2. Export with enrichment fields (company name cleanup, title normalization)
3. Run through Instantly's email verification
4. Segment by meaningful variables before import into campaigns
5. Reserve 100-200 contacts for initial testing before full list send
6. Expected output: 1,000-5,000 contacts/week at 3-7% reply rate

## 5. List Hygiene — The Non-Negotiable

List hygiene is what Instantly's warmup and infrastructure protect. Without clean lists,
even perfect infrastructure fails. See `references/infrastructure-deliverability.md` for
the domain reputation thresholds that list hygiene protects.

### Target Metrics

| Metric | Target | Warning | Critical |
|---|---|---|---|
| Hard bounce rate | ≤ 1% | 1-2% | > 2% — pause and re-verify |
| Spam complaint rate | < 0.05% | 0.05-0.1% | > 0.3% — Google enforcement threshold |
| Email verification rate (pre-send) | ≥ 97% | 95-97% | < 95% — re-verify entire list |
| Catch-all address percentage | < 15% | 15-25% | > 25% — high bounce risk |
| Data freshness | < 30 days since verification | 30-60 days | > 60 days — re-verify |

### Pre-Campaign Hygiene Protocol

**Step 1: Verification**
- Run all new lists through email verification before import
- Instantly's built-in verify-catch-all flags risky addresses
- Target: remove all invalid addresses; treat catch-alls with caution
- External options: NeverBounce, ZeroBounce, Millionverifier for bulk verification
- **Benchmark**: Top operators achieve < 0.5% hard bounce rate with proper pre-verification

**Step 2: Deduplication**
- Check against all existing Instantly lists (global block list)
- Check against CRM (exclude existing customers, active opportunities, do-not-contacts)
- Check against previous campaigns (don't re-contact unless intentional re-engagement)
- Use Instantly's global block list for permanent exclusions (unsubscribes, competitors, etc.)
- See `references/compliance-legal.md` for mandatory exclusion lists by jurisdiction

**Step 3: Data Quality**
- Clean company names (Instantly's Copilot "Company Name Cleanup" template)
- Normalize job titles (inconsistent titles break personalization variables)
- Fill missing fields that personalization variables reference
- Remove contacts with insufficient data for your personalization strategy

**Data quality scoring per contact**:
```
Complete (all fields present, verified email, trigger data)     → Priority send
Partial (verified email, basic firmographic, no trigger data)   → Standard send
Minimal (verified email only, limited data)                     → Low priority / enrich first
Incomplete (unverified email OR missing critical fields)        → Do not send
```

**Step 4: Segmentation Validation**
- Spot-check 10% of the list manually before launch (minimum 20 contacts)
- Verify that the ICP criteria are actually met (not just claimed by the data source)
- Confirm personalization data accuracy for the first 20-30 contacts
- **Common failure**: Apollo reports "VP Sales" but the person is actually "Sales Associate" —
  title accuracy from any single provider averages 75-85%

### Ongoing Hygiene Operations

**Bounce Management**:
- Hard bounces: remove immediately from all lists; add to block list
- Soft bounces: flag and monitor; remove after 2-3 soft bounces
- Instantly's auto-pause on high bounce rate is a safety net, not a strategy
- Review bounce codes to diagnose patterns:

| Bounce Code | Meaning | Action |
|---|---|---|
| 550 | Mailbox doesn't exist | Hard bounce — remove immediately, add to block list |
| 551 | User not local | Hard bounce — remove immediately |
| 552 | Exceeded storage | Soft bounce — retry in 7 days, remove after 2 occurrences |
| 553 | Mailbox name invalid | Hard bounce — remove, check data source quality |
| 554 | Transaction failed (spam block) | Deliverability issue — check blacklists, see `infrastructure-deliverability.md` |

**Engagement-Based Cleaning**:
- Contacts who don't open across 3+ campaigns → remove or move to re-engagement segment
- Contacts who open but never reply across 5+ campaigns → test new angle then remove
- Stale contacts (no engagement in 6+ months) → re-verify before any new campaign

**The Tiered Engagement Model**:
```
Active (opened/replied in last 30 days)   → Normal cadence, full sequence
Warming (engaged 30-90 days ago)           → Lighter cadence, different angle
Cold (no engagement 90+ days)              → Re-engagement campaign or remove
Dormant (no engagement 6+ months)          → Re-verify before any contact
Dead (bounced or unsubscribed)             → Block list — never contact again
```

### List Decay Rate Reference

| Data Type | Monthly Decay Rate | Annual Decay | Implication |
|---|---|---|---|
| Email addresses | 2-3% | 22-31% | Re-verify lists older than 60 days |
| Job titles | 1-2% | 12-22% | Title-dependent targeting needs quarterly refresh |
| Company data (size, funding) | 1-3% | 12-31% | Trigger data expires fastest |
| Phone numbers | 1-2% | 12-22% | Cross-reference before phone campaigns |

## 6. Micro-Segmentation in Practice

The practical implementation of the segmentation framework inside Instantly:

### Building Segments as Separate Campaigns

The most important architectural decision: each meaningful segment gets its own campaign
with its own copy, hook, cadence, and testing setup. Never combine segments that should
receive fundamentally different messages.

**Segment separation criteria** — create separate campaigns when:
- The hook type would be different (problem vs. trigger vs. social proof)
- The specific pain you're referencing is different
- The company stage requires different language (startup vs. enterprise)
- The intent level is different (high-intent vs. fit-only)
- The industry context changes the proof points you'd use

See `references/campaign-architecture.md` for the full sequence design framework for
each campaign type.

**Example micro-segment breakdown for a sales tool**:
```
Campaign 1: "Series A SaaS founders, no dedicated sales hire yet"
  Hook: Problem ("most founders hate selling their own product")
  Expected reply rate: 5-8%
  Segment size: 200-400 contacts
  Refresh cadence: Monthly (new funding rounds)

Campaign 2: "Series B SaaS, first SDR hired in last 90 days"
  Hook: Trigger ("just started building outbound")
  Expected reply rate: 8-12%
  Segment size: 50-150 contacts
  Refresh cadence: Weekly (new hires)

Campaign 3: "VP Sales, Salesforce + no outbound sequencer in stack"
  Hook: Technographic gap ("you're running $X in Salesforce but...")
  Expected reply rate: 6-10%
  Segment size: 300-500 contacts
  Refresh cadence: Quarterly (tech stack changes)

Campaign 4: "Head of RevOps, company posted 3+ GTM roles in 30 days"
  Hook: Trigger ("scaling fast usually means messy pipeline")
  Expected reply rate: 10-15%
  Segment size: 50-100 contacts
  Refresh cadence: Weekly (new job postings)
```

Each campaign above has a different person, different pain, different moment. Sending the
same email to all four would average across all of them and perform below any individual.

### Segment Personalization Mapping

For each segment, define:

```
SEGMENT MAPPING TEMPLATE

Segment Name: [descriptive name]
Target Persona: [exact title + company type]
Primary Pain Line: [one sentence — their specific frustration, in their language]
Trigger Reference: [the specific event/signal you're referencing]
Proof Match: [most relevant case study for THIS segment]
CTA Fit: [lowest-friction ask for where they are]
Custom Variables: [which {{variables}} and from what data field]
Hook Type: [Problem / Timeline / Social Proof / Curiosity / Direct]
Expected Reply Rate: [based on intent tier]
A/Z Test Priority: [what to test first for this segment]
```

## 7. The Segment Sizing Rule

Counter-intuitive but data-backed: **smaller, more targeted segments consistently outperform
large broad lists.** Reply rates drop significantly when a segmented list exceeds ~100-300
contacts, because at that size you're usually diluting the targeting.

### Optimal Segment Sizes by Campaign Type

| Campaign Type | Optimal Size | Minimum for Testing | Maximum Before Splitting |
|---|---|---|---|
| High-intent (pricing page + ICP) | 20-100 | 20 | 100 |
| Trigger-matched (funding + ICP) | 50-200 | 50 | 200 |
| Firmographic + persona (tight) | 200-500 | 100 | 500 |
| Firmographic-only (broad) | 500-1,000 | 200 | 1,000 |

**Statistical validity requirements**:
- Minimum 50 contacts per campaign for directional data
- Minimum 150-200 contacts per A/Z variant for statistically significant testing
- Below 50 contacts: valid for outreach, invalid for testing conclusions

See `references/analytics-optimization.md` for the full A/Z testing statistical methodology.

**The evergreen campaign model** (Instantly native):
Use SuperSearch saved searches to automatically populate campaigns as new contacts matching
your criteria become available. This creates a continuously running, always-fresh pipeline
rather than a one-time blast.

### Segment Refresh Cadences

| Segment Type | Refresh Frequency | Method |
|---|---|---|
| Trigger-based (funding, hires) | Weekly | Automated via SuperSearch saved search + webhook |
| Technographic | Monthly | Re-run BuiltWith/Clearbit queries; update Clay tables |
| Firmographic-only | Quarterly | Re-pull from Apollo/ZoomInfo with updated filters |
| Intent-based (G2, Bombora) | Daily-Weekly | Automated feed from intent provider |
| Re-engagement (previous contacts) | Quarterly | Pull from Instantly CRM: replied "not now" 90+ days ago |

## 8. Dynamic Segmentation and List Maintenance

Lists are not static assets. They decay at ~2-3% per month (job changes, company shutdowns,
email address changes). Elite operators treat lists as living systems.

### Trigger-Based Dynamic Segments

Set up automation (n8n or Make + Instantly webhook) to:
- Auto-add contacts who match a trigger (e.g., just raised funding + ICP match) to the
  relevant campaign within 24-48 hours of the event
- Auto-remove contacts who've been promoted out of ICP role
- Auto-move contacts between campaigns based on engagement (opened 3x but no reply →
  move to higher-touch, different angle campaign)

**Automation architecture**:
```
Source Trigger          → Filter Logic              → Destination Campaign
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Crunchbase webhook      → Matches ICP criteria?     → "Funded Companies" campaign
  (new funding round)     Series A-B + SaaS + 50-500
                          employees?

LinkedIn webhook        → Matches ICP title?        → "New Exec" campaign
  (job change alert)      VP/Head of + relevant
                          function?

Instantly webhook       → Opened 3x, no reply?      → "Re-angle" campaign
  (engagement data)       AND in fit-only campaign?    (different hook type)

G2/Bombora feed         → In target category?       → "High-Intent" campaign
  (buyer intent signal)   AND matches ICP firmographic? (faster cadence)
```

See `references/ai-personalization.md` for Clay-based automation that enriches each new
contact before they enter the campaign.

### The Re-Engagement Protocol

For contacts who previously received a campaign with no positive reply:

| Dimension | Requirement | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Wait period | Minimum 90 days | Shorter intervals damage sender reputation |
| Angle | Completely different hook type and opening line | Same message = same result + reputation risk |
| Trigger | Must reference something that has changed | Justifies the re-contact |
| Sequence length | 3-4 steps maximum | Shorter — they already know you |
| Expected performance | 3-5x vs. cold (for "not now" replies); 1.5-2x (for no response) | Previous awareness gives a lift |

**Re-engagement sequence template**:
```
Step 1 (Day 1): Reference time passed + what's changed + new angle
  "It's been a few months since we last connected — since then, [new development
  or new proof point]. Has [original pain] become more of a priority?"

Step 2 (Day 4): New proof or resource
  "Wanted to share this — [new case study/result since last contact].
  Thought it might be relevant given [their situation]."

Step 3 (Day 10): Clean close
  "Last note on this — if the timing still isn't right, completely understand.
  If [pain] becomes a priority, I'm here."
```

## 9. Lead Scoring — Prioritizing Within Segments

Not all leads within a segment are equal. Lead scoring determines send order and
campaign assignment within a qualified list.

### The Lead Score Model

```
Lead Score (0-100)

Fit Score (40 points max):
  Title match to ideal persona          0-15 pts
  Company size match to sweet spot      0-10 pts
  Industry match (exact vs. adjacent)   0-10 pts
  Tech stack match                      0-5 pts

Intent Score (40 points max):
  First-party intent signal             0-20 pts (pricing page = 20, blog = 5)
  Third-party intent signal             0-10 pts (G2 visit = 10, job posting = 5)
  Trigger event recency                 0-10 pts (this week = 10, this month = 5, >60 days = 0)

Accessibility Score (20 points max):
  Email verified                        0-10 pts (verified = 10, catch-all = 3, unverified = 0)
  Personalization data completeness     0-5 pts
  Previous engagement history           0-5 pts (replied before = 5, opened = 3, new = 0)
```

**Score-based routing**:

| Score Range | Action | Campaign Type |
|---|---|---|
| 80-100 | Immediate outreach, highest priority | High-intent sequence (fast cadence) |
| 60-79 | Standard outreach | Trigger-matched sequence |
| 40-59 | Queue for enrichment, then outreach | Fit-only sequence (slower cadence) |
| Below 40 | Hold — enrich or deprioritize | Do not send until score improves |

See `references/ai-personalization.md` for using Clay to automate lead scoring with
AI-powered enrichment and signal detection.

## 10. ICP Validation & Iteration Protocol

Your ICP is a hypothesis. It must be validated and refined based on actual campaign data.

### The Validation Cycle

```
Week 1-4: Initial ICP hypothesis → Build list → Launch campaign
Week 4-8: Collect data → Analyze which contacts reply positively → Pattern-match

Questions to answer at Week 8:
  - Which company sizes reply most? (narrow or widen range)
  - Which titles convert to meetings? (adjust persona)
  - Which triggers correlate with positive replies? (double down)
  - Which industries have highest positive reply rate? (specialize or broaden)
  - What objections repeat? (adjust messaging or ICP)
```

### ICP Iteration Decision Matrix

| Signal | Action |
|---|---|
| Reply rate > 8% in a sub-segment | Expand this sub-segment; create dedicated campaign |
| Reply rate < 2% after 500+ sends | Retire segment OR fundamentally re-angle |
| Meetings don't convert to pipeline | ICP fit issue — they respond but aren't real buyers |
| "Not now" replies > 30% of total replies | Timing issue — adjust trigger criteria |
| "Wrong person" replies > 10% | Title targeting is off — adjust persona |
| Positive replies but no case study match | Build new proof for this segment before scaling |

### Closed-Loop ICP Refinement

The most powerful refinement comes from connecting email performance to closed revenue.
See `references/analytics-optimization.md` for the full attribution framework.

```
Monthly: Which ICP segments produce the most meetings?
Quarterly: Which ICP segments produce the most closed revenue?
Bi-annually: Which ICP segments have the highest LTV?

→ The segment that produces the most revenue gets the most resources
→ The segment with highest LTV refines your ICP definition
→ Everything else is secondary
```

## 11. Common ICP and Segmentation Failures

### Failure Pattern Reference

| Failure Pattern | Symptom | Root Cause | Fix |
|---|---|---|---|
| **The Broad ICP Trap** | 1-3% reply rate, generic objections | "CEOs of companies with 10-500 employees" — range too wide | Narrow to 2-3 sub-segments with distinct copy per segment |
| **The Firmographic-Only Trap** | Low reply rate despite "right" companies | No situation, trigger, or intent layer | Add Layer 3-4 segmentation; prioritize trigger-based contacts |
| **The Data Assumption Trap** | High bounce rate (>3%), wrong-person replies | Trusting provider data without verification | Always verify before sending; expect 15-25% annual data decay |
| **The Personalization Theater Trap** | Reply rate same as non-personalized | `{{firstName}}` and `{{companyName}}` only | Real personalization references specific pain, trigger, or context unique to the segment |
| **The Over-Segmentation Trap** | 47 segments with 10 contacts each, no test validity | Segments too small for statistical significance | Minimum 50 contacts/campaign; find meaningful differences, not slight variations |
| **The Re-Contact Without Change Trap** | Spam complaints, reputation damage | Same message to same contact after no/negative reply | Wait 90+ days, bring genuinely new angle/trigger, shorter sequence |
| **The Scale-Before-Validate Trap** | Declining metrics as volume increases | Scaled a segment before proving it works | Validate with 200-300 contacts first; see `references/analytics-optimization.md` for scaling prerequisites |
| **The Stale List Trap** | Bounce rate creeping up over time | Lists older than 60 days without re-verification | Re-verify any list not used within 60 days; monitor decay rate monthly |
