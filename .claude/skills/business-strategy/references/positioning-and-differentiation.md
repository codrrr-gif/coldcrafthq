## Part 1: Positioning (Ries & Trout)

### The Core Concept

Positioning is NOT what you do to the product. Positioning is what you do to the
MIND of the prospect.

In an overcommunicated society, the mind filters out most information. The only way
to cut through is to have a simplified, focused message that fits what the mind already
knows or believes. You don't "create" something new in the mind — you manipulate
what's already there, retying the connections that already exist.

"The basic approach of positioning is not to create something new and different, but
to manipulate what's already up there in the mind, to retie the connections that
already exist."

### The Overcommunicated Society

The average person is exposed to thousands of marketing messages per day.
The mind has developed a defense mechanism: it screens and rejects most information.
The mind accepts only new information that matches its prior knowledge or experience.
It filters out everything else.

**Implication:** You cannot fight the mind. You must work with it. Your positioning
must be consistent with what the prospect already believes.

### Mental Ladders

For every product category, the prospect has a mental "ladder" — a ranking of brands.
The top rung is the brand that comes to mind first. Most ladders have 2-3 rungs maximum.
Some have only one.

- Rental cars: Hertz → Avis → National
- Cola: Coke → Pepsi
- Search: Google → (distant second)

**The Law of the Ladder:** Your marketing strategy should depend on which rung
you occupy. If you're #2, you need a different strategy than #1. If you're not on
the ladder at all, you need to create a new ladder (new category).

"Many marketing people live in a fool's paradise. They are convinced that the
better product will win. It won't."

### Being First: The Law of Leadership

It's better to be first than to be better. The first brand in any category gets
disproportionate mind share. The leader gets "twice the market share of the
second brand, which in turn has twice the market share of the third."

If you can't be first in a category, create a new category where you CAN be first.

Examples:
- Charles Lindbergh was first to fly solo across the Atlantic. Who was second? (Bert Hinkler. Nobody remembers.)
- IBM was first in computers. Apple couldn't beat IBM at "computers" — so they created "personal computers."
- Red Bull didn't enter the "soft drink" category. They created "energy drinks."

### Cherchez le Creneau — Finding the Hole

Look for the hole in the prospect's mind that no one else has filled.

**Size creneau:** Volkswagen Beetle — "Think Small" in an era of big American cars.
**Price creneau:** Michelob — first premium domestic beer. High price IS the positioning.
**Sex/identity creneau:** Marlboro — first masculine cigarette. Virginia Slims — first feminine.
**Time creneau:** Nyquil — first nighttime cold remedy.
**Distribution creneau:** L'eggs — first hosiery in supermarkets.

**The Traps:**
- **Factory creneau** — filling a hole in YOUR product line, not in the prospect's mind (Ford Edsel)
- **Technology creneau** — a great technical achievement that has no mental slot (Frost 8/80 dry white whisky)
- **Everybody trap** — trying to appeal to everyone and standing for nothing

### Repositioning the Competition

When there's no creneau available, create one by REPOSITIONING the competition.
Move the existing brand out of its position to make room for yours.

**Tylenol vs. aspirin:** "For the millions who should not take aspirin." Repositioned aspirin
as dangerous (stomach irritation, bleeding), then positioned Tylenol as the safe alternative.
Result: Tylenol became #1.

**Scope vs. Listerine:** Two words: "Medicine breath." Repositioned Listerine's strength
(strong taste = effective) as a weakness (bad taste = unpleasant). Scope took #2.

**Stolichnaya vs. American vodkas:** Exposed that "Russian-sounding" brands like Smirnoff
and Wolfschmidt were made in Connecticut and Indiana. Repositioned them as fakes.

**Key principle:** Repositioning is NOT comparative advertising ("we're better than them").
It's about changing how the prospect perceives the COMPETITOR, which opens up space
for you. "If your brand is so good, how come it's not the leader?" kills comparative ads.

### The Power of the Name

A strong name is the first and most important positioning tool.

**Good names:** Head & Shoulders (tells you what it does), People Express (populist airline),
Close-Up (intimacy toothpaste)

**Bad names:** Those that don't communicate anything or try to stretch across categories.

**The Line Extension Trap:** The most common positioning mistake. Taking a successful
brand name and applying it to a new product in a different category.

Examples of line extension disasters:
- Xerox tried to extend from copiers to computers → failed
- Bayer tried to extend from aspirin to non-aspirin pain reliever → confused consumers

"Line extension is the most common trap of all. What makes it so insidious is that
it works in the short term. In the long run, it erodes the brand's position."

**The inverse relationship:** The more products you hang on a brand name, the weaker
the name becomes. The specialist beats the generalist. The narrow focus beats the
broad focus.

### Positioning Strategies by Market Position

**If you're the leader:**
- Reinforce your position ("the real thing" — Coke)
- Cover competitive moves with multi-brand strategy (P&G's multiple detergent brands)
- Avoid saying "we're #1" — it's insecure and invites attack
- The power of the leader is in the position itself, not in the company

**If you're the follower:**
- Find the creneau — the unoccupied position
- Reposition the leader — change how people see the leader to open space for you
- "Cherchez le creneau" — look for the hole, don't try to outdo the leader at their game
- Avis: "We're #2. We try harder." — turned a weakness into a position

## Part 2: The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing (Ries & Trout)

### Law 1: Leadership
It's better to be first than it is to be better. The first brand in the category wins
disproportionate mind share. If you can't be first, see Law 2.

### Law 2: Category
If you can't be first in a category, set up a new category you CAN be first in.
DEC was first in minicomputers (couldn't beat IBM in mainframes). Dell was first
in direct-sale computers. Amelia Earhart was the third person to fly the Atlantic,
but the first WOMAN — a new category.

### Law 3: Mind
It's better to be first in the mind than first in the market. Being first in the market
only matters insofar as it helps you get into the mind first.

### Law 4: Perception
Marketing is not a battle of products, it's a battle of perceptions. There IS no
objective reality — only perception. What people believe is "true" IS true, for
marketing purposes. Honda is a motorcycle in Japan but a car in America — same
company, different perceptions.

### Law 5: Focus
The most powerful concept in marketing is owning a word in the prospect's mind.
- Federal Express owns "overnight"
- Volvo owns "safety"
- BMW owns "driving" (performance)
- Mercedes owns "engineering"
- Domino's owns "delivery" (pizza)

When you own a word, competitors cannot take it from you. But you must choose
ONE word. Trying to own two words = owning none.

### Law 6: Exclusivity
Two companies cannot own the same word in the prospect's mind. If a competitor
owns a word, it's futile to try to own the same word.
Burger King tried to own "fast" from McDonald's. Failed.
FedEx tried to take "worldwide" from DHL. Failed.

### Law 7: Ladder
Your strategy depends on which rung you occupy on the mental ladder.
#1 strategy ≠ #2 strategy ≠ #3 strategy. Avis succeeded by explicitly claiming #2.
Seven-Up succeeded by positioning as the "Un-Cola" (defining against #1 and #2).

### Law 8: Duality
In the long run, every market becomes a two-horse race. Coke/Pepsi. McDonald's/
Burger King. Nike/Adidas. The #3 brand is always in trouble.

### Law 9: Opposite
If you're #2, your strategy is determined by the leader. Find the leader's strength,
then present the OPPOSITE.

Coca-Cola was the established, traditional choice. Pepsi positioned as the "choice of
a new generation" — the opposite. Don't try to be better than the leader. Be DIFFERENT.

### Law 10: Division
Over time, categories divide into subcategories. Computers → mainframes,
minicomputers, workstations, PCs, laptops, tablets, phones. Each subcategory
needs its own brand. Leaders should launch new brands for new subcategories,
not extend the existing brand.

### Law 11: Perspective
Marketing effects take place over an extended period of time. Short-term results
often contradict long-term results. Sales (promotions) boost short-term revenue
but train customers to wait for the next sale, destroying long-term brand value.
Line extensions boost short-term sales but weaken long-term positioning.

### Law 12: Line Extension
There's an irresistible pressure to extend the equity of a brand. This almost always fails
in the long run. Less is more. Narrow the focus to strengthen the brand.

### Law 13: Sacrifice
You have to give up something to get something. Three things to sacrifice:
1. **Product line** — don't sell everything, specialize
2. **Target market** — don't appeal to everyone, focus
3. **Constant change** — don't change position every year

"The target is not the market. The apparent target of your marketing is not the same
as the people who will actually buy your product." (Pepsi targets teenagers but
sells to everyone who wants to feel young.)

### Law 14: Attributes
For every attribute, there is an opposite, effective attribute.
If the leader owns "cavity prevention" (Crest), own "taste" (Aim) or "fresh breath"
(Close-Up). Don't try to own the same attribute better.

### Law 15: Candor
When you admit a negative, the prospect will give you a positive.
"Avis is only No. 2 in rent-a-cars. So why go with us? We try harder."
"Listerine: the taste you hate, twice a day."
Admitting a weakness is disarming — it opens the mind to your positive message.

### Law 16: Singularity
In each situation, only one move will produce substantial results. The key to victory
is finding the one critical point of leverage — the singular, bold stroke — not
a diffuse effort on multiple fronts.

### Law 17: Unpredictability
You can't predict the future. But you CAN identify trends and build flexibility.
Write plans that work under multiple scenarios.

### Law 18: Success
Success often leads to arrogance. Arrogance leads to failure. Stay close to
the front lines. Don't let ego replace judgment.

### Law 19: Failure
Failure is to be expected and accepted. The key is to recognize failure early
and cut your losses. "The Japanese seem to be able to admit a mistake early
and then make the necessary changes."

### Law 20: Hype
The situation is often the opposite of the way it appears in the press. When
things are going well, you don't need hype. When they need hype, things
are not going well. Real revolutions arrive quietly.

### Law 21: Acceleration
Successful programs are not built on fads, they're built on trends.
A fad is a wave — it rises fast and falls fast. A trend is the tide — slow,
powerful, and lasting. Don't confuse the two.

### Law 22: Resources
Without adequate funding, an idea won't get off the ground. The best
positioning in the world is worthless without the money to establish it
in the mind. "You'll go further with a mediocre idea and a million dollars
than with a great idea alone."

## Part 3: Obviously Awesome (April Dunford)

### The Problem with Positioning

Most companies have a positioning problem but don't know it. Symptoms:
- Long sales cycles with lots of "education" needed
- Customers saying "I don't get it" or comparing you to the wrong things
- Features that sound impressive but don't connect to value
- Marketing that gets attention but doesn't convert

"Positioning defines how your product is a leader at delivering something that a
well-defined set of customers cares a lot about."

### The 5-Step Positioning Process

#### Step 1: Understand Your Competitive Alternatives

NOT "who are your competitors" — the question is "what would customers do if
you didn't exist?"

This includes:
- Direct competitors (other products in your category)
- Indirect competitors (different approaches to the same problem)
- Doing nothing (the status quo — often your biggest competitor)
- Manual workarounds (spreadsheets, hiring someone, DIY)

Start here because everything else is relative to the alternatives.

#### Step 2: Identify Your Unique Attributes

What do you have that the alternatives DON'T? These are features, capabilities,
processes, or properties that are genuinely unique to you.

Be rigorous: if a competitor also has it, it's not unique. List only what's truly
differentiated. This is usually a shorter list than you expect.

#### Step 3: Map Attributes to Value

Each unique attribute enables a VALUE for customers. Features are not value.
Value is the OUTCOME the feature creates for the customer.

"Encrypted messaging" (feature) → "Your messages can't be intercepted by hackers" (value)
"3x faster processing" (feature) → "You can serve 3x more customers without hiring" (value)

Only list value themes that matter — that customers would actually pay for.

#### Step 4: Identify Your Best-Fit Customers

Who cares MOST about the unique value you deliver? These are your best-fit
customers — the ones who will buy fastest, pay the most, churn least, and
refer others.

Characteristics of best-fit customers:
- They have the problem your unique value solves
- They know they have the problem (and care about solving it)
- They're already spending time/money trying to solve it
- Your unique value is a must-have for them, not a nice-to-have

### The 3 Market Positioning Styles

#### Style 1: Head to Head (Existing Market)
Position directly against established competitors in an existing category.

**When to use:** When you're genuinely better on the dimensions that matter
most in this category.

**Risk:** You're fighting on the incumbent's terms. They define what "good" means.

**How:** Identify the existing category. Show you're better on the key buying criteria.

#### Style 2: Big Fish, Small Pond (Subsegment)
Position as the dominant player in a well-defined subsegment of an existing market.

**When to use:** When you can't win the whole market but can dominate a niche.

**This is Moore's Bowling Alley strategy.** Own one segment completely, then expand.

**How:** Define the subsegment clearly. Show you're the best choice for that specific
group. The subsegment must be recognizable — customers must self-identify as part of it.

#### Style 3: Create a New Category
Position as the first and only player in an entirely new category you define.

**When to use:** When existing categories don't capture what you do, and trying
to fit into them creates confusion.

**Risk:** You have to educate the market on why this category exists and matters.
This is expensive and slow. Category creation is not for the faint-hearted.

**How:** Name the category. Define the problem it solves. Explain why existing
approaches are inadequate. Establish yourself as the obvious leader.

### Running a Positioning Exercise

Dunford recommends a team-based positioning workshop:

1. **Assemble the right team** — founders, marketing, sales, product, customer success
2. **Start with competitive alternatives** — list what customers would do without you
3. **List unique attributes** — what you have that alternatives don't
4. **Map to value** — translate each attribute to customer value
5. **Identify best-fit customers** — who cares most about this value
6. **Choose a market frame** — head-to-head, big fish/small pond, or new category
7. **Layer on a trend** (optional) — a relevant macro trend that makes your positioning more compelling

### Common Positioning Mistakes

1. **Positioning by analogy only** ("We're the Uber of X") — useful as a starting point
   but limits your thinking and may mislead
2. **Positioning for everyone** — trying to appeal to all segments means appealing
   strongly to none
3. **Positioning based on features** — customers don't buy features, they buy value
4. **Ignoring competitive alternatives** — your positioning must differentiate from
   what the customer would actually use instead
5. **Assuming positioning is permanent** — positioning should evolve as markets shift
6. **Confusing positioning with messaging** — positioning is the strategy; messaging
   and branding are the execution of that strategy

### Positioning → Messaging → Campaigns

```
POSITIONING (strategy)
    ↓
MESSAGING (how you communicate the position)
    ↓
CAMPAIGNS (specific marketing/sales executions)
```

Change your positioning FIRST. Then update messaging. Then update campaigns.
Most companies try to fix campaigns when the real problem is positioning.

## Part 4: Positioning Style Decision Matrix

This framework provides a systematic method for choosing between the three positioning
styles from Dunford's model: Head-to-Head, Big Fish Small Pond, and Create a New Category.
The decision is one of the highest-leverage choices a company makes — get it wrong and
even excellent execution will underperform.

### Evaluation Criteria

Score each criterion 1-5 for your situation. The scoring guidance below shows which
style each score range favors.

#### Criterion 1: Market Maturity

How established is the existing market category?

| Score | Description | Favors |
|-------|-------------|--------|
| 1 | No recognized category exists yet; prospects don't have language for the problem | Create a New Category |
| 2 | Emerging category with early adopters but no consensus definition | Create a New Category or Big Fish Small Pond |
| 3 | Growing category with 3-5 recognized players and an accepted definition | Big Fish Small Pond |
| 4 | Mature category with entrenched leaders and well-understood buying criteria | Head-to-Head or Big Fish Small Pond |
| 5 | Hyper-mature/commoditized category with price-driven competition | Big Fish Small Pond (escape the commodity trap) |

#### Criterion 2: Competitive Density

How many credible competitors exist in the space you are considering?

| Score | Description | Favors |
|-------|-------------|--------|
| 1 | 0-1 competitors; wide open space | Create a New Category (you can define the field) |
| 2 | 2-3 competitors; early-stage market | Head-to-Head (if you can beat them) or Create a New Category |
| 3 | 4-7 competitors; moderately crowded | Big Fish Small Pond (differentiate by segment) |
| 4 | 8-15 competitors; crowded and noisy | Big Fish Small Pond (you need a niche to stand out) |
| 5 | 15+ competitors; saturated | Big Fish Small Pond or Create a New Category (the old category is too crowded) |

#### Criterion 3: Available Budget and Resources

What is your marketing and sales budget relative to the leading competitor?

| Score | Description | Favors |
|-------|-------------|--------|
| 1 | Less than 5% of the leader's budget | Big Fish Small Pond (cannot afford broad competition) |
| 2 | 5-15% of the leader's budget | Big Fish Small Pond |
| 3 | 15-40% of the leader's budget | Big Fish Small Pond or Create a New Category |
| 4 | 40-80% of the leader's budget | Head-to-Head becomes viable |
| 5 | Equal to or exceeding the leader's budget | Head-to-Head (you can compete on their terms) |

#### Criterion 4: Brand Awareness

How well-known is your brand among target buyers today?

| Score | Description | Favors |
|-------|-------------|--------|
| 1 | No awareness; unknown brand | Big Fish Small Pond (build reputation in a niche first) |
| 2 | Known by a small group of early adopters | Big Fish Small Pond or Create a New Category |
| 3 | Moderate awareness in one segment | Big Fish Small Pond (expand from your known base) |
| 4 | Broad awareness but not category-defining | Head-to-Head |
| 5 | Widely recognized brand with strong associations | Head-to-Head |

#### Criterion 5: Product Differentiation Strength

How meaningfully different is your product from the dominant alternatives?

| Score | Description | Favors |
|-------|-------------|--------|
| 1 | Nearly identical to competitors on all key dimensions | Big Fish Small Pond (differentiate by who you serve, not what you are) |
| 2 | Minor differences on secondary features | Big Fish Small Pond |
| 3 | Clear differences on 1-2 important dimensions | Head-to-Head (if those dimensions are what the market values most) |
| 4 | Significantly different approach or architecture | Create a New Category or Head-to-Head |
| 5 | Fundamentally different — existing categories feel forced | Create a New Category |

#### Criterion 6: Customer Education Required

How much do prospects need to learn before they can evaluate your product?

| Score | Description | Favors |
|-------|-------------|--------|
| 1 | Zero education — they already know the category and buying criteria | Head-to-Head |
| 2 | Minimal education — they understand the category but need to learn your differentiators | Head-to-Head or Big Fish Small Pond |
| 3 | Moderate education — they know the problem but not this type of solution | Big Fish Small Pond |
| 4 | Significant education — they may not realize they have the problem yet | Create a New Category (with caution) |
| 5 | Massive education — you must first convince them the problem exists | Create a New Category (very expensive; consider whether Big Fish Small Pond can reduce the education burden) |

#### Criterion 7: Sales Cycle Tolerance

How long can you afford your sales cycle to be?

| Score | Description | Favors |
|-------|-------------|--------|
| 1 | Must close within days/weeks; velocity-driven model | Head-to-Head (buyers already understand the category) |
| 2 | 1-3 month sales cycle acceptable | Head-to-Head or Big Fish Small Pond |
| 3 | 3-6 month sales cycle acceptable | Big Fish Small Pond or Create a New Category |
| 4 | 6-12 month sales cycle acceptable | Create a New Category (you can afford the education investment) |
| 5 | 12+ month cycle acceptable; enterprise patience | Create a New Category |

#### Criterion 8: Urgency of the Problem You Solve

How acutely do prospects feel the pain of the problem you address?

| Score | Description | Favors |
|-------|-------------|--------|
| 1 | Low urgency — nice to have, not must-have for most buyers | Big Fish Small Pond (find the niche where it IS urgent) |
| 2 | Moderate urgency for some segments | Big Fish Small Pond |
| 3 | Moderate urgency across the market | Head-to-Head or Big Fish Small Pond |
| 4 | High urgency — prospects are actively searching for solutions | Head-to-Head |
| 5 | Hair-on-fire urgency — prospects will buy the first credible option | Head-to-Head (capitalize on existing demand) |

#### Criterion 9: Ecosystem and Partner Leverage

How much can you leverage existing ecosystems, integrations, or partner channels?

| Score | Description | Favors |
|-------|-------------|--------|
| 1 | No existing ecosystem to plug into | Create a New Category (you are building the ecosystem) |
| 2 | Nascent ecosystem with few partners | Create a New Category or Big Fish Small Pond |
| 3 | Established ecosystem you can integrate with | Big Fish Small Pond (become the specialist for that ecosystem) |
| 4 | Strong ecosystem with channel partners who can distribute for you | Head-to-Head or Big Fish Small Pond |
| 5 | Dominant ecosystem where being "the X for [platform]" is powerful | Big Fish Small Pond |

### Interpreting Your Scores

Sum your scores across all 9 criteria and use these ranges as directional guidance:

- **9-20:** Strong lean toward Big Fish Small Pond. You lack the resources, awareness, and market conditions for broad competition. Own a niche first.
- **21-30:** Big Fish Small Pond is safest; Create a New Category is possible if differentiation is high (Criterion 5 scored 4-5) and you have sales cycle tolerance (Criterion 7 scored 3+).
- **31-37:** The middle zone. Your specific pattern of scores matters more than the total. Look at which style appears most often in the "Favors" column.
- **38-45:** Head-to-Head is viable. You have the resources, awareness, and market conditions to compete directly — but verify that your differentiation (Criterion 5) scored at least 3.

**Critical override rules:**
- If Criterion 3 (Budget) scored 1-2, Head-to-Head is almost always wrong regardless of other scores.
- If Criterion 5 (Differentiation) scored 5, Create a New Category deserves serious consideration regardless of other scores.
- If Criterion 6 (Education) scored 4-5 and Criterion 7 (Sales Cycle) scored 1-2, you have a contradiction — your product needs education but your business model demands speed. Resolve this before choosing a style.

### Worked Example 1: Head-to-Head — Monday.com vs. Asana/Trello

**Context:** Monday.com entered the project management space in 2017 (originally as dapulse),
competing directly against established players Asana, Trello, and Basecamp.

**Why Head-to-Head was correct:**
- **Market maturity (4):** Project management was a well-understood, mature category.
  Buyers already searched for "project management software" and had established buying criteria.
- **Budget (4-5):** Monday.com raised $234M before going public. They could afford massive
  ad spend — they outspent competitors on paid advertising, including TV commercials.
- **Differentiation (3):** Their visual, colorful interface and flexibility were genuine
  differentiators on dimensions buyers cared about (ease of use, visual clarity), but the
  product was recognizably "project management."
- **Education required (1-2):** Buyers already understood what project management software
  does. Monday.com just had to show they did it better/differently.
- **Urgency (4):** Teams actively searching for PM tools had immediate need.

**What they did right:** They accepted the existing category framing ("project management")
and competed on the established buying criteria (ease of use, flexibility, visual appeal),
but with enough budget to earn awareness through sheer media weight. They did not try
to "create a new category" or invent jargon. They said: "We are project management.
We are better at it."

**What would have gone wrong with a different style:**
- Big Fish Small Pond would have been unnecessarily limiting — they had the resources to
  compete broadly from the start.
- Create a New Category (e.g., "Work OS") would have required educating the market on a
  concept nobody was searching for. Notably, Monday.com did eventually adopt "Work OS"
  messaging, but only AFTER they had established broad awareness through Head-to-Head
  positioning. The category creation was a phase 2 move, not a launch strategy.

### Worked Example 2: Big Fish Small Pond — ConvertKit in Email Marketing

**Context:** ConvertKit entered the email marketing space in 2013, where Mailchimp dominated
for small businesses and enterprise tools (Marketo, HubSpot, Pardot) owned the upmarket.

**Why Big Fish Small Pond was correct:**
- **Market maturity (4-5):** Email marketing was a hyper-mature, commoditized category.
  Dozens of tools competed on the same features.
- **Competitive density (5):** Mailchimp, AWeber, Constant Contact, ActiveCampaign,
  GetResponse, Drip, Campaign Monitor — the list was long.
- **Budget (1):** Nathan Barry bootstrapped ConvertKit. Annual revenue was under $100K
  for the first two years. He had a fraction of a percent of Mailchimp's resources.
- **Brand awareness (1-2):** Known only within a small circle of bloggers.
- **Differentiation (2):** The product was not fundamentally different from competitors.
  The features were similar. The difference was in who it was designed FOR.

**The subsegment they chose:** Professional bloggers and online creators.

**Why this worked:** Professional bloggers self-identified as a community. They had specific
needs (landing pages, subscriber tagging, automation for digital products) that Mailchimp
addressed poorly. ConvertKit said: "We are email marketing for professional creators."
Bloggers told other bloggers. ConvertKit grew to $1M ARR, then $10M, then $30M+,
all within the creator segment before broadening.

**What would have gone wrong with a different style:**
- Head-to-Head against Mailchimp would have been suicide. A bootstrapped solo founder
  cannot outspend a company with hundreds of millions in revenue.
- Create a New Category was unnecessary — creators understood "email marketing."
  They just wanted one that was built for them.

### Worked Example 3: Create a New Category — HubSpot and "Inbound Marketing"

**Context:** In 2006, HubSpot launched into a world where marketing software meant email
blasting (Constant Contact, ExactTarget), web analytics (Omniture), CRM (Salesforce),
and SEO tools (Moz). No single term described the integrated approach HubSpot was building.

**Why Create a New Category was correct:**
- **Market maturity (1-2):** No existing category captured "attract customers through
  content, convert them with landing pages, nurture them with email, measure everything
  in one platform." The concept existed in practice (scattered across tools) but not
  as a named, coherent approach.
- **Differentiation (5):** HubSpot's integrated approach was fundamentally different from
  buying 5 separate point solutions. Trying to position as "email marketing" or "SEO tool"
  would have undersold the product and invited comparison with specialists who were better
  at each individual function.
- **Budget (3-4):** HubSpot raised $33M before their Series D. They had enough resources to
  invest in market education, though not unlimited.
- **Education required (4):** Prospects did not think of marketing as "inbound vs. outbound."
  HubSpot had to teach this entire worldview before the product made sense.
- **Sales cycle tolerance (3-4):** HubSpot's mid-market focus allowed for a moderate sales cycle
  with inside sales reps who could educate prospects.

**What they did to make category creation work:**
1. Coined the term "inbound marketing" and trademarked it.
2. Published a book (Inbound Marketing, 2009) to legitimize the concept.
3. Created the INBOUND conference — turning the category into a movement.
4. Produced massive amounts of free educational content (the blog became one of the
   most-read marketing blogs in the world).
5. Built certification programs that trained marketers in "inbound methodology,"
   creating an army of advocates who used HubSpot's language.

**What would have gone wrong with a different style:**
- Head-to-Head in any existing category (email, CRM, analytics) would have positioned
  them against specialists who were better at each individual function.
- Big Fish Small Pond could have worked short-term (e.g., "marketing software for
  small B2B companies"), but it would have left the broader opportunity on the table
  and made them a niche tool instead of the category-defining platform they became.

### Common Mistakes by Style

#### Head-to-Head Mistakes

1. **Choosing Head-to-Head without sufficient resources.** The #1 mistake. You need at
   least 40% of the leader's marketing/sales resources to compete credibly on their terms.
   If you cannot match their spend, you will be invisible regardless of product quality.

2. **Competing on the same dimensions without being meaningfully better.** Head-to-Head
   only works when you can credibly claim superiority on the criteria buyers already use.
   Being "a little better" is not enough — the switching cost plus incumbent advantage
   means you need a visible, demonstrable gap.

3. **Ignoring the leader's ability to respond.** If you compete Head-to-Head on a feature
   the leader can easily copy, your advantage is temporary. Only compete Head-to-Head on
   attributes that are structurally difficult for the leader to replicate (architecture,
   business model, deep specialization).

4. **Choosing Head-to-Head because it feels "ambitious."** Some founders view Big Fish
   Small Pond as "thinking small." This is ego, not strategy. Many of the most successful
   companies started in a niche (Facebook at Harvard, Amazon with books, Tesla with
   high-end sports cars).

#### Big Fish Small Pond Mistakes

1. **Choosing a pond that is too small.** The subsegment must be large enough to build a
   viable business. "Email marketing for left-handed veterinarians" is not a market.
   The segment needs at least enough revenue potential to sustain your business for 2-3 years.

2. **Choosing a pond where customers do not self-identify.** If customers don't think of
   themselves as part of the segment, they won't respond to your positioning. "Professional
   bloggers" self-identify. "Mid-size companies with 47-83 employees in the Southeast"
   do not.

3. **Never expanding beyond the pond.** Big Fish Small Pond is a starting strategy, not
   a forever strategy. The plan should include a path from the initial segment to
   adjacent segments. If you stay in the pond too long, you become a niche product with
   a ceiling.

4. **Defining the pond by demographics rather than needs.** The subsegment should be
   defined by shared needs and behaviors, not just firmographic characteristics. "Retail
   companies with 50-200 employees" is a demographic. "Ecommerce brands managing 50+
   SKUs with complex return logistics" is a need-based segment.

#### Create a New Category Mistakes

1. **Creating a category when an existing one would work.** Category creation is expensive
   and slow. If you can position effectively within an existing category, do that. Only
   create a new category when existing frames genuinely fail to communicate your value.

2. **Naming the category with jargon that no one understands.** The category name must be
   self-explanatory or at least intuitive. "Revenue Operations Platform" is understandable.
   "Synergistic Value Orchestration Engine" is not. If you need a paragraph to explain the
   category name, the name is wrong.

3. **Category creation without category evangelism.** Naming a category is 10% of the work.
   The other 90% is educating the market — content, events, analyst relations, community
   building. If you are not prepared to invest heavily in education for 2-3 years, do not
   attempt category creation.

4. **Confusing "we're different" with "we need a new category."** Many products are different
   enough to win within an existing category. The test is: when you tell prospects your
   existing category, do they immediately compare you to the wrong things? If they compare
   you to roughly the right things, you do not need a new category — you need better
   differentiation messaging within the existing one.

### Hybrid Approaches: When and How to Combine Styles

In practice, positioning styles are not always mutually exclusive. The most sophisticated
positioning strategies often blend elements from multiple styles or sequence them over time.

**Hybrid 1: Big Fish Small Pond as a beachhead for Head-to-Head**
Start by dominating a niche, then broaden to compete in the full market once you have
the resources and reputation.
- **Example:** Salesforce started as "CRM for small businesses" (Big Fish Small Pond) and
  expanded to compete Head-to-Head with Siebel Systems in enterprise CRM.
- **When to use:** When you believe you can win the broader market eventually but lack
  resources to compete broadly today.
- **Sequencing:** 12-24 months in the niche to build revenue, case studies, and brand
  recognition. Then reposition for the broader market.

**Hybrid 2: Big Fish Small Pond + Category Creation**
Create a new subcategory within an existing market that is defined by your niche.
- **Example:** Drift did not just position as "chatbots for B2B websites" (Big Fish Small
  Pond). They created the term "Conversational Marketing" — a new subcategory of the
  broader marketing technology space.
- **When to use:** When your niche audience has a problem that is distinct enough to
  warrant its own name, and you want to own the language.
- **Advantage:** You get the recognition benefits of an existing market (people know what
  "marketing" is) plus the ownership benefits of category creation (you define the rules).

**Hybrid 3: Head-to-Head on feature, Category Creation on vision**
Compete on existing buying criteria to win deals today while simultaneously evangelizing
a new category that reframes the future.
- **Example:** Gainsight competed Head-to-Head as "better customer success software" to
  win deals in the short term, while also evangelizing "Customer Success" as a new business
  function — investing in conferences (Pulse), books, and community.
- **When to use:** When you can win within the existing frame but believe the market is
  shifting toward a new paradigm you want to own.
- **Risk:** You are running two strategies in parallel, which demands more resources
  and message discipline.

## Part 5: Complete Dunford 5-Step Worked Example

### The Product: Beacon Analytics

Beacon Analytics is a marketing attribution platform built specifically for direct-to-consumer
(DTC) ecommerce brands. It connects first-party customer data across channels (paid social,
email, SMS, organic, influencer, affiliate) and uses probabilistic modeling to attribute
revenue to marketing touchpoints — accounting for the iOS 14+ privacy changes, cookie
deprecation, and cross-device behavior that have broken traditional attribution tools.

The company has 12 employees, $1.8M in ARR, and 140 paying customers. Most customers
are DTC ecommerce brands doing $2M-$50M in annual revenue, primarily in fashion, beauty,
health/wellness, and home goods. The product was built by a team with a background in
data science and ecommerce operations.

The positioning exercise begins now.

---

### Step 1: Understand Your Competitive Alternatives

The question is NOT "who are our competitors?" It is: "What would our customers do
if Beacon Analytics did not exist?"

We interview 20 existing customers and 10 recently churned customers. We ask: "Before
you used Beacon, how did you handle marketing attribution? If Beacon disappeared tomorrow,
what would you do instead?"

The answers cluster into 7 categories of competitive alternatives:

**1. Google Analytics (free / GA4)**
The default. Every DTC brand has it installed. It provides last-click attribution for free.
Limitations: no cross-device tracking, no view-through attribution, terrible for paid social
measurement, requires significant technical skill to configure properly in GA4.
*Why customers chose us over this:* "GA4 told me Google ads were responsible for 80% of
my revenue. I knew that was wrong because I was spending 60% of my budget on Meta."

**2. Platform-native reporting (Meta Ads Manager, Google Ads, TikTok Ads, etc.)**
Each ad platform reports its own performance. Marketers look at each dashboard separately.
Limitations: every platform takes credit for everything, so the numbers never add up.
Attribution is self-serving. No unified view.
*Why customers chose us over this:* "Meta told me I had a 5x ROAS. Google told me I had
a 4x ROAS. When I added them up, it was more revenue than my Shopify dashboard showed."

**3. Spreadsheets and manual blending**
Export data from each platform into a spreadsheet. Manually attempt to reconcile.
Limitations: extremely time-consuming (8-15 hours/week for a skilled analyst), error-prone,
not real-time, does not handle cross-device or view-through.
*Why customers chose us over this:* "I was spending every Monday morning trying to figure
out what happened last week. By the time I had answers, it was too late to optimize."

**4. Multi-touch attribution (MTA) tools — e.g., Rockerbox, Triple Whale, Northbeam**
These are the closest direct competitors. They use various modeling approaches to attribute
revenue across touchpoints.
Limitations: vary by vendor. Some use UTM-based tracking (breaks with iOS changes), some
use pixel-based approaches (also degraded), some use media mix modeling. Quality and
accuracy vary significantly.
*Why customers chose us over this:* Answers vary — we need to identify what specific
differentiators drove the switch. (This is Step 2.)

**5. Media Mix Modeling (MMM) agencies or tools — e.g., Recast, Paramark**
Statistical modeling that uses aggregate data (spend and revenue over time) to estimate
channel impact. Does not require user-level tracking.
Limitations: requires significant historical data (12+ months), expensive ($5K-$15K/month
for tools, $15K-$50K/month for agencies), slow to produce results, not actionable at the
campaign level, overkill for brands under $20M in revenue.
*Why customers chose us over this:* "We're a $5M brand. We can't afford $10K/month for
an MMM tool that takes 3 months to calibrate and only tells us high-level channel allocation."

**6. Hiring an in-house data analyst / data scientist**
Build internal capability to do attribution analysis.
Limitations: expensive ($80K-$150K/year salary plus tools and infrastructure), hard to
recruit for this specialty, takes months to ramp up, still depends on degraded tracking data.
*Why customers chose us over this:* "We tried hiring a data person. It took 4 months to
find someone, 3 months to ramp them up, and their model was less accurate than what
Beacon gave us on day one."

**7. Doing nothing / gut feeling**
Make budget allocation decisions based on intuition, past experience, or very rough metrics.
Limitations: high error rate, no ability to optimize, impossible to justify spend to
investors or board.
*Why customers chose us over this:* "I was basically guessing. I knew Meta was probably
working but I couldn't prove it to my investors, and I had no idea if TikTok was
worth the spend."

**Summary of competitive alternatives:**
The two most common alternatives are (a) platform-native reporting plus spreadsheets
(the manual approach) and (b) direct MTA competitors like Triple Whale and Northbeam.
These are the alternatives everything else is relative to.

### Step 2: Identify Your Unique Attributes

Now we ask: what does Beacon have that the alternatives listed above do NOT?

We compare specifically against the two most common alternatives: manual/platform-native
reporting and direct MTA competitors (Triple Whale, Northbeam, Rockerbox).

**Unique Attribute 1: Hybrid attribution model combining deterministic matching, probabilistic
modeling, and incrementality testing in a single platform.**
Most MTA competitors rely on a single methodology. Triple Whale primarily uses pixel-based
tracking with Bayesian modeling. Northbeam uses multi-touch with decay models. Beacon
uniquely combines three approaches and cross-validates them against each other, flagging
discrepancies. No competitor offers built-in incrementality testing (holdout experiments)
alongside attribution modeling.

**Unique Attribute 2: Shopify-native architecture with zero-configuration setup.**
Beacon was built from day one as a Shopify app. Installation takes under 5 minutes. Data
flows automatically from Shopify, ad platforms, Klaviyo, and other DTC-standard tools via
pre-built connectors. Competitors require 2-6 weeks of implementation with technical support.

**Unique Attribute 3: Plain-language, pre-built dashboards designed for DTC operators,
not data scientists.**
Beacon's interface shows answers in the language DTC operators use: "How much incremental
revenue did my Meta prospecting campaigns drive this week?" Competitors surface raw data
tables, multi-dimensional reports, and technical metrics that require analytical expertise
to interpret.

**Unique Attribute 4: Automated weekly budget reallocation recommendations.**
Every Monday, Beacon delivers a specific recommendation: "Shift $2,000 from Google Brand
to Meta Advantage+ and $1,500 from TikTok to email campaigns." This is a concrete action
item. Competitors show performance data and leave the interpretation to the user.

**Unique Attribute 5: Calibrated accuracy scores with confidence intervals.**
Beacon displays a confidence level for every attribution number it shows (e.g., "Meta
prospecting drove $42,000 in revenue last week — 78% confidence"). This transparency
is unique. Competitors present numbers as if they are exact, which creates false precision
and erodes trust when numbers don't match Shopify.

**Unique Attribute 6: Pricing built for emerging DTC brands ($200-$800/month).**
Beacon's pricing starts at $200/month for brands under $5M revenue, scaling to $800/month
for brands up to $50M. Most MTA competitors start at $500-$1,000/month and scale to
$3,000+. MMM tools start at $5,000+. Beacon is accessible to the $2M-$10M DTC brands
who need attribution most but are priced out of existing solutions.

Each unique attribute maps to a customer value. We cluster related attributes into

|-----------------|----------------|-------|

**Theme 1: Trustworthy Answers**

**Theme 2: Built for DTC Speed**

**Theme 3: Decisions, Not Dashboards**

to interpret.

### Step 4: Identify Your Best-Fit Customers

Who cares MOST about these three value themes? Who is the customer for whom "trustworthy
answers," "built for DTC speed," and "decisions not dashboards" are not just nice-to-have
but must-have?

**Profile of the best-fit customer:**

- **Company type:** DTC ecommerce brand selling physical products through Shopify.
- **Revenue range:** $2M-$20M annually. Below $2M, they don't spend enough on paid marketing

- **Vertical sweet spots:** Fashion, beauty, health/wellness, home goods — categories where
  Meta and TikTok are primary acquisition channels and iOS 14 hit hardest.
- **Marketing team size:** 1-4 people. The marketing lead (often the founder or a head of

- **Current pain:** They are spending $30K-$300K/month on paid acquisition across 3-5 channels.
  They know their platform-reported numbers don't add up. They are making budget decisions

- Brands under $2M revenue (not enough marketing spend for attribution to matter)

### Step 5: Choose a Market Category

We have three options. Let us evaluate each.

**Option A: Head-to-Head — "Marketing Attribution Platform"**

## Part 6: Positioning Validation Checklist

Use this 15-item checklist to test whether your positioning is strong. Each item is a
specific, testable criterion with pass/fail guidance.

### Severity Levels

**Items 1-5: Foundation.** If you fail any of these, your positioning is broken at the
foundation level. Stop all marketing and sales execution. Go back to the positioning
process and start over. Executing against broken positioning wastes every dollar spent.

**Items 6-10: Strength.** If you fail any of these, your positioning is weak but salvageable.
You can continue operating, but you should schedule a positioning sprint within the next
30 days. Revenue growth will be harder than it needs to be until these are fixed.

**Items 11-15: Excellence.** If you fail any of these, your positioning is good but not great.
These items separate competent positioning from exceptional positioning. Address them as
part of your regular strategy reviews.

---

### Foundation Tests (Items 1-5)

**1. Clarity Test: Can 80%+ of target customers explain your position back to you in their own words?**

How to test: Interview 10 target-fit prospects after a 2-minute pitch (not existing customers
who have had months of exposure). Ask: "In your own words, what does [product] do and
who is it for?"

Pass: 8 out of 10 can articulate the core position without using jargon you taught them.
They may use different words than your positioning statement, but the meaning is the same.

Fail: Fewer than 8 out of 10 get it right, or they describe you in vague terms
("it's some kind of analytics tool"), or they confuse you with a competitor's positioning.

**2. Differentiation Test: Can your target customer name at least one thing you do that
no competitor does — without being prompted?**

How to test: After the clarity test, ask the same prospects: "What makes [product] different
from [most obvious competitor]?" Do NOT lead them.

Pass: At least 6 out of 10 can name a genuine differentiator that aligns with your
unique attributes (Step 2 of Dunford). They don't need to use your exact language.

Fail: They say "I'm not sure" or name attributes that are not actually unique to you
(e.g., "good customer support" or "nice interface").

**3. Category Test: When you name your market category, do prospects compare you to
the right competitors?**

How to test: Tell a prospect your category (e.g., "marketing attribution platform for DTC").
Ask: "What other products come to mind in that space?"

Pass: They name the actual competitive alternatives from your Step 1 analysis.

Fail: They name products that are not your actual competitors, or they say "I have no
idea what that category is." Either means your category framing is wrong.

**4. Value Test: Does your stated value match what customers actually experience?**

How to test: Interview 10 existing customers. Ask: "What is the #1 benefit you get from
using [product]?" Compare their answers to your value themes from Step 3.

Pass: At least 7 out of 10 name a benefit that maps to one of your stated value themes.

Fail: They name benefits you did not include in your positioning, or they are unable to
articulate a clear benefit. If they name different benefits than you claim, your
positioning is disconnected from reality — fix it by updating positioning to match
actual delivered value.

**5. Relevance Test: Does your positioning address a problem the target customer is
actively trying to solve?**

How to test: Ask 10 target-fit prospects (before pitching): "What are the top 3 challenges
in [relevant domain] right now?" See if the problem your positioning addresses appears
unprompted.

Pass: At least 5 out of 10 mention the problem your positioning addresses (in their own
words) as a top-3 challenge.

Fail: The problem you solve is not on their radar. This means either your target customer
definition is wrong (Step 4) or the problem is not urgent enough to anchor positioning on.

---

### Strength Tests (Items 6-10)

**6. Recall Test: After 48 hours, can prospects recall your positioning from a single
exposure?**

How to test: Pitch 10 prospects. Call them 48 hours later. Ask: "What do you remember
about [product]?"

Pass: At least 5 out of 10 recall the core position (category + key differentiator)
without prompting.

Fail: They remember your name but not what you do, or they confuse you with a competitor.

**7. Specificity Test: Does your positioning exclude at least 50% of the broader market?**

How to test: Look at your positioning statement. Does it clearly define who you are
NOT for? Is the target segment narrow enough that a large portion of the broader market
would say "that's not for me"?

Pass: You can name specific types of companies or buyers who should NOT buy your product,
and your positioning makes this obvious without requiring explanation.

Fail: Your positioning could apply to almost anyone in your general space. "Project
management for modern teams" excludes almost no one.

**8. Consistency Test: Is your positioning communicated identically across website,
sales deck, sales conversations, onboarding, and customer success?**

How to test: Audit your homepage, sales deck (first 3 slides), last 5 recorded sales
calls (first 2 minutes), onboarding emails, and customer success kickoff script. Do
they all communicate the same position?

Pass: All channels communicate the same category, same key differentiators, and same
target customer — even if the specific words vary.

Fail: Your website says one thing, your sales team says another, and your onboarding
says a third. This is more common than you think, and it means your positioning
exists as a document but not as operational reality.

**9. Sales Velocity Test: Does your positioning shorten sales cycles?**

How to test: Compare average sales cycle length from the quarter before the positioning
change to the quarter after (or compare deals where the new positioning was used
consistently vs. deals where it was not).

Pass: Sales cycle shortened by at least 15%, or the first sales call has materially
less "education" and more "evaluation."

Fail: Sales cycles are unchanged or getting longer. This suggests the positioning is not
doing its job of pre-qualifying and pre-educating prospects.

**10. Competitive Win Rate Test: Do you win at least 30% of competitive deals against
your most common alternative?**

How to test: Track win/loss data specifically for deals where you were evaluated against
your primary competitive alternative (from Step 1).

Pass: Win rate of 30%+ against your most common competitor. (Higher is better, but 30%
indicates your positioning is creating a viable competitive narrative.)

Fail: Win rate below 30% in competitive deals. Your positioning is not giving buyers a
compelling reason to choose you.

---

### Excellence Tests (Items 11-15)

**11. Word Ownership Test: Is there a single word or short phrase that prospects
associate with your brand?**

How to test: Ask 20 customers and prospects: "If you had to describe [product] in one
word, what would it be?" See if a consensus word emerges.

Pass: At least 8 out of 20 converge on the same word or a close synonym.

Fail: Answers are scattered with no consensus. You are not owning a word in the mind
(Law 5 violation).

**12. Referral Language Test: When customers refer you, do they use your positioning
language?**

How to test: Ask new inbound leads: "How did you hear about us, and what did the person
who referred you say?" Compare to your positioning.

Pass: Referrers describe you using language that reflects your positioning (category +
key differentiator + target customer).

Fail: Referrers use vague or incorrect descriptions ("it's some kind of data tool").
This means your positioning has not penetrated deeply enough to become the natural
way people talk about you.

**13. Pricing Power Test: Can you charge at least 20% more than the closest comparable
competitor without losing deals on price?**

How to test: Analyze deal data. In competitive deals, is price the primary objection?
What percentage of deals are lost specifically on price?

Pass: Price is mentioned as an objection in fewer than 20% of lost deals, and you are
priced at or above the market average for your segment.

Fail: Price is a top-3 objection in most lost deals. This means your positioning is not
creating enough perceived value to justify your price — you are being treated as a
commodity within the category.

**14. Trend Alignment Test: Does your positioning feel more relevant today than it did
12 months ago?**

How to test: Review industry conversations, conference topics, customer language, and
media coverage. Is the problem your positioning addresses becoming more or less
prominent in market discourse?

Pass: The trends are moving in your favor. The problem you solve is getting more
attention, and your positioning language is being adopted by the broader market.

Fail: The trends are moving away from you, or the market conversation has shifted to
a different framing that makes your positioning feel dated.

**15. Expansion Test: Does your positioning create a natural path to adjacent segments
without requiring a full repositioning?**

How to test: Evaluate whether your current position could stretch to serve 2-3 adjacent
customer segments or use cases with minor messaging adjustments (not a positioning
overhaul).

Pass: You can identify 2-3 adjacent segments where your core position still resonates
with slight modifications to the "target customer" component.

Fail: Your positioning is so narrow or so tied to current market conditions that any
expansion would require starting the positioning process from scratch.

---

### Positioning Decay Indicators

Even strong positioning degrades over time. Watch for these signs that your positioning
needs refreshing:

**1. The "Yeah, so does everyone" response.**
When you state your differentiator and prospects respond with "yeah, [competitor] does
that too," your uniqueness has been eroded. Competitors have caught up, or the market
has shifted to make your differentiator table stakes. Timeline: usually 18-36 months
after initial positioning.

**2. Rising customer acquisition costs with flat or declining conversion rates.**
This often indicates that your positioning is losing resonance — you are spending more
to get the same message in front of people, but the message itself is less compelling.

**3. Increasing "wrong-fit" customers.**
If you are attracting and closing customers who churn quickly or who require heavy
support, your positioning may be attracting the wrong audience. The positioning has
drifted from your actual best-fit customer.

**4. New competitor with better framing.**
A new entrant has repositioned the category in a way that makes your positioning feel
outdated. Their language is being adopted by analysts, media, and customers. You are
being compared using their frame, not yours.

**5. Sales team inventing their own positioning.**
When your sales team stops using the official positioning and creates their own talk
tracks, it is a strong signal that the positioning is not working in live conversations.
The sales team is on the front line — if they are abandoning the positioning, listen to
them.

**6. Category convergence.**
Multiple competitors in your space start sounding identical. Every website hero section
uses the same words. Buyers cannot distinguish between options. The category has
commoditized and your positioning needs to find a new dimension of differentiation.

**7. Your best customers describe you differently than your positioning states.**
Interview your top 10 customers. If they describe why they love you using language that
does not match your positioning statement, your positioning is disconnected from your
actual value. Update the positioning to match reality, not the other way around.

**Recommended cadence:** Formally reassess positioning every 12 months, or immediately
when 2+ decay indicators appear.

## Part 7: 22 Laws Application Guide

### Laws Grouped by Situation

The 22 Laws are most useful when you know which situation you are in. Below, the laws
are grouped by the strategic situation they most directly address, with scenarios showing
how they apply together.

---

#### Situation A: Entering a New Market
**Relevant Laws: 1 (Leadership), 2 (Category), 3 (Mind), 8 (Duality), 22 (Resources)**

**Scenario:** You are launching a product into a market where you have no existing presence.

How these laws work together:
- **Law 1 (Leadership):** Can you be first in this category? If yes, move fast — being first
  in the mind creates a structural advantage that is nearly impossible to overcome later.
- **Law 2 (Category):** If you cannot be first, do not enter the existing category as a
  follower. Instead, define a new category (or subcategory) where you can be first. This
  is the most important strategic decision you will make.
- **Law 3 (Mind):** Being first in the market is necessary but not sufficient. You must be
  first in the MIND. That means spending on awareness, not just on product. A launched
  product that nobody knows about is not "first" in any meaningful sense.
- **Law 8 (Duality):** Look forward 5-10 years. This market will become a two-horse race.
  Are you positioned to be one of the two? If you are entering as the 5th player in a
  maturing market, you are likely too late — unless you can redefine the category (Law 2).
- **Law 22 (Resources):** You need enough funding to establish your position in the mind.
  Do not enter a new market with a shoestring budget and expect positioning alone to save
  you. "A mediocre idea with a million dollars will beat a great idea with no money."

**Application example:** A startup entering the "AI code review" space. If no clear leader
exists yet, the priority is to be first in the mind (Law 3) by investing heavily in
awareness (Law 22). If tools like GitHub Copilot already dominate the "AI coding" mental
space, create a new category — perhaps "AI code security review" (Law 2) — where you
can be first.

---

#### Situation B: Defending an Established Position
**Relevant Laws: 4 (Perception), 5 (Focus), 12 (Line Extension), 13 (Sacrifice)**

**Scenario:** You are the market leader or a strong #2. You want to protect and strengthen
your position against challengers.

How these laws work together:
- **Law 5 (Focus):** Your greatest strength is the word you own. Reinforce it relentlessly.
  Every piece of marketing, every product decision, every sales conversation should
  reinforce your word. The moment you dilute your word, you open the door to challengers.
- **Law 12 (Line Extension):** The most common way leaders destroy themselves is by extending
  the brand into adjacent categories. It works in the short term and kills you in the
  long term. Use new brands for new categories (like P&G does).
- **Law 13 (Sacrifice):** To keep your position, you must sacrifice opportunities that
  would dilute it. Say no to new market segments, new product lines, and new messaging
  strategies that do not reinforce your core position.
- **Law 4 (Perception):** Your competitors cannot take your position by building a better
  product. They can only take it by changing the perception. Watch for competitors who
  are trying to reframe the category — that is the real threat, not feature competition.

**Application example:** Volvo owns "safety." The greatest threat is not a competitor building
a safer car — it is Volvo itself diluting the position by launching a sports car line
(line extension) or messaging "performance and safety and luxury and value" (losing focus).
The correct defense: double down on safety in every new model, every ad, every innovation.

---

#### Situation C: Attacking a Market Leader
**Relevant Laws: 9 (Opposite), 14 (Attributes), 15 (Candor), 7 (Ladder)**

**Scenario:** You are #2 or #3 and want to take share from the established leader.

How these laws work together:
- **Law 9 (Opposite):** Study the leader's positioning. Your strategy must be the opposite,
  not a better version of the same thing. If the leader is "established and trusted,"
  you are "new and innovative." If the leader is "comprehensive," you are "simple and
  focused." The leader's strength is your playbook — invert it.
- **Law 14 (Attributes):** For every attribute the leader owns, there is an opposite attribute
  that is equally effective. If they own "fast," you own "thorough." If they own
  "enterprise-grade," you own "built for startups." Do not try to out-attribute them on
  their own dimension.
- **Law 15 (Candor):** If you are the underdog, admit it. "We're smaller" or "we're newer"
  disarms the prospect and opens their mind to your positive message. Avis's "We're #2,
  we try harder" is the canonical example.
- **Law 7 (Ladder):** Acknowledge your rung. Your strategy must match your position. A #2
  brand using a #1 strategy (ignoring the leader, acting as if they don't exist) will fail.
  A #2 brand must explicitly address the leader and differentiate against them.

**Application example:** A project management tool attacking Asana. Asana's strength is
its comprehensive, all-in-one approach for large teams. The attacker should position as
the opposite: "Simple project management for small teams who don't need enterprise
complexity." Admit the limitation (Law 15): "We don't do everything Asana does."
Then turn it into a strength: "Because we don't, we're 10x faster to set up and you'll
actually use us."

---

#### Situation D: Navigating a Fragmenting Market
**Relevant Laws: 10 (Division), 2 (Category), 5 (Focus), 8 (Duality)**

**Scenario:** Your market category is splitting into subcategories. New specialized players
are emerging. The single-category leader is losing relevance.

How these laws work together:
- **Law 10 (Division):** This is inevitable. Every category eventually divides. The question
  is not whether it will happen but when, and whether you will own a subcategory when it does.
- **Law 2 (Category):** Each new subcategory needs its own leader. If your market is
  fragmenting, identify the subcategory that is forming around your strengths and claim
  leadership of it.
- **Law 5 (Focus):** As the category divides, the temptation is to serve all subcategories
  (to "be everything to everyone"). Resist this. Pick one subcategory and own it.
- **Law 8 (Duality):** Within each subcategory, the same duality dynamic will emerge.
  Position to be one of the two eventual winners.

**Application example:** The "marketing automation" category has fragmented into email
marketing, SMS marketing, marketing orchestration, CDP, customer engagement, and more.
A player like Klaviyo recognized that "email and SMS for ecommerce" was a subcategory
forming around their strengths, and they claimed it rather than trying to be
"marketing automation for everyone."

---

#### Situation E: Managing Resource Constraints
**Relevant Laws: 22 (Resources), 16 (Singularity), 13 (Sacrifice), 5 (Focus)**

**Scenario:** You have limited budget and team, and must make every dollar count.

How these laws work together:
- **Law 22 (Resources):** Accept that positioning requires investment to establish in the
  mind. A great position that no one hears about is worthless. Allocate budget to
  awareness, not just product.
- **Law 16 (Singularity):** When resources are constrained, find the one move that will
  produce the most disproportionate result. Do not spread budget across 5 channels
  and 3 messages. Find the single biggest point of leverage and pour everything into it.
- **Law 13 (Sacrifice):** You cannot afford to address multiple segments or maintain
  multiple product lines. Sacrifice everything that is not your core strength and
  your core audience.
- **Law 5 (Focus):** With limited resources, the narrower your focus, the stronger your
  position. Own one word. Serve one segment. Solve one problem. Do it better than anyone.

**Application example:** A bootstrapped SaaS with $50K/year marketing budget. The singular
move (Law 16): become the undisputed expert in one narrowly defined subsegment by producing
the definitive content library on that specific problem. Sacrifice all other marketing
activities (Law 13). Own one word (Law 5): "the [specific term] platform." Invest the
entire $50K in making that one position stick in the minds of that one audience (Law 22).

---

#### Situation F: Recovering from a Positioning Mistake
**Relevant Laws: 19 (Failure), 11 (Perspective), 4 (Perception), 3 (Mind)**

**Scenario:** Your current positioning is not working. Sales cycles are long, messaging is
confused, and growth is stalled. You need to reposition.

How these laws work together:
- **Law 19 (Failure):** Admit the positioning is not working. Cut losses early. The biggest
  mistake is continuing to invest in a broken position because of sunk costs.
- **Law 11 (Perspective):** Remember that marketing effects play out over time. A positioning
  change will not produce immediate results. Plan for a 6-12 month transition, not a
  2-week fix.
- **Law 4 (Perception):** You are not changing reality — you are changing perception. The
  product is the same. The change is in how the market perceives you. This means you
  must invest in retraining the market's mental model of your brand.
- **Law 3 (Mind):** It is extremely difficult to change a position once it is established
  in the mind. If you are "the cheap option," becoming "the premium option" is nearly
  impossible under the same brand. Consider whether a new brand or product name is needed.

---

### Law Conflict Resolution

The 22 Laws sometimes appear to contradict each other. Here is how to resolve the most
common conflicts:

**Conflict: Law 1 (Leadership — be first) vs. Law 22 (Resources — need money to establish)**

*Tension:* Being first requires speed, but establishing the position requires resources
you may not have yet.

*Resolution:* Being first in a SMALL category requires fewer resources than being first in
a large one. Use Law 2 (Category) to define a category small enough that your available
resources are sufficient to establish leadership. You can always expand the category later.

**Conflict: Law 5 (Focus — own one word) vs. Law 10 (Division — categories split)**

*Tension:* You own one word in one category, but the category is dividing. Your word may
end up in a subcategory that is not the one you want.

*Resolution:* When you see division coming, proactively choose which subcategory to own.
Redefine your focus word for the subcategory that aligns with your strengths. It is
better to voluntarily narrow your focus than to be pushed into irrelevance by a
fragmenting market.

**Conflict: Law 9 (Opposite — be different from the leader) vs. Law 5 (Focus — own a word)**

*Tension:* Being the opposite of the leader might lead you to a position that is not
focused enough, or to a word that is defined negatively ("un-" or "not-").

*Resolution:* The opposite must be a positive position, not merely a negation. Pepsi is
not "un-Coca-Cola." Pepsi is "the choice of a new generation." The opposite of "traditional"
is not "not traditional" — it is "modern" or "fresh" or "next-generation." Find the
affirmative word that represents the other side of the leader's position.

**Conflict: Law 12 (Line Extension — avoid it) vs. Law 10 (Division — categories divide)**

*Tension:* As your category divides, you need products for new subcategories, but Law 12
says extending your brand into new categories destroys your position.

*Resolution:* Use new brands for new subcategories. This is the P&G model. Tide for
heavy-duty laundry. Cheer for cold-water laundry. Dreft for baby clothes. Same company,
different brands, different positions. If you extend one brand across subcategories, the
specialist in each subcategory will beat you.

**Conflict: Law 15 (Candor — admit a weakness) vs. Law 5 (Focus — own a word)**

*Tension:* Admitting a weakness might undermine your focused position.

*Resolution:* The weakness you admit must be RELATED to your strength. Avis admitting
"we're #2" sets up "we try harder." The candor opens the mind to the positive message.
Never admit a weakness that undermines your core focus word. If you own "reliability,"
don't admit to being unreliable. Admit to something adjacent: "We're not the flashiest
— because we focus on reliability."

**Conflict: Law 17 (Unpredictability — can't predict the future) vs. Law 21 (Acceleration — build on trends)**

*Tension:* You can't predict the future, but you should build on trends. How do you
distinguish a real trend from a fad if the future is unpredictable?

*Resolution:* Law 17 says you can't predict specific outcomes. Law 21 says you can identify
large-scale trends by their characteristics: trends build slowly, are driven by structural
forces, and sustain over years. Fads rise fast, are driven by novelty, and collapse quickly.
The test: has this movement been growing steadily for 3+ years? Is it driven by structural
forces (technology shifts, demographic changes, regulatory changes) or by hype? Build on
trends. Experiment cautiously with potential trends. Never bet the company on a fad.

### The 5 Most Commonly Violated Laws

#### 1. Law 12: Line Extension

**Violation frequency:** Nearly universal. This is the most violated law by a wide margin.

**Example:** Amazon's Fire Phone (2014). Amazon extended its brand from ecommerce and
cloud services into smartphones. Despite massive resources, the product failed spectacularly
— Amazon took a $170M write-down and discontinued the product within a year.

**Why it keeps happening:** Line extension produces short-term revenue (existing brand
awareness drives trial), which makes the spreadsheet look good. The long-term brand
dilution is invisible in quarterly reports. Executives reward the visible short-term
gain and cannot see the invisible long-term cost.

**Consequence of violation:** Each extension weakens the brand's association with its
core word. Google extended into social (Google+), hardware (Google Glass), messaging
(Google Allo, Duo, Hangouts, Chat, Meet), and dozens of other categories. The brand
retains strength in "search" but has failed in nearly every extension where a focused
specialist existed.

#### 2. Law 5: Focus

**Violation frequency:** Extremely common, especially during growth phases.

**Example:** Yahoo in the 2000s. Yahoo started as a search engine but expanded to become
a portal offering email, news, finance, sports, shopping, games, photos, and more. They
stood for everything and therefore stood for nothing. When Google arrived with a singular
focus on "search," Yahoo lost the category it originally created.

**Why it keeps happening:** Growth pressure. When a company's core market matures,
leadership looks for new revenue. The easiest path (which is the wrong path) is to add
more products and serve more segments. Every addition is individually rational but
collectively destructive.

**Consequence of violation:** The brand loses its mental anchor. Prospects cannot complete
the sentence "[Brand] is the best at ___." When asked, they say "they do a lot of things"
— which is the kiss of death in positioning.

#### 3. Law 13: Sacrifice

**Violation frequency:** Common, especially in companies with strong sales teams.

**Example:** Every enterprise software company that starts with a focused product for a
specific ICP, then adds features for every prospect who says "we'd buy if you also did X."
The product bloats. The messaging becomes generic. The originally-focused positioning
dissolves into "we do everything."

**Why it keeps happening:** Sacrifice means saying no to revenue. It means watching a
prospect walk out the door because you won't add their feature request. It requires
discipline that most organizations lack, especially when sales compensation is tied to
individual deal closure rather than long-term brand health.

**Consequence of violation:** Trying to serve everyone means optimizing for no one. The
product becomes a mediocre generalist. The focused competitors in each subsegment win
their respective niches while the un-sacrificed brand struggles in all of them.

#### 4. Law 6: Exclusivity

**Violation frequency:** Common in competitive markets where companies fail to do
differentiation homework.

**Example:** Burger King has repeatedly tried to own "fast" — the word McDonald's already
owns. Their campaigns emphasizing speed have consistently failed to change the mental
association. More recently, multiple cloud computing providers have tried to own "AI-first"
simultaneously, with none succeeding because they are fighting over the same word.

**Why it keeps happening:** Companies look at the leader's position and think "we can
do that better." They see the leader's success and attribute it to the word they own,
then try to take the same word by being superior on that dimension. This ignores the
structural reality that the mind has already assigned that word to someone else.

**Consequence of violation:** Marketing spend is wasted on a position that cannot be taken.
The money would have been better spent finding an unoccupied word (Law 14 — for every
attribute, there is an opposite effective attribute). The company burns budget, fails to
move perception, and ends up with neither the contested word nor an alternative.

#### 5. Law 9: Opposite

**Violation frequency:** Common among #2 and #3 brands that try to be "a better version
of the leader" instead of being different.

**Example:** Bing vs. Google. Microsoft has spent billions positioning Bing as "better search"
— better results, better image search, better rewards for searching. This is a direct
violation of Law 9. The correct strategy for #2 against Google would be to position as the
OPPOSITE of Google's strength. Google's strength is "knows everything about you" (which
enables personalization). The opposite: "private search" — which is exactly what DuckDuckGo
did successfully with a fraction of Microsoft's resources.

**Why it keeps happening:** Being "better" feels intuitive and safe. Being "different" (the
opposite) feels risky because you are explicitly not competing on the dimension the market
values most. But "better" does not work against an entrenched leader because the prospect's
mind has already assigned that attribute to the leader (Law 6).

**Consequence of violation:** The challenger spends enormous resources trying to convince
prospects that the leader's primary attribute actually belongs to the challenger. This
almost never works. The prospects' pre-existing mental association is too strong. Meanwhile,
the opposite position sits vacant, waiting for someone to claim it.
