## Part 1: The 7 Powers (Helmer)

Power = the potential for persistent differential returns.
A business has Power when it possesses a Benefit (creates value) AND a Barrier
(prevents competitors from copying or arbitraging the benefit away).

Without Power, competition drives profits to zero — no matter how well you execute.
"Compelling value" is necessary but not sufficient. You must also have a barrier.

### Power #1: Scale Economies

**Definition:** A business where unit costs decline as production volume increases,
and volume is large enough that smaller competitors face a structural cost disadvantage.

**Benefit:** Lower per-unit costs at scale → higher margins or ability to price aggressively.

**Barrier:** A challenger must achieve comparable scale to match costs, requiring enormous
capital investment with uncertain returns. The incumbent's installed base acts as a fortress.

**When it applies:** Industries with high fixed costs and low marginal costs.
Software, semiconductors, manufacturing, logistics.

**Example:** Intel's fabrication plants cost billions. Each new chip costs almost nothing
to produce once the fab exists. A new entrant would need to spend billions on fabs
before selling a single chip — and compete against Intel's amortized cost base.

**Subtypes:**
- Volume/area relationships (physical: pipelines, warehouses)
- Distribution network economies (FedEx, UPS)
- Learning economies (yield improvement in manufacturing)
- Purchasing economies (Walmart's buying power)

**Key insight:** Scale economies have diminishing returns. The advantage is greatest when
you are large and competitors are small. Once competitors also reach scale, the advantage
narrows. The "surplus leader margin" (SLM) depends on the ratio of your scale to theirs.

### Power #2: Network Economies

**Definition:** The value of a product or service increases as more users adopt it.
Each new user makes the network more valuable for all existing users.

**Benefit:** Self-reinforcing growth — each new user attracts more users, creating
a flywheel. Higher value → more users → higher value.

**Barrier:** Winner-take-all or winner-take-most dynamics. Once a network achieves
critical mass, switching to a smaller network means losing the value of the connections.
New entrants face the "cold start problem" — the network is useless until it has users.

**When it applies:** Platforms, marketplaces, social networks, communication tools,
payment systems, operating systems.

**Types:**
- Direct network effects (each user benefits from other users: phone, fax, social media)
- Indirect/two-sided network effects (more buyers attract more sellers and vice versa:
  eBay, Uber, app stores)
- Data network effects (more usage → more data → better product: Google, Waze)
- Protocol/standard network effects (TCP/IP, USB, QWERTY)

**Example:** Facebook/Meta. Once your friends are on Facebook, any competing social
network that doesn't have your friends is worthless to you. This is why Google+ failed
despite Google's massive resources.

**Key insight:** Network effects are NOT the same as virality. Virality is about growth
speed. Network effects are about the defensibility of the value once achieved.

### Power #3: Counter-Positioning

**Definition:** A newcomer adopts a superior business model that the incumbent cannot
copy because doing so would damage their existing business.

**Benefit:** The new entrant's model is genuinely better — lower cost, better experience,
or superior economics.

**Barrier:** The incumbent faces "collateral damage" — adopting the new model would
cannibalize their existing revenue, alienate current customers, or destroy their margin
structure. It's not that they CAN'T copy you; it's that copying you hurts them more than
it helps.

**When it applies:** When a new approach genuinely threatens an incumbent's cash cow.
The incumbent recognizes the threat but rationally chooses not to respond (at first).

**Example:** Netflix vs. Blockbuster. Netflix's streaming model was clearly superior, but
Blockbuster couldn't adopt it without destroying its retail store network, late fee revenue,
and franchise relationships. Blockbuster's rational analysis said "don't cannibalize our
$5 billion business to compete in a $50 million business" — until it was too late.

**Example:** Vanguard vs. actively managed funds. Index funds had lower fees and (often)
better performance. Active fund managers couldn't switch without destroying their fee
structure. Fidelity launching an index fund meant admitting their $100+ million fee
structure was a bad deal for customers.

**Key insight:** Counter-positioning is the most common power source for startups.
The incumbent's response is NOT stupidity — it's rational short-term thinking.
They are optimizing for their existing business. That's their trap.

**Helmer's collateral damage formula:** The incumbent will NOT respond until the
expected gain from entering the new market exceeds the expected loss in their old market.
By the time this tips, the new entrant often has an insurmountable lead.

### Power #4: Switching Costs

**Definition:** The costs (financial, procedural, relational) a customer incurs when
switching from your product to a competitor's.

**Benefit:** Customer retention is high even without continuous outperformance.
Predictable revenue streams. Pricing power on renewals and expansions.

**Barrier:** Competitors must not only match your offering — they must provide enough
additional value to overcome the switching cost. The higher the switching cost, the
larger the "value gap" a competitor must bridge.

**Types of switching costs:**
- Financial: Contractual penalties, migration costs, retraining expenses
- Procedural: Learning curve, workflow disruption, data migration, integration effort
- Relational: Personal relationships, institutional knowledge, trust built over time
- Data lock-in: Historical data trapped in a platform (CRM records, analytics history)

**Example:** Enterprise software (SAP, Oracle, Salesforce). Once a company implements
SAP across their operations, switching to a competitor means years of migration work,
millions in consulting fees, retraining thousands of employees, and risking operational
disruption. Even when competitors offer better products, the switching cost keeps
customers locked in.

**Key insight:** Switching costs accumulate over time. A new product has zero switching
costs — the power builds as usage deepens and data accumulates. This is why SaaS
companies focus on "land and expand" — get in the door, then make switching
increasingly expensive through deeper integration.

### Power #5: Branding

**Definition:** A durable attribution of higher value to an objectively identical offering
arising from historical seller behavior.

**Benefit:** Charge a price premium for the same (or similar) product. Reduce customer
acquisition costs through trust and recognition. Lower customer uncertainty.

**Barrier:** Branding takes years and significant investment to build, with highly uncertain
outcomes. A competitor cannot "buy" a brand quickly — it must be earned through
consistent behavior over long periods.

**Two dimensions of brand value:**
1. **Affective valence** — The emotional association. How does the brand make you FEEL?
   (Apple = creativity/sophistication, Nike = achievement, Volvo = safety)
2. **Uncertainty reduction** — The trust that the product will perform as expected.
   (Tylenol = safe pain relief, Toyota = reliable car)

**Example:** Tiffany's. The same diamond costs 2-3x more in a Tiffany's box. The blue box
IS the product — it signals taste, quality, and romantic effort. A competitor selling
identical diamonds at half the price cannot compete because the customer isn't buying
the diamond; they're buying what Tiffany's means.

**Key insight:** Branding is the ONLY power that applies to commodities. When the
product can't be differentiated on features, the brand itself becomes the differentiator.
But branding is also the hardest power to build intentionally — it requires years of
consistent behavior and massive investment with no guarantee of success.

### Power #6: Cornered Resource

**Definition:** Exclusive access to a valuable resource — talent, IP, regulatory license,
location, data set, or natural resource — that competitors cannot obtain.

**Benefit:** Possession of the resource enables superior products, lower costs, or
unique capabilities that competitors cannot match.

**Barrier:** The resource is genuinely scarce and non-replicable. Others cannot obtain it
at any price, or the cost of obtaining it is prohibitive.

**Types:**
- Talent: Key individuals (Steve Jobs, specific inventors, elite teams)
- Intellectual property: Patents, proprietary technology, trade secrets
- Regulatory: Licenses, spectrum rights, certificates of need
- Geographic: Prime real estate, mineral deposits, port access
- Data: Proprietary datasets that improve with scale (Google Search data)

**Example:** Pixar's "brain trust" — the specific combination of creative talent that
consistently produced hit films. Disney's acquisition of Pixar was largely a purchase
of this cornered resource (the team and creative culture), not the technology.

**Key insight:** Cornered resources are fragile. Key talent can leave. Patents expire.
Regulations change. This power requires active maintenance and renewal.

### Power #7: Process Power

**Definition:** A business whose organization and culture have evolved complex processes
that enable superior outcomes, and these processes are so deeply embedded that they
cannot be copied even when fully understood.

**Benefit:** Superior operational outcomes — better quality, lower cost, faster delivery,
fewer errors — that persist over time.

**Barrier:** The processes are embedded in organizational culture, tacit knowledge,
and thousands of small, interconnected practices. Even if a competitor understands
WHAT you do, they cannot replicate HOW you do it because it requires years of
institutional learning.

**Example:** Toyota Production System. Toyota openly shared their manufacturing
principles (lean, kaizen, just-in-time). Competitors visited their plants, read the books,
hired Toyota consultants. Yet decades later, Toyota's quality and efficiency advantages
persisted. The "process" wasn't a checklist — it was a culture of continuous improvement
embedded in every employee's behavior, honed over 50+ years.

**Key insight:** Process power is the slowest to build but potentially the most durable.
It cannot be bought, copied, or reverse-engineered. It must be grown organically over
long periods. This is why it's rare — most companies don't have the patience or
stability to develop it.

### Strategy Dynamics: When Each Power Is Available

Helmer's critical insight: different powers are available at different stages.

**Origination (startup/launch):**
- Counter-Positioning — available immediately (your new model vs. incumbents)
- Cornered Resource — available if you can secure it early (key hire, IP, license)
- Network Economies — available from the start if you're building a network

**Takeoff (rapid growth):**
- Scale Economies — begin to matter as volume increases
- Network Economies — accelerate if you've achieved critical mass
- Switching Costs — begin to accumulate as customers integrate your product

**Stability (mature business):**
- Branding — fully developed after years of consistent behavior
- Process Power — only available after decades of organizational learning
- All other powers — maintained and defended

**The Strategy Equation:**
Value = Market Size × Power

A huge market with no power = a commodity business with thin margins.
A powerful position in a tiny market = a profitable but limited business.
You need BOTH.

## Part 2: Good Strategy / Bad Strategy (Rumelt)

### The Kernel of Good Strategy

Every good strategy has exactly three elements:

**1. Diagnosis** — A clear-eyed assessment of the situation, defining the nature of the
challenge. The diagnosis simplifies the overwhelming complexity of reality by identifying
the critical aspects of the situation.

"A good diagnosis simplifies the often overwhelming complexity of reality by identifying
certain aspects of the situation as critical."

Diagnosis is NOT a list of problems. It's a judgment about what's MOST important.
It's a theory about what's happening and why.

**2. Guiding Policy** — The overall approach chosen to cope with or overcome the
obstacles identified in the diagnosis. A guiding policy channels action in certain
directions without defining exactly what shall be done.

A guiding policy creates advantage by anticipating the actions and reactions of others,
reducing the complexity of what needs to be done, and exploiting leverage — applying
effort where it will have the most effect.

"Good strategy works by focusing energy and resources on one, or a very few, pivotal
objectives whose accomplishment will lead to a cascade of favorable outcomes."

**3. Coherent Actions** — A set of coordinated, feasible steps designed to carry out
the guiding policy. These actions must be COHERENT — they must work together,
not pull in different directions.

"Strategic coordination is not ad-hoc mutual adjustment. It is coherence imposed on a
system by policy and design."

### The Four Hallmarks of Bad Strategy

**1. Fluff** — Superficial restatement of the obvious combined with buzzwords to
create the illusion of high-level thinking.

"A hallmark of true expertise and insight is making a complex subject understandable.
A hallmark of mediocrity and bad strategy is unnecessary complexity."

**2. Failure to Face the Challenge** — Bad strategy fails to identify or address the
actual challenge. If you can't define the challenge, you can't evaluate a strategy.

**3. Mistaking Goals for Strategy** — Stating desired outcomes (grow 20%, be #1)
without any plan for how to get there. "We will grow by 20% this year" is not a strategy.
It's a wish.

"A leader may justly ask for 'ichiban' — being the best — as a general direction. But
it is not a strategy. It doesn't tell you what to do."

**4. Bad Strategic Objectives** — A long list of "priorities" that are really just a
repackaging of the current wish list. If everything is a priority, nothing is.
Real objectives are prioritized and focused.

### Sources of Power (Rumelt)

**Leverage** — Anticipate a pivot point or focus of effort where relatively small actions
achieve large outcomes. "A good strategy draws power from focusing minds, energy,
and action."

**Proximate Objectives** — Objectives close enough to be feasible. A good leader
translates the ultimate goal into a proximate objective that is achievable and whose
accomplishment will shift the position favorably.

Kennedy's "put a man on the moon by the end of the decade" was a proximate objective
— specific, time-bound, and just barely feasible. It concentrated effort.

**Chain-Link Systems** — A system where performance is limited by its weakest link.
Improving one link while leaving others weak accomplishes nothing. The system must
be improved as a whole. IKEA's strategy works because ALL elements reinforce each
other — change one and the whole system breaks.

**Design** — Strategy as an integrated design rather than a collection of choices.
The elements must fit together like the components of a machine.

**Focus** — Concentrating resources on a narrow front. Refusing to do many things
to do one thing extraordinarily well. Implies sacrifice.

**Growth** — Growth itself is NOT a strategy. It can be an outcome of a good strategy.
Companies that pursue growth for its own sake often destroy value.

**Advantage** — True competitive advantage must have both:
1. A way of producing superior value or lower cost
2. An isolating mechanism that prevents competitors from duplicating it

"No one has an advantage at everything. Teams, organizations, and even nations have
advantages in certain kinds of rivalry and not others."

**Dynamics** — Riding waves of change. External shifts (technology, regulation,
demographics, taste) create asymmetries that favor some companies over others.
The strategist's job is to identify these shifts and position to exploit them.

**Inertia and Entropy** — Incumbent organizations suffer from both:
- Inertia: resistance to change even when the environment has shifted
- Entropy: the natural tendency for organizations to become less organized,
  less focused, and less efficient over time without active effort

These create opportunities for challengers.

### Strategy as Problem-Solving

Rumelt's fundamental point: strategy is NOT about vision, motivation, or aspiration.
It's about identifying the most critical challenge and devising a practical approach
to overcome it.

"The core of strategy work is always the same: discovering the critical factors in a
situation and designing a way of coordinating and focusing actions to deal with
those factors."

The DEC Example: When Digital Equipment Corporation's senior leadership couldn't
agree on strategy (Chips vs. Solutions vs. Boxes), they compromised with a bland
statement that committed to nothing. This isn't consensus — it's the absence of
strategy. Real strategy requires someone to make the hard choice.

## Part 3: Playing to Win (Lafley & Martin)

### The Strategy Choice Cascade

Strategy is an integrated set of choices that positions a business to win in its
chosen market. These choices form a cascade — each choice constrains and
enables the ones below it.

```
1. WINNING ASPIRATION
   ↓
2. WHERE TO PLAY
   ↓
3. HOW TO WIN
   ↓
4. CORE CAPABILITIES
   ↓
5. MANAGEMENT SYSTEMS
```

### Choice 1: Winning Aspiration

What does winning look like? This is NOT a vague mission statement. It defines
specific, meaningful aspirations for what the organization will achieve.

P&G example: "We want to win with the consumer at the first moment of truth
(when they choose the product at the shelf) and the second moment of truth
(when they use the product at home)."

Bad aspiration: "Be the best." Good aspiration: "Be the low-cost leader in
mass-market laundry detergent in North America."

### Choice 2: Where to Play

This defines the playing field. Choices include:
- **Geography**: Which countries, regions, or markets?
- **Customer segments**: Which demographics, psychographics, or need-states?
- **Channels**: Retail, direct, online, wholesale, distributor?
- **Product categories**: Which specific product lines or services?
- **Vertical stage**: Where in the value chain? (Design, manufacture, distribute, retail?)

The most important where-to-play decision is which customers to serve and
which to NOT serve. "Where not to play" is as important as "where to play."

P&G's Olay example: Chose to play in "masstige" (mass-market prestige) — a
space between mass-market and luxury that no one owned. This was a WHERE
decision that opened up a $1 billion opportunity.

### Choice 3: How to Win

This is the theory of competitive advantage. There are two fundamental approaches:

**Cost Leadership** — Win by offering acceptable value at a meaningfully lower cost
than competitors. Requires scale, efficiency, and ruthless cost management.

**Differentiation** — Win by offering distinctly higher value that customers will pay
a premium for. Requires deep understanding of what customers truly value.

"Where to Play" and "How to Win" must be mutually reinforcing. You cannot play
in premium segments with a cost-leadership strategy, or in price-sensitive segments
with a differentiation strategy.

P&G Tide example: Differentiation strategy — invest heavily in R&D to make Tide
genuinely the best-performing detergent, then price at a premium. The "how to win"
(product superiority) matched the "where to play" (consumers who care about clean).

### Choice 4: Core Capabilities

What capabilities must the organization have to execute the where-to-play and
how-to-win choices? These are the 3-6 things you must be WORLD-CLASS at.

P&G's core capabilities:
1. Deep consumer understanding (research and insight)
2. Innovation (R&D, product development)
3. Brand building (marketing, advertising)
4. Go-to-market capability (retail relationships, distribution)
5. Global scale (manufacturing, supply chain)

**Activity System Maps:** Map how your capabilities connect and reinforce each other.
The strength of a strategy lies in the fit between activities — not in any single activity.

### Choice 5: Management Systems

What structures, processes, and measures are needed to support and reinforce
the other four choices?

This is the least glamorous but most important enabler. Without systems that
reinforce strategy, the organization defaults to inertia and entropy.

Includes: planning processes, resource allocation, performance measurement,
talent development, incentive structures, cultural norms.

### The Logic Flow Test

For any strategy, test:
1. What would have to be true about the INDUSTRY for this to be a winning strategy?
2. What would have to be true about our CHANNEL for this to work?
3. What would have to be true about the CUSTOMER for this to work?
4. What would have to be true about our CAPABILITIES for this to work?
5. What would have to be true about the COMPETITIVE RESPONSE for this to work?

If any of these "what would have to be true" conditions is clearly false,
the strategy has a fatal flaw.

### Reverse-Engineering Competitors

Use the Choice Cascade to decode competitor strategies:
- Where are they choosing to play? (Which segments, geographies, channels?)
- How are they trying to win? (Cost or differentiation?)
- What capabilities are they investing in?
- Where are the gaps in their strategy?
- What "what would have to be true" conditions might be false for them?

This reveals opportunities: spaces they've chosen NOT to play, capabilities
they lack, assumptions they're making that might be wrong.

## Integration: How the Three Frameworks Connect

| Framework | Core Question | Key Contribution |
|-----------|--------------|------------------|
| 7 Powers | "Can we sustain advantage?" | Identifies whether durable competitive advantage is possible |
| Good Strategy | "What's the real challenge?" | Forces honest diagnosis and coherent response |
| Playing to Win | "What choices define our strategy?" | Provides a systematic cascade for making integrated choices |

**Use them together:**
1. Start with Rumelt's Diagnosis → identify the challenge
2. Apply Lafley's Cascade → make the where/how/capabilities choices
3. Validate with Helmer's 7 Powers → confirm that a source of power exists

If you complete steps 1-2 but step 3 reveals no source of power, you have a
plan but not a sustainable strategy. Revisit your choices.

### Connection to Hormozi's Frameworks

Hormozi operates primarily at the OFFER and CUSTOMER level, but his work has deep
strategic implications:

- **Hormozi's "starving crowd"** = Rumelt's diagnosis applied to market selection.
  The biggest strategic mistake is choosing a market that doesn't desperately need
  what you sell — no amount of strategy can fix an absent market.

- **The Value Equation** (Dream Outcome × Likelihood / Time × Effort) is a practical
  tool for Lafley's "How to Win" choice. It answers: how do we create such compelling
  value that the customer has no rational reason to say no?

- **Hormozi's avatar exercise** (find top 20%, study them, replicate the buying journey)
  is a practical implementation of Lafley's "Where to Play." Instead of choosing markets
  theoretically, Hormozi uses customer data to discover which segments are already
  the most profitable and doubles down.

- **The Money Model** (attraction → core → upsell → downsell → continuity) creates
  Helmer's Scale Economies in customer acquisition: the business that makes the most
  per customer can outspend everyone else on acquisition, creating a compounding
  flywheel that competitors can't match without restructuring their entire model
  (which is Counter-Positioning).

| # | Question | Y/N | Evidence |

| 4 | Would a new entrant need to invest heavily before reaching competitive cost parity? | ___ | _________________________ |
| 5 | Are your cost advantages widening (not narrowing) over time? | ___ | _________________________ |

**Scoring Criteria:**
- **Exists:** 4-5 Y answers. You have demonstrable per-unit cost advantages driven by volume. You can show the cost curve and where competitors sit on it. Competitors would need to match your scale to compete on cost — and doing so is prohibitively expensive or slow.
- **Building:** 2-3 Y answers. You have some cost advantages from volume but they are not yet decisive. Your scale lead is growing but competitors could still catch up. You have a plausible path to cost-structure dominance within 2-3 years.
- **Absent:** 0-1 Y answers. Your costs do not materially benefit from scale, or competitors operate at comparable or greater scale. No structural cost advantage exists or is forming.

**Diagnostic Questions:**

| # | Question | Y/N | Evidence |
|---|----------|-----|----------|
| 1 | Does each new user make the product more valuable for existing users? | ___ | _________________________ |
| 2 | Have you passed a critical mass threshold where the network is self-sustaining? | ___ | _________________________ |

**Scoring Criteria:**
- **Exists:** 4-5 Y answers. Your network is large enough that competitors face a cold-start problem they cannot realistically solve. Users explicitly cite "everyone is already here" as a reason to stay. Switching would mean losing access to connections, data, or marketplace liquidity.
- **Building:** 2-3 Y answers. You see early network effects (users inviting others, marketplace liquidity improving) but have not yet reached a tipping point. A well-funded competitor could still build a rival network.
- **Absent:** 0-1 Y answers. Your product's value does not increase with the number of users, or you are building a network but have not achieved meaningful density in any segment.

**Status:** [ Exists / Building / Absent ]

### Power #3: Counter-Positioning Assessment

**Diagnostic Questions:**

| # | Question | Y/N | Evidence |
|---|----------|-----|----------|
| 1 | Does your business model create value in a way that incumbents cannot copy without damaging their existing business? | ___ | _________________________ |
| 2 | Have incumbents publicly acknowledged your approach but chosen not to adopt it? | ___ | _________________________ |
| 3 | Would an incumbent adopting your model need to cannibalize >20% of existing revenue? | ___ | _________________________ |
| 4 | Is the incumbent's rational response to ignore you (at least for now)? | ___ | _________________________ |

**Scoring Criteria:**
- **Exists:** 3-4 Y answers. You have a clearly superior model and incumbents are visibly choosing not to respond. You can articulate the specific collateral damage they would suffer. Incumbents may be publicly dismissing your approach while privately worried.
- **Building:** 1-2 Y answers. Your model is different from incumbents but it is unclear whether the difference creates enough collateral damage to prevent response. Incumbents may be able to launch a separate brand or division to match you.
- **Absent:** 0 Y answers. Your model does not create a meaningful dilemma for incumbents, or incumbents can easily copy your approach without harming their existing business.

**Status:** [ Exists / Building / Absent ]

### Power #4: Switching Costs Assessment

**Diagnostic Questions:**

| # | Question | Y/N | Evidence |
|---|----------|-----|----------|
| 1 | Would a customer switching to a competitor face >$10K in direct costs (migration, retraining, lost data)? | ___ | _________________________ |
| 2 | Does your product become more embedded in the customer's workflow over time? | ___ | _________________________ |
| 3 | Is your gross revenue retention >90% annually (excluding expansion)? | ___ | _________________________ |
| 4 | Do customers store critical data or build custom configurations in your product? | ___ | _________________________ |
| 5 | When customers do churn, do they cite price or company-level decisions — not product dissatisfaction? | ___ | _________________________ |

**Scoring Criteria:**
- **Exists:** 4-5 Y answers. Customers are deeply integrated. Switching would require weeks or months of effort and significant direct cost. Your retention rate exceeds industry average by 10+ points. Competitors must offer dramatically superior value to overcome inertia.
- **Building:** 2-3 Y answers. Some customers are deeply embedded but others use your product lightly enough to switch easily. You have a roadmap for deeper integration (APIs, data capture, workflow embedding) that will increase switching costs over time.
- **Absent:** 0-1 Y answers. Customers can switch to a competitor in days or weeks with minimal friction. No significant data, workflow, or financial lock-in exists.

**Status:** [ Exists / Building / Absent ]

### Power #5: Branding Assessment

**Diagnostic Questions:**

| # | Question | Y/N | Evidence |
|---|----------|-----|----------|
| 1 | Can you charge a >15% price premium over functionally equivalent competitors? | ___ | _________________________ |
| 2 | Do customers choose you partly because of what the brand signals about THEM? | ___ | _________________________ |
| 3 | Does your brand reduce customer uncertainty enough to shorten sales cycles? | ___ | _________________________ |
| 4 | Has your brand been built over >3 years of consistent behavior and messaging? | ___ | _________________________ |

**Scoring Criteria:**

- **Building:** 1-2 Y answers. You have early brand recognition in your niche but it does not yet command a price premium or shorten sales cycles materially. Your brand is known but not yet trusted or aspirational.

### Which Powers Conflict or Create Tension

## Part 6: Worked Strategy Diagnosis

### Company: CommandPost — Project Management for Commercial Construction

**Background:** CommandPost is a B2B SaaS company founded 3 years ago by two former
construction project managers. The product is a project management platform built
specifically for commercial construction companies (general contractors, subcontractors,
and owners managing $5M-$500M projects). The platform handles daily logs, RFIs,
submittals, change orders, punch lists, and schedule management.

**Current state:**
- $4.2M ARR, growing 65% year-over-year
- 180 paying companies, average contract value $23K/year
- 14 employees (6 engineering, 3 sales, 2 customer success, 2 founders, 1 ops)
- Net revenue retention: 112%
- Gross margin: 78%
- $1.8M remaining in seed/Series A funding at current burn rate (~9 months runway)
- Primary market: Southeastern US commercial general contractors, $10M-$200M in annual revenue

**Competitive landscape:**
- Procore ($700M+ revenue, publicly traded, dominant market leader in construction PM)
- PlanGrid (acquired by Autodesk for $875M, focused on field management)
- Autodesk Construction Cloud (suite approach, deep BIM integration)
- Buildertrend (focused on residential, expanding to light commercial)
- Various legacy tools: Excel, paper-based systems, email chains
- 15+ smaller SaaS competitors in construction tech niches

---

### Step 1: Rumelt Kernel — Diagnosis

**The naive diagnosis (wrong):** "We need to grow faster and raise more money to
compete with Procore."

This is bad strategy — it mistakes a goal for a diagnosis. It does not identify
the actual challenge. Let us go deeper.

**The real diagnosis:**

CommandPost's fundamental challenge is that it operates in a market with a dominant
incumbent (Procore) that has 150x its revenue, massive brand recognition, and deep
feature coverage — yet the mid-market commercial construction segment that CommandPost
serves is systematically underserved by Procore for three specific reasons:

1. **Procore's pricing model is per-user, and scales with project volume.** For a
   50-person GC running $100M in annual projects, Procore costs $75K-$150K/year.
   This is painful enough that many mid-market GCs either (a) limit who gets licenses,
   creating information silos, or (b) refuse to adopt Procore at all, staying on
   spreadsheets and paper.

2. **Procore's product complexity reflects enterprise needs.** It was built for
   Turner Construction and Skanska — companies with dedicated technology teams and
   formal training programs. A 50-person GC does not have a training department.
   Their superintendents are 55-year-old former carpenters who will not attend a
   3-day Procore certification course. Procore's power features (advanced analytics,
   BIM integration, financial management) are noise that makes the product harder
   to learn for users who need daily logs, RFIs, and punch lists.

3. **The construction industry's technology adoption is bifurcated.** The top 100
   ENR contractors have adopted Procore or Autodesk. The remaining 35,000+
   commercial GCs in the US (the mid-market and SMB tier) still run on Excel,
   text messages, and paper. This is not because software doesn't exist — it's
   because the available software was designed for the top of the market and
   priced/complicated accordingly.

**The critical insight (the diagnosis):** The mid-market construction segment does
not need a "better Procore." They need a fundamentally simpler, cheaper, and more
opinionated product that makes the 80% of workflows that matter dead simple — and
ignores the 20% of enterprise features they will never use. The challenge is NOT
technology; it is building a product and go-to-market motion that reaches and
converts a segment that is deeply skeptical of technology, difficult to reach through
digital channels, and accustomed to solving problems with phone calls and relationships.

This is a distribution challenge wrapped in a product challenge.

---

### Step 2: Rumelt Kernel — Guiding Policy

**Guiding policy:** CommandPost will become the "QuickBooks of construction project
management" — the product that mid-market GCs adopt not because they need sophisticated
project management but because they need to stop losing money on miscommunication,
missed RFIs, and change order disputes. We will win by being radically simpler, priced
per-company (not per-user) to encourage full-team adoption, and distributed through
the relationship networks that construction companies already trust (trade associations,
material suppliers, insurance brokers).

**What this guiding policy rules out:**
- Competing with Procore on features (we will never match their feature depth)
- Selling to ENR Top 100 contractors (they already have Procore)
- Pursuing a platform/marketplace strategy (premature complexity)
- Expanding to residential (different buyer, different workflow, dilutes focus)
- Raising a large Series B to "out-spend" competitors on sales (unsustainable for our segment)

**What this guiding policy channels effort toward:**
- Product: Radical simplicity. If a superintendent cannot use a feature within 60 seconds
  of seeing it for the first time, the feature is too complex. Mobile-first. Offline-capable
  (construction sites have poor connectivity).
- Pricing: Flat annual fee per company, unlimited users. This is the single most important
  go-to-market decision because it removes the adoption barrier. When everyone on the
  jobsite has access, the product becomes the communication backbone — not a tool that
  a few project managers use in isolation.
- Distribution: Channel partnerships with construction trade associations, material
  suppliers (e.g., ABC Supply), and insurance brokers who already have trusted
  relationships with our target buyers. This is cheaper and more effective than
  outbound sales for reaching a segment that does not read SaaS blogs or attend
  tech conferences.
- Geography: Deepen in the Southeast before expanding. Construction is regional.
  Relationships, regulations, and workflows vary by region. Dominate one region,
  then expand.

### Step 3: Rumelt Kernel — Coherent Actions

These actions are coordinated and mutually reinforcing. They work together as a system.

**Product actions (Q1-Q2):**
- Ship offline mode for daily logs and punch lists (addresses #1 pain point cited by
  churned prospects: "we can't use it on the jobsite")
- Remove 30% of settings and options from the UI (simplification sprint — every
  removed option is a reduced training burden)
- Build "QuickStart" template: when a new GC signs up, they answer 5 questions and
  get a pre-configured workspace for their project type (commercial office, retail
  buildout, industrial). Time to first value: under 10 minutes.
- Add photo markup on mobile (the #1 feature request — superintendents communicate
  with photos, not text)

**Pricing actions (Q1):**
- Shift from per-user pricing ($45/user/month) to flat company pricing ($399/month for
  companies <50 people, $799/month for 50-200). Unlimited users. This will reduce
  short-term ARPU but dramatically increase adoption density per company (more users
  per account → more switching costs → higher retention → more word-of-mouth).
- Offer annual prepay discount (20%) to improve cash flow and reduce churn.

**Distribution actions (Q2-Q3):**
- Sign partnership agreements with 3 Southeast regional trade associations
  (AGC chapters in GA, FL, NC). Offer members a 15% discount. Trade associations
  get a referral fee. CommandPost gets booth space at regional events and inclusion
  in member communications.
- Pilot a co-marketing program with a regional material supplier (ABC Supply or
  equivalent). When a GC opens a new account with the supplier, they get a

  who use technology are more organized and place orders more efficiently.
- Hire 1 "field sales" rep who is a former superintendent. This person visits jobsites,
  not offices. They demo the product on an iPad in a trailer, not in a conference room.

**Customer success actions (Q2-Q4):**
- Launch "First 30 Days" onboarding program: dedicated CSM for the first month,
  focused on getting at least 5 users per company actively logging daily activity.
  The key metric is "weekly active users per company" — once this crosses 5, retention
  is 95%+. Below 5, retention drops to 60%.
- Build a community Slack for GC owners using CommandPost. Goal: peer-to-peer
  knowledge sharing. This is free marketing and creates switching costs (relationships
  and tribal knowledge in the community).

### Step 4: Helmer 7 Powers Assessment

**Scale Economies: ABSENT**

CommandPost is a small SaaS company competing against Procore ($700M+ revenue).
We have no scale advantage and will not achieve one in the foreseeable future. Procore
can outspend us 100:1 on R&D, sales, and marketing. However, scale economies in SaaS
have diminishing returns — Procore's marginal cost advantage over us in cloud
infrastructure and development is real but not decisive. Our cost structure is adequate
at current margins (78% gross). Scale economies are not our path to power.

Score: **Absent.** Not a viable power for CommandPost.

**Network Economies: BUILDING**

CommandPost has early, weak network effects. When multiple companies on the same
project (GC + subcontractors) use CommandPost, the product becomes more valuable —
RFIs, submittals, and change orders flow between companies seamlessly. Today, only
12% of our accounts have subcontractors also using the platform. But the unlimited-user
pricing model is designed to accelerate this: when the GC pays a flat fee and invites
subs to collaborate for free, the subs get exposure to the product. Some of those subs
then buy CommandPost for their own projects. We have seen 8 new accounts in the last
quarter that originated from a sub first using CommandPost as a guest on a GC's project.
This is the seed of a network effect, but it is far from critical mass.

Score: **Building.** Network effects are a plausible future power, but only if we
deliberately design for them. The unlimited-users pricing model is the key enabler.
Priority: instrument and accelerate the GC → sub → new GC viral loop.

**Counter-Positioning: EXISTS**

This is CommandPost's most important current power. Procore cannot adopt our model
without damaging their existing business in three ways:

1. Procore's per-user pricing generates $75K-$150K per mid-market account. Our flat
   $399-$799/month model generates $5K-$10K per account. For Procore to match our
   pricing for mid-market, they would need to either (a) create a separate product line
   at 90% lower price points, cannibalizing accounts that might have bought full
   Procore, or (b) offer steep discounts that would anger their existing enterprise
   customers paying full price.

2. Procore's product complexity is a feature for enterprise customers. Simplifying it for
   mid-market means either (a) creating a separate, simpler product (expensive, dilutes
   engineering focus) or (b) removing features from the core product (angers enterprise
   customers). Neither option is attractive when mid-market accounts generate 10x less
   revenue than enterprise accounts.

3. Procore's sales motion is enterprise: outbound SDRs, multi-month sales cycles, in-person
   demos. Serving the mid-market profitably requires a self-serve or channel-led motion at
   a fraction of the sales cost. Building this inside an organization optimized for $75K+
   ACV enterprise sales is culturally and operationally disruptive.

Procore's rational response is to ignore mid-market (as they largely do today) and
continue moving upmarket to larger contractors and project owners. This gives CommandPost
room to grow.

Score: **Exists.** Counter-positioning is our primary power today. It is time-limited —
if we succeed, Procore will eventually respond. We must use the window to build
additional powers (switching costs, network effects) before counter-positioning fades.

**Switching Costs: BUILDING**

Current switching costs are moderate. Companies that use CommandPost for 6+ months
have project history, templates, and document archives that would be costly to migrate.
However, the data is exportable (we provide CSV/PDF exports), and most companies
have been on the platform for less than a year. Switching costs are accumulating but
not yet decisive.

The coherent actions in our strategy are designed to accelerate switching cost
accumulation:
- Unlimited users means more people in the organization depend on the tool
- Photo archives on the platform become the project's visual record
- Community membership creates relational switching costs
- Integration with accounting software (planned) will deepen data lock-in

Score: **Building.** Switching costs are our second-priority power to develop.
Every product decision should be evaluated partly on whether it increases switching
costs. Target: within 18 months, a customer who has used CommandPost for 1+ year
should face a migration effort of 40+ hours to switch.

**Branding: ABSENT**

CommandPost has no meaningful brand recognition outside the Southeast. Even within
the Southeast, brand awareness is limited to a few hundred companies that have heard
of us through word-of-mouth or trade show appearances. We are years away from
branding as a source of power.

Score: **Absent.** Not a priority for the next 2-3 years. Focus on distribution
partnerships and word-of-mouth, which will build brand organically over time.

**Cornered Resource: BUILDING (FRAGILE)**

CommandPost's two founders have a genuine cornered resource: deep domain expertise
combined with technical ability. They spent 15 years each as construction project
managers before learning to code and building CommandPost. This combination (decade+
of field experience + technical skill + entrepreneurial drive) is exceptionally rare.
Their domain knowledge informs every product decision and is a primary reason the
product resonates with users.

However, this resource is fragile. It lives in two people. It is not yet institutionalized
in process, documentation, or culture. If either founder left, the domain expertise
advantage would erode.

Score: **Building (fragile).** We must transfer founder domain knowledge into
organizational knowledge: documented design principles, customer research processes,
and product decision frameworks that persist beyond any individual.

**Process Power: ABSENT**

CommandPost is a 14-person startup. Process power requires years of institutional
learning and is structurally unavailable at this stage. Not a relevant consideration
for the next 5+ years.

Score: **Absent.** Structurally unavailable. Ignore for now.

### Step 5: Lafley/Martin Choice Cascade

**Choice 1: Winning Aspiration**

"Become the default project management platform for mid-market commercial construction
companies in the United States — the product that GCs with $10M-$200M in annual revenue
choose because it is the simplest tool that makes their teams more organized, reduces
disputes, and protects their margins."

This aspiration is specific (mid-market commercial GCs), measurable (default = market
share leadership in the segment), and differentiating (simplest, not most powerful).

**Choice 2: Where to Play**

- **Customer segment:** Commercial general contractors with $10M-$200M in annual
  revenue and 20-200 employees. These companies are large enough to benefit from
  project management software but too small to have dedicated IT teams or to justify
  Procore's pricing. Secondary segment: subcontractors who collaborate with GC
  customers (viral expansion).

- **Geography:** Southeast US first (GA, FL, NC, SC, TN, AL). Expand to Texas and
  Mid-Atlantic by Year 5. National by Year 7. Construction is regional — workflows,
  regulations, and relationship networks vary. Winning regionally before expanding
  nationally is more defensible than thin national coverage.

- **Product scope:** Daily logs, RFIs, submittals, change orders, punch lists, photo
  documentation, and basic schedule management. Explicitly NOT: financial management,
  BIM integration, estimating, advanced analytics, or resource planning. We serve
  the 80% of workflows that matter to field teams.

- **Channel:** Channel-first distribution through trade associations, material suppliers,
  and insurance brokers. Supplemented by field sales (former superintendents visiting
  jobsites) and self-serve signup. NOT outbound SDR teams. NOT paid digital advertising
  (our buyers do not respond to LinkedIn ads or Google searches for construction software).

- **Where NOT to play:**
  - ENR Top 100 contractors (Procore's territory, enterprise sales cycle, different needs)
  - Residential construction (different buyer, different workflow, Buildertrend's territory)
  - International markets (premature, regulatory complexity, no distribution relationships)
  - Project owners / developers (different buyer persona, different workflow)

**Choice 3: How to Win**

CommandPost wins through **differentiation on simplicity and adoption density,** enabled
by a pricing model that removes the per-user adoption barrier.

This is NOT cost leadership. We do not win by being the cheapest (there are free tools).
We win by being the tool that EVERYONE on the jobsite actually uses because it is simple
enough that a 55-year-old superintendent can learn it in one lunch break and priced so
that the GC puts it in everyone's hands without worrying about per-seat costs.

The competitive advantage is adoption density: when 90% of people on a project use
CommandPost (vs. 15% who have Procore licenses at a typical mid-market GC), the data
quality is dramatically higher, the coordination is dramatically better, and the value
is dramatically greater. The product becomes the communication backbone of the project,
not a reporting tool that a few PMs use.

**What would have to be true for this to work:**
- Mid-market GCs must value simplicity over feature depth. (TRUE: validated through
  180 customer interviews during product development)
- Unlimited-user pricing must drive meaningfully higher adoption rates per company.
  (HYPOTHESIS: testing now. Early data shows companies on the new pricing model
  have 3.2x more active users than those on per-user pricing.)
- Channel distribution must reach GCs more effectively than direct sales. (HYPOTHESIS:
  testing with AGC Southeast chapter. Results expected in 6 months.)
- Procore must not respond by launching a simplified mid-market product. (PROBABLE:
  see counter-positioning analysis above. Procore's rational response is to continue
  moving upmarket.)

**Choice 4: Core Capabilities**

CommandPost must be world-class at exactly four things:

1. **Domain-informed product design.** The ability to translate construction field
   workflows into software that feels intuitive to non-technical users. This requires
   deep construction domain expertise embedded in the product team — not just UX
   skill. Every design decision must be informed by how a superintendent actually
   works: standing on a jobsite, phone in one hand, coffee in the other, hard hat on,
   30 seconds between interruptions.

2. **Channel relationship management.** The ability to build and maintain distribution
   partnerships with trade associations, suppliers, and brokers. This is a capability
   most SaaS companies do not have. It requires people who understand the construction
   industry's relationship-driven culture, can speak the language, and can create
   win-win partnership structures.

3. **Frictionless onboarding.** The ability to take a company from signup to productive
   use in under one week, without requiring dedicated training sessions. This is a
   product AND customer success capability. It requires obsessive attention to the
   first-use experience, smart defaults, and proactive customer success intervention
   when adoption stalls.

4. **Mid-market SaaS economics.** The ability to operate profitably at a $5K-$10K ACV
   price point. This requires low customer acquisition cost (channel distribution, not
   outbound sales), high retention (adoption density drives retention), and efficient
   operations. Most SaaS companies optimize for $50K+ ACV. We must build a fundamentally
   different operating model.

**Choice 5: Management Systems**

- **Metric system:** Primary metric is "weekly active users per company" (not revenue,
  not logos). This drives all downstream outcomes: retention, expansion, NPS, and referrals.
  Every team meeting starts with this number. Every product decision is evaluated against
  it. Revenue is a lagging indicator; adoption density is a leading indicator.

- **Product development process:** Two-week sprints. Every sprint includes at least one
  "simplification ticket" (remove a setting, reduce a workflow to fewer steps, eliminate
  a screen). The product must get simpler over time, not more complex. Feature requests
  from individual customers are captured but not automatically prioritized — they go
  through a "would this help 80% of our customers?" filter.

- **Customer feedback loop:** Every new customer gets a 15-minute call at Day 7, Day 30,
  and Day 90. The call is recorded and tagged. Product team listens to every Day 7 call
  to understand the onboarding experience in real time. Churn interviews are mandatory
  and reviewed by the founders.

- **Partnership scorecard:** Each channel partnership is measured on referrals generated,
  conversion rate of referrals, and partner satisfaction (quarterly survey). Partnerships
  that do not generate at least 5 qualified leads per quarter are restructured or ended.

- **Hiring filter:** Every hire, including engineering, must pass a "construction IQ" test.
  We do not hire people who think construction is an unsexy industry. Our people must
  genuinely respect the craft and the people who build things.

### Step 6: Integration — How the Three Frameworks Reinforce Each Other

**Rumelt's diagnosis** identified the core challenge: the mid-market is underserved not
because software does not exist but because the available software was designed for a
different segment. The challenge is distribution and simplicity, not technology.

**Lafley's cascade** translated this diagnosis into specific choices: WHERE (mid-market
commercial GCs, Southeast, channel distribution), HOW (simplicity + adoption density +
flat pricing), CAPABILITIES (domain-informed design, channel management, frictionless
onboarding, mid-market economics), and SYSTEMS (WAU per company as the north star metric).

**Helmer's 7 Powers** validated that a durable advantage is possible: counter-positioning
exists today (Procore cannot rationally respond), switching costs are building (adoption
density increases stickiness), and network effects are seedling-stage (GC → sub viral loop).
The assessment also revealed what will NOT be a source of power (scale economies, branding,
process power) and therefore should not receive disproportionate investment.

The three frameworks caught errors that any single framework would have missed:
- Without Rumelt: We might have stated "be the best construction PM tool" (bad strategy:
  a goal, not a strategy) without diagnosing WHY the mid-market is underserved.
- Without Lafley: We might have a good diagnosis but no systematic set of choices —
  trying to serve all segments, use all channels, build all features.
- Without Helmer: We might have a coherent plan that lacks any source of durable
  advantage — Procore could copy everything and crush us with scale.

- Launch accounting software integration (QuickBooks, Sage)
- Build photo archive and project history features that make switching painful
- Launch customer community
- Deepen trade association partnerships, sign 2 more
- Pilot material supplier co-marketing program
- Target: 400 companies, $8M ARR, >8 WAU per company average

**Months 13-24: Prove network effects and expand geography**
- Instrument and optimize the GC → sub → new GC viral loop
- Launch subcontractor collaboration features (free for subs on GC projects)
- Expand to Texas and Mid-Atlantic (new trade association partnerships)

  and early network effects

**Months 25-36: Establish as mid-market standard**

**The bet we are making:** Procore will not launch a simplified mid-market product

## Part 7: Strategy Quality Scorecard

Use this scorecard to evaluate whether a strategy is rigorous enough to act on.
Score each criterion 1-5. Minimum passing score: 53/75 (70%). Below that threshold,
the strategy needs more work before implementation.

### Category A: Diagnosis Quality (5 criteria)

| # | Criterion | Score (1-5) | Notes |
|---|-----------|-------------|-------|
| A1 | **Challenge specificity.** The diagnosis identifies a specific, falsifiable challenge — not a vague problem like "we need to grow." | ___ | ___ |
| A2 | **Root cause depth.** The diagnosis explains WHY the challenge exists, not just WHAT it is. It identifies structural causes, not symptoms. | ___ | ___ |
| A3 | **Evidence grounding.** The diagnosis is supported by concrete evidence (data, customer interviews, market analysis), not assumptions or intuition alone. | ___ | ___ |
| A4 | **Competitive honesty.** The diagnosis honestly assesses competitive position — including where the business is weak, where competitors are strong, and what could go wrong. | ___ | ___ |
| A5 | **Simplification power.** The diagnosis simplifies a complex situation into a clear framing that tells you where to focus. Reading it, you think "yes, THAT is the problem." | ___ | ___ |

**Anchor descriptions:**

| Criterion | Score 1 (Failing) | Score 3 (Adequate) | Score 5 (Excellent) |
|---|---|---|---|
| A1 | "Our challenge is to grow revenue." No specific obstacle identified. | Identifies a specific problem (e.g., "our churn rate is too high") but does not frame it as a strategic challenge. | Identifies a specific structural challenge with clear implications for action (e.g., "our mid-market segment is underserved because incumbents' pricing and complexity create an adoption barrier"). |
| A2 | States the problem with no analysis of causes. Reads like a complaint. | Identifies one cause but misses deeper structural drivers. Treats symptoms as causes. | Traces the challenge to its structural roots — market structure, competitive dynamics, buyer behavior, or industry economics. You understand not just what is happening but why. |
| A3 | Pure opinion. "We believe..." with no supporting evidence. | Some evidence cited but not systematically gathered. Relies on anecdotes rather than patterns. | Multiple evidence types (quantitative data, customer interviews, competitive analysis, market research) converge on the same diagnosis. Evidence is specific and recent. |
| A4 | Ignores competitors or dismisses them. "We're better than everyone." No mention of weaknesses. | Acknowledges competitors exist but does not analyze their strategies or strengths in depth. | Honest, specific assessment of where competitors are stronger. Names the specific advantages competitors hold and the specific conditions under which the strategy would fail. |
| A5 | Reading the diagnosis, you still don't know what the main problem is. It's a laundry list. | The diagnosis narrows focus to 2-3 issues but doesn't create a unifying frame. | One clear sentence captures the core insight. The diagnosis reframes the situation in a way that makes the strategic response feel almost obvious. |

**Common failure pattern (score of 2):** The diagnosis is actually a list of goals
disguised as challenges. "Our challenge is that we need to reach $10M ARR" is not a
diagnosis — it is an aspiration restated as a problem. A real diagnosis explains what
obstacle stands between here and there, and why that obstacle exists.

### Category B: Strategic Coherence (5 criteria)

| # | Criterion | Score (1-5) | Notes |
|---|-----------|-------------|-------|
| B1 | **Guiding policy clarity.** The strategy states a clear guiding policy that channels action. You could explain it to a new hire in 30 seconds. | ___ | ___ |
| B2 | **Choice commitment.** The strategy explicitly states what the business will NOT do. Real trade-offs are visible. Something meaningful has been sacrificed. | ___ | ___ |
| B3 | **Action coherence.** The actions listed all serve the guiding policy. They reinforce each other. No action contradicts another. | ___ | ___ |
| B4 | **Feasibility.** The actions are achievable with the resources available (team, funding, time). The strategy does not require magical increases in capacity. | ___ | ___ |
| B5 | **Cascade integration.** Where-to-play, how-to-win, capabilities, and systems are all aligned. Changing one would require changing the others. | ___ | ___ |

**Anchor descriptions:**

| Criterion | Score 1 (Failing) | Score 3 (Adequate) | Score 5 (Excellent) |
|---|---|---|---|
| B1 | No guiding policy stated. Strategy jumps from problems to actions with no connective logic. | A guiding policy exists but is too vague to guide decisions. "We will differentiate through innovation." Which innovation? In what dimension? | The guiding policy is specific enough that a team member could use it to make a decision the CEO would agree with, even without asking. It includes what TO do and what NOT to do. |
| B2 | Everything is a priority. The strategy tries to serve all segments, use all channels, and build all features. No trade-offs visible. | Some trade-offs stated but they are easy ones ("we won't serve the Mongolian yak farming market"). The hard trade-offs are avoided. | Trade-offs are painful and real. The strategy explicitly walks away from revenue, segments, or capabilities that would be nice to have. Reading the "will not do" list, you wince slightly because some of those things are tempting. |
| B3 | Actions are a disjointed list. Each action addresses a different problem. They do not work together as a system. | Actions are related to the guiding policy but there are 1-2 that seem disconnected or that pull in a different direction. | Every action directly serves the guiding policy AND reinforces at least one other action. The actions form a chain-link system where the whole is greater than the sum of parts. |
| B4 | The strategy requires 3x current resources or capabilities that do not exist and would take years to build. It is aspirational, not actionable. | Actions are technically feasible but stretch resources to the breaking point. Execution risk is very high. | Actions are achievable with current or near-term resources. The strategy concentrates force rather than spreading it thin. Each action has an implicit "by when" and "by whom" that is realistic. |
| B5 | Where/how/capabilities/systems are disconnected. You could change one without affecting the others — a sign they are not truly integrated. | Most elements are aligned but one or two feel bolted on. The management systems, in particular, don't clearly reinforce the strategic choices. | All five cascade elements are tightly integrated. The where-to-play choice makes the how-to-win choice obvious. The capabilities required flow directly from how-to-win. The management systems explicitly measure and reinforce the cascade. |

**Common failure pattern (score of 2):** The strategy has a clear guiding policy but the
actions do not match. The policy says "we will win through simplicity" but the roadmap
is packed with complex new features. This is organizational inertia overriding strategy.
The words point one direction; the resource allocation points another.

### Category C: Power Assessment (5 criteria)

| # | Criterion | Score (1-5) | Notes |
|---|-----------|-------------|-------|
| C1 | **Power identification.** The strategy explicitly identifies which of the 7 Powers is being built or leveraged. The power source is named, not implied. | ___ | ___ |
| C2 | **Barrier articulation.** The strategy explains WHY competitors cannot simply copy the approach. The barrier is specific and structural, not "we'll execute better." | ___ | ___ |
| C3 | **Power timeline.** The strategy acknowledges which powers are available now, which are being built, and which are structurally unavailable. There is a sequencing plan. | ___ | ___ |
| C4 | **Durability assessment.** The strategy addresses how long the power source will last and what could erode it. It names the specific conditions under which the advantage disappears. | ___ | ___ |
| C5 | **Power-market fit.** The identified power source is appropriate for the market and business type. The strategy is not trying to build network effects in a market that doesn't support them, or branding in year one. | ___ | ___ |

**Anchor descriptions:**

| Criterion | Score 1 (Failing) | Score 3 (Adequate) | Score 5 (Excellent) |
|---|---|---|---|
| C1 | No mention of competitive advantage durability. "We'll win because we're better." No power source identified. | A power source is implied but not explicitly named or analyzed. "Our product is hard to switch from" (switching costs, unnamed). | One or two specific powers are named, assessed for current status (exists / building / absent), and connected to specific strategic actions that build or leverage them. |
| C2 | "We'll execute better" or "we'll move faster" — no structural barrier. Competitors could do the same thing tomorrow. | A barrier is identified but it is weak or temporary. "We have a 6-month head start" is not a barrier — it is a countdown timer. | The barrier is structural and specific. You can name the specific reason competitors cannot or will not copy the approach, and this reason is rooted in market structure, business model conflict, or accumulated assets — not just speed. |
| C3 | No awareness that powers have timing dependencies. Trying to build branding in year one or process power in a startup. | Some awareness of timing but no explicit sequencing plan. "We'll build switching costs and eventually develop branding." | Clear sequencing: "Counter-positioning is our power today. We are using the counter-positioning window (estimated 3 years) to build switching costs and network effects. Branding and process power are structurally unavailable and are not investment priorities." |
| C4 | No mention of what could go wrong. No consideration of how the advantage might erode. | Acknowledges that advantages are not permanent but does not analyze specific erosion scenarios or timelines. | Names specific threats to each power source (e.g., "Procore could launch a simplified mid-market product, which would erode our counter-positioning. We estimate a 3-year window based on their current strategy and organizational incentives."). Includes a contingency. |
| C5 | The power being built does not match the market. E.g., trying to build network effects in a market with no interaction between users. | The power matches the market but the fit is not rigorously argued. It feels like the right power but you cannot articulate exactly why. | The power source is a natural fit for the market structure. The argument for why THIS power in THIS market is rigorous: "Network effects are possible because construction projects inherently involve multiple companies who must coordinate, creating natural multi-tenant usage patterns." |

**Common failure pattern (score of 2):** The strategy claims "our product is better" as
the competitive advantage but cannot articulate a structural barrier. Product superiority
without a barrier is a temporary advantage — competitors can and will match features.
If the only answer to "why can't they copy us?" is "we'll stay ahead," there is no power.

### Red Flags That Your Strategy Is Actually Bad Strategy

This checklist operationalizes Rumelt's four hallmarks. If any of these are true,
stop and rework. Do not execute bad strategy harder.

**Fluff Indicators:**
- [ ] The strategy document uses words like "synergy," "leverage" (as a verb without a specific lever), "best-in-class," "world-class," "cutting-edge," or "innovative" without defining what those words mean in concrete terms
- [ ] You could replace your company name with a competitor's name and the strategy would still read as true
- [ ] The strategy reads like a press release: optimistic, forward-looking, and devoid of hard choices
- [ ] Key terms are not defined. "Premium experience" — premium compared to what? For whom?

**Failure to Face the Challenge:**
- [ ] The strategy does not name the single most important obstacle to success
- [ ] When asked "what is the hardest problem this strategy solves?", the team gives different answers
- [ ] The diagnosis section could be removed and the rest of the strategy would not change (because the actions were not derived from the diagnosis)
- [ ] Competitive threats are acknowledged in a perfunctory paragraph but do not influence any strategic choices

**Goals Masquerading as Strategy:**
- [ ] The strategy's "guiding policy" is actually a financial target ("achieve $20M ARR by 2027")
- [ ] The word "strategy" could be replaced with "goals" throughout the document without changing its meaning
- [ ] There is no explanation of HOW the desired outcomes will be achieved — only WHAT outcomes are desired
- [ ] The strategy would be equally valid for any company in the industry (it contains no company-specific insight)

**Bad Strategic Objectives:**
- [ ] There are more than 5 "strategic priorities." Real strategy has 1-3 priorities and many explicit depriorities.
- [ ] The priorities were generated by asking each department head for their top 3 objectives, then combining the lists. This is not strategy — it is aggregation.
- [ ] No objective has been removed from the list in the last 12 months. Lists that only grow indicate an inability to make trade-offs.
- [ ] The objectives do not have a clear logical relationship to each other. They are parallel rather than cascading.

## Part 8: Framework Selection Guide

### When to Use Which Framework

Not every situation requires all three frameworks. Use this decision matrix to determine
where to start based on your most pressing strategic question.

| Your Situation | Start With | Why | Then Add |
|---|---|---|---|
| "We're losing and I don't know why" | **Rumelt Diagnosis** | You need to identify the real challenge before you can choose a response. Most "losing" situations stem from a failure to diagnose correctly. | Lafley Cascade (to restructure choices), then Helmer (to validate power exists) |
| "We're winning now but I'm not sure it will last" | **Helmer 7 Powers** | You need to assess whether your current advantage is durable. If you lack power, your success is temporary and competition will erode it. | Rumelt (to diagnose emerging threats), then Lafley (to adjust choices if needed) |
| "We need to enter a new market or make a major strategic choice" | **Lafley/Martin Cascade** | You need a systematic framework for making integrated choices. Where to play and how to win are the core decisions. | Helmer (to validate that power is achievable in the chosen space), then Rumelt (to diagnose the specific challenge in the new market) |
| "We're a startup figuring out our strategy for the first time" | **Rumelt Diagnosis** then **Helmer 7 Powers** | Startups must first understand what challenge they are solving (Rumelt), then confirm that a source of power is available to them (Helmer). Lafley's Cascade helps formalize the choices but can feel premature for very early-stage companies. | Lafley Cascade (once you have product-market fit and need to formalize strategic choices) |
| "We need to evaluate a competitor's strategy" | **Lafley/Martin Cascade (reverse-engineered)** | The cascade gives you a structured way to decode what a competitor is choosing (and not choosing), revealing gaps and vulnerabilities. | Helmer (to assess which powers the competitor holds and where they're vulnerable) |
| "I need to prepare for a board meeting or investor presentation" | **All three, integrated** | Investors and boards want to see diagnosis (what's the challenge?), choices (what's the plan?), and power (why will you win long-term?). The integration protocol below shows how to combine them. | N/A — use the full integration protocol |
| "Our strategy exists but feels incoherent or unconvincing" | **Strategy Quality Scorecard** (Part 7) then **Rumelt Diagnosis** | Score the existing strategy first to identify specific weaknesses. Most strategies fail on diagnosis — start there. | Rework the weakest category using the appropriate framework |

### Integration Protocol: Using All Three Frameworks Together

When you need a complete strategic analysis, use this sequence. Each framework builds
on the output of the previous one.

```
STEP 1: RUMELT DIAGNOSIS
│
│  Input: Situation analysis, market data, competitive landscape, customer feedback
│  Process: Identify the single most critical challenge. Articulate WHY it exists.
│  Output: Diagnosis statement (the challenge), Guiding Policy (the approach),
│          Coherent Actions (the coordinated steps)
│
│  Key question answered: "What is the real problem, and what is our approach?"
│
│  ──── feeds into ────►
│
STEP 2: LAFLEY/MARTIN CHOICE CASCADE
│
│  Input: Rumelt's diagnosis and guiding policy
│  Process: Translate the guiding policy into the 5 cascade choices.
│           The diagnosis constrains where-to-play (focus on the segment where
│           the challenge is most acute). The guiding policy constrains
│           how-to-win (the approach must address the diagnosed challenge).
│  Output: Completed cascade (aspiration, where, how, capabilities, systems)
│
│  Key question answered: "What specific choices define our strategy?"
│
│  ──── feeds into ────►
│
STEP 3: HELMER 7 POWERS VALIDATION
│
│  Input: Completed choice cascade
│  Process: For the chosen where-to-play and how-to-win, assess which of
│           the 7 Powers exists, is building, or is absent. Confirm that at
│           least one power source is achievable.
│  Output: Power assessment, power sequencing plan, durability analysis
│
│  Key question answered: "Can we sustain this advantage, and for how long?"
│
│  ──── feeds back into ────►
│
STEP 4: ITERATION (if needed)
│
│  If Step 3 reveals no achievable power source:
│  → Return to Step 2 and change where-to-play or how-to-win
│  → If no combination yields power, return to Step 1 and re-diagnose
│    (the challenge may be that the market itself offers no path to power)
│
│  If Step 3 reveals power but on a different axis than expected:
│  → Adjust the choice cascade to align with the available power source
│  → This is normal — strategy is iterative, not linear
│
│  Output: Validated, integrated strategy with diagnosis, choices, and power
```
