# Ad Copy: Social, PPC, Display & Paid Media

Ads are copywriting in its most compressed, high-stakes form. You have 1-3 seconds to
stop the scroll, a few more to earn the click. Every word must be surgically precise.
This reference covers paid ad copy across platforms — informed by Hopkins' scientific
testing principles, Schwartz's awareness/sophistication framework, Sugarman's triggers,
Ogilvy's research-driven approach, and Sutherland's behavioral economics.

## 1. Universal Ad Copy Principles

> *See `psychology.md` Section 1 (Five Levels of Awareness) for the full awareness framework
> referenced throughout this section. See `frameworks.md` Section 17 (Chet Holmes' Buyer's
> Pyramid) for the 3%/7%/90% model applied below.*

### Ads Are Not Mini Sales Pages
An ad has ONE job: earn the click (or the conversion, for direct-response ads). It doesn't
need to tell the whole story. It needs to create enough curiosity, desire, or urgency that
the prospect takes the next step.

Hopkins: "The purpose of a headline is to pick out people you can interest. You wish to
talk to someone in a crowd. So the first thing you say is, 'Hey there, Bill Jones.' That
is what a headline should do." Your ad is a headline for your sales page.

### The Three Jobs of an Ad
1. **Stop** — interrupt the scroll / search / feed with a pattern break
2. **Engage** — deliver enough relevance and intrigue to hold attention
3. **Move** — compel the specific next action (click, watch, sign up, buy)

### The Awareness-Ad Match
The prospect's awareness level determines your ad strategy:

- **Cold traffic (Unaware/Problem Aware):** Lead with pain, story, curiosity, or
  identity. Focus on getting the click — let the landing page do the selling. Use indirect
  approaches. Schwartz: at low awareness, you cannot mention the product. Lead with the
  desire, the fear, or the fascination.
- **Warm traffic (Solution/Product Aware):** Lead with differentiators, proof, mechanism,
  or offers. Can be more direct. The mechanism enters the ad.
- **Hot traffic (Most Aware / Retargeting):** Lead with the offer, urgency, scarcity, or
  objection handling. Be direct. These people know you — just give them the reason to act NOW.

### Chet Holmes' Buyer's Pyramid Applied to Ads
Only 3% of your market is "buying now." Another 7% is "open to it." That's 10% reachable
with direct-response ads. The other 90% requires education-based and curiosity-based
approaches. This is why story ads and education ads outperform "buy now" ads for cold
traffic — they speak to the 90%, not just the 3%.

### Ogilvy's Ad Principles
- Every ad should tell the complete sales story — don't assume the reader will see
  multiple ads
- Make your ad look like editorial content, not advertising (Schwartz's Camouflage)
- Long copy sells more than short copy (for complex offers)
- Specificity in ads always outperforms vagueness
- Don't be clever — be clear. Sell, don't entertain.

## 2. The Hook — How to Stop the Scroll

The hook is the first 1-3 seconds (video) or the first line (text). It must create an
irresistible pull to keep consuming. The hook is the ad's headline — it does 80% of the
work (Ogilvy).

> *See `frameworks.md` Section 4 (Headline Formulas & Systems) for the full headline toolkit
> that feeds hook writing. See `psychology.md` Section 6 (Sugarman's 31 Psychological Triggers)
> — curiosity, story, specificity, and exclusivity are the four triggers that power the strongest
> hooks. See `voice-and-craft.md` Section 3 (The Art of the First Line) for sentence-level
> craft that makes hooks land harder.*

### Hook Categories

**Callout hooks** — directly address the target audience (Schwartz's selector principle):
"If you run a SaaS company doing $1M-$10M ARR, read this."
"Attention: homeowners with credit scores above 720"

**Contrarian hooks** — challenge conventional wisdom (creates the knowledge gap):
"Stop posting content. It's killing your business."
"Everything your dentist told you about whitening is wrong."

**Result hooks** — lead with a specific, impressive outcome:
"We generated $47K in 30 days for a local plumber using this method."
"She went from 0 to 10K followers in 6 weeks. Here's how."

**Curiosity hooks** — open an information gap (Sugarman's #1 trigger):
"There's a reason most ads fail in the first 3 seconds..."
"What happened when we broke every 'rule' of Facebook ads..."

**Story hooks** — open with the most compelling moment of a story (Caples technique):
"I was about to shut down my agency when I got an email that changed everything."
"Last Tuesday, a client called me crying. But these were happy tears."

**Question hooks** — force self-reflection (Schwartz's inclusion questions):
"What would your business look like with 50 new leads every week?"
"Why are your competitors outranking you with worse products?"

**Pain hooks** — nail the exact frustration using their exact language (VOC mining):
"Spending $5K/month on ads and getting nothing but tire-kickers?"
"Tired of hiring freelancers who disappear mid-project?"

**"You know what I hate?" hooks** — Blair Warren's "throw rocks at enemies":
"You know what I hate about the [industry]? The way they [common frustration]..."
Instantly bonds through shared frustration.

**Mechanism hooks** — lead with the differentiator (for Solution Aware markets):
"The reason your [approach] isn't working has nothing to do with [obvious thing]..."
"There's a little-known [mechanism] that [specific result]."

## 3. Platform-Specific Character Limits & Specs (2026)

Hard constraints define the playing field. Know these cold — writing a headline at 32 characters
when the limit is 30 wastes everyone's time. These specs reflect current platform limits as of
early 2026.

### Facebook & Instagram Ads

| Element | Character Limit | Best Practice Length | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Primary text | 125 visible (truncates at "See more"), 2,200 max | 40-80 chars above fold for short-form; 800-1,500 for long-form | First line IS the hook — everything above "See more" must earn the click to expand |
| Headline | 40 chars (truncates), 255 max | 27-40 chars | Appears below the image/video. Benefit-driven. |
| Link description | 30 chars (truncates), 255 max | 20-30 chars | Supporting proof or urgency. Often hidden on mobile. |
| Image (single) | — | 1080x1080px (1:1) or 1080x1350px (4:5) | 4:5 takes more vertical space in feed — higher stop rate. Max 20% text overlay (soft rule, no longer hard-enforced but affects delivery). PNG or JPG, under 30MB. |
| Image (carousel) | — | 1080x1080px (1:1), 2-10 cards | Each card gets its own headline and description. Tell a sequential story or stack benefits across cards. |
| Video (feed) | — | 1:1 or 4:5, up to 240 min | Optimal: 15-60 sec. First 3 sec = hook. Add captions — 85% of feed video is watched without sound. .MP4 or .MOV, max 4GB. |
| Reels ad | — | 9:16 (1080x1920), 15-90 sec | Must feel native to Reels. Full-screen vertical. Sound-on optimized. Hook in first 1-2 seconds. |
| Stories ad | — | 9:16 (1080x1920), 5-120 sec | Auto-plays between organic stories. First 3 seconds critical. Swipe-up CTA. |

### Google Search Ads

| Element | Character Limit | Best Practice Length | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Headline (RSA) | 30 chars per headline, up to 15 headlines | 25-30 chars to avoid truncation | Google mixes and matches. Write each headline to work independently and in any combination. |
| Description (RSA) | 90 chars per description, up to 4 descriptions | 80-90 chars | Use all available space. Include keywords, proof, and CTA. |
| Display URL path | 15 chars per path field (2 fields) | Use both fields | Customize to match the search intent: `/free-quote` or `/pricing` |
| Responsive Search Ad (RSA) | Min 3 headlines + 2 descriptions | Provide all 15 headlines + 4 descriptions | Google's algorithm optimizes combinations. More assets = better optimization surface. Pin only when essential — over-pinning reduces performance. |
| Sitelink extension | 25 char link text, 35 char description (x2 lines) | Use all 4-8 sitelinks | Each sitelink adds real estate and click surface. Must point to different pages. |
| Callout extension | 25 chars each, up to 10 callouts | 12-18 chars each | Short proof points: "Free Shipping" / "24/7 Support" / "4.9★ Rating" |
| Structured snippet | 25 chars per value, up to 10 values | Cover 3+ snippet types | Header categories: Brands, Services, Types, Destinations, etc. |

### LinkedIn Ads

| Element | Character Limit | Best Practice Length | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Single image ad — intro text | 150 chars visible (truncates), 600 max | 100-150 chars above fold | Professional tone. Lead with data or results, not hype. |
| Single image ad — headline | 70 chars (truncates), 200 max | 40-70 chars | Appears below the image. Value-proposition driven. |
| Single image ad — description | 100 chars | 70-100 chars | Only shows in Audience Network placements, not in-feed. |
| Single image ad — image | — | 1200x627px (1.91:1) or 1080x1080px (1:1) | 1:1 gets more engagement in-feed. JPG or PNG, max 5MB. |
| Carousel ad | 10 card max, 45 char headline per card | 2-5 cards optimal | Each card: 1080x1080px. Tell a sequential story or break down a framework. |
| Text ad | 25 char headline, 75 char description | Use full limits | Right-rail placement. Low CTR but cheap. |
| Sponsored content (video) | Same text limits as single image | — | Video: MP4, 3 sec-30 min (optimal 15-90 sec), max 200MB. Square or vertical outperforms landscape. |
| Message ad (InMail) | 60 char subject, 1,500 char body | Subject: 30-40 chars, body: 500-800 chars | One CTA button (25 chars). Personal tone — write like a real message, not a blast. |
| Document ad | Same text limits, PDF/PPT/DOC | 5-10 pages optimal | Gate with lead gen form for download. Each page = a slide in-feed. |

### TikTok Ads

| Element | Character Limit | Best Practice Length | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| In-feed ad — display name | 40 chars | Brand name or handle | Must match registered business or brand name. |
| In-feed ad — ad description | 100 chars (including spaces) | 80-100 chars | Text overlay on the video matters more. This is the caption under the video. |
| In-feed ad — CTA button | Pre-set options (Learn More, Shop Now, Download, etc.) | Pick the lowest-friction option matching your goal | "Learn More" has highest CTR for cold traffic. "Shop Now" for warm e-commerce. |
| In-feed video | 9:16 (1080x1920), 5-60 sec | 21-34 sec sweet spot | .MP4 or .MOV, max 500MB. Sound-on platform — music/voiceover required. First 2 sec = hook or die. |
| TopView | 9:16, 5-60 sec | 10-15 sec | First ad users see when opening TikTok. Premium placement. Full-screen, sound-on. |
| Spark Ads | Inherits organic post specs | — | Boost organic posts as ads. Keeps likes/comments/shares. Higher trust signal than standard in-feed. Uses existing creator content. |

### YouTube Ads

| Element | Character Limit | Best Practice Length | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Bumper ad | — | 6 sec max, non-skippable | One message only. Brand awareness or single benefit. 16:9 or 1:1. |
| In-stream skippable | — | 12 sec-3 min (optimal 30-90 sec) | Skip button at 5 sec. First 5 sec must earn the rest. 16:9. |
| In-stream non-skippable | — | 15-20 sec max (varies by region) | 15 sec in most markets, 20 sec in some. Every second must carry weight — no warmup. 16:9. |
| Video discovery ad | 25 char headline, 35 char description (x2 lines) | Use full limits | Thumbnail + text in search results and related videos. Thumbnail is 90% of CTR. |
| Companion banner | — | 300x60px | Appears alongside desktop video ads. Include logo and CTA. |
| CTA overlay | 10 char headline, 15 char CTA | Use full limits | Clickable overlay on video. Must be direct and action-oriented. |
| Video specs (all) | — | Min 1280x720 (720p), recommended 1920x1080 | .MP4, .MOV, .AVI. Max 256GB. Captions recommended — always provide .SRT file. |

## 4. Facebook & Instagram Ad Copy

> *See Section 3 above for current Facebook/Instagram character limits and specs.
> See `psychology.md` Section 14 (Behavioral Economics — Sutherland) for why native-looking
> content outperforms polished ads. See `voice-and-craft.md` Section 2 (The Writing Moves
> Library) for sentence-level techniques that work in compressed ad formats.*

### Format Options
- **Short-form (1-3 lines above the fold):** For warm audiences, simple offers, retargeting.
- **Medium-form (5-10 lines):** For most direct-response ads. Enough room for hook, body, CTA.
- **Long-form (15+ lines):** For cold audiences, complex offers, story-driven ads. The "mini
  sales letter" approach. Sugarman: if the product requires education, give it the space.

### Structure: The Facebook Ad Formula

**Line 1: Hook** — pattern interrupt, callout, or curiosity (this is the "headline" —
everything above "See more")

**Body (above the fold):**
- Agitate the problem OR share a compelling result
- Introduce the mechanism or unique angle
- Build desire with 2-3 specific benefits

**Body (below the fold / after "See more"):**
- Social proof (brief — one result or testimonial)
- Address the #1 objection
- Stack additional benefits or fascination bullets

**CTA:**
- Clear instruction: "Click the link below to [specific benefit]"
- Urgency if truthful: "Spots are filling up" / "Offer ends Friday"

**Creative (Image/Video):**
- The visual must stop the scroll BEFORE the text is read
- Text overlay on images: benefit-driven, large, readable
- Video: hook in the first 1-3 seconds or lose them
- Native-looking content outperforms polished ads (Sutherland's paradox: roughness
  signals authenticity)

### Facebook Ad Copy Formulas

**PAS Ad:**
"Still [painful situation]? (hook)
Here's why [reason it's not getting better]. (agitate)
We built [solution] specifically for [target audience] who want [result]. (solve)
[One line of proof].
Click below to [CTA]."

**Testimonial Ad:**
"[Direct quote from customer — specific result].
[Customer name, title, or description].
Want the same results? Here's how:
[1-2 lines about the offer]
[CTA]"

**Story Ad (Long-form):**
"[Compelling opening moment of the story — start at the climax]
[Brief middle — the struggle or discovery]
[The result — specific and measurable]
[Bridge: what made it possible]
[Mechanism: why this approach is different]
[Bridge to the offer]
[CTA]"

**Education Ad (Holmes' Stadium Pitch approach):**
"[Surprising fact or statistic about their industry]
Most [audience] don't realize [counterintuitive insight].
Here's what's actually happening: [brief education]
We put together a [free resource/webinar/guide] that shows you [specific outcome].
[CTA]"

## 5. Google Search Ads (PPC)

Google ads are fundamentally different: the prospect is actively searching. They have
intent. Your job is to match that intent better than any other result on the page.

> *See Section 3 above for current Google Search character limits and RSA specs.
> See `frameworks.md` Section 18 (Hopkins' Rules of Scientific Advertising) — Hopkins
> wrote the book on search-intent copywriting before Google existed. His principles of
> specificity and testing apply directly to PPC.*

### Principles
- **Intent matching is everything.** Your ad must mirror the searcher's intent in language
  and specificity. Hopkins: "The best ads answer a want."
- **Keywords in headlines.** When the searcher sees their exact query reflected in your
  headline, click-through rate increases dramatically.
- **Specificity wins.** "Get 3 Free Quotes in 60 Seconds" beats "Get a Free Quote."
  Hopkins: "Generalities roll off the human understanding like water off a duck."
- **Differentiate from organic results.** Make yours clearly different and more compelling.

### Google Ad Structure
**Headline 1 (30 chars):** Primary benefit or keyword match
**Headline 2 (30 chars):** Secondary benefit, differentiator, or proof
**Headline 3 (30 chars):** CTA or urgency element
**Description 1 (90 chars):** Expand on the promise. Include a specific benefit.
**Description 2 (90 chars):** Social proof, guarantee, or objection handling.

### Google Ad Formulas

**Benefit + Proof + CTA:**
H1: "Lose 20 Lbs in 90 Days"
H2: "3,400+ Success Stories"
H3: "Start Your Free Trial Today"
D1: "Science-backed program designed for busy professionals. No extreme diets."
D2: "Join 3,400+ people who've transformed their health. 30-day money-back guarantee."

**Problem + Solution + Differentiator:**
H1: "Struggling to Hire Engineers?"
H2: "Vetted Devs in 48 Hours"
H3: "Risk-Free Trial Period"
D1: "Stop wasting months on bad hires. We pre-screen senior engineers so you don't have to."
D2: "Trusted by 200+ startups. Replace any hire free within 90 days."

## 6. LinkedIn Ad Copy

LinkedIn audiences are professional, time-starved, and skeptical of being sold to.

> *See Section 3 above for current LinkedIn character limits and ad format specs.
> See `psychology.md` Section 14 (Behavioral Economics — Sutherland) for the "nobody got
> fired for buying IBM" dynamic that governs B2B purchase psychology on LinkedIn.*

### Principles
- Lead with professional credibility, data, and specific results
- Avoid hype language — it triggers skepticism in professional contexts
- Frame the CTA as an investment in professional growth, not a purchase
- Case studies and data outperform emotional appeals on LinkedIn
- Sutherland's insight: in professional contexts, people buy to avoid criticism ("nobody
  ever got fired for buying IBM"). Reduce perceived risk of choosing you.

### LinkedIn Ad Formula
"[Specific result] — here's how [company/person like them] did it:
[2-3 bullet points of the method or insight]
[One line of proof/credibility]
[CTA: Download the guide / Join the webinar / Book a demo]"

### Thought-Leadership Ad (for B2B)
"I've spent [X years/months] studying [topic]. Here are [number] things I wish someone
told me earlier:
[2-3 counterintuitive insights — value-first]
Want the full breakdown? [CTA to content/resource]"

## 7. TikTok & Short-Form Video Ad Scripts

TikTok ads must feel native to the platform. Polished, corporate-looking ads get skipped.
Sutherland's paradox in action: imperfection signals authenticity.

> *See Section 3 above for current TikTok ad specs and character limits.
> See `voice-and-craft.md` Section 1 (The Five Master Voices) — TikTok demands the
> "Conversational" or "Provocateur" voice. Corporate or Authority voice kills performance.
> See `psychology.md` Section 2 (Mass Desire — Schwartz) for Camouflage technique.*

### Principles
- Hook in the first 1-2 seconds — visual or verbal
- Feel like organic content, not an ad (Schwartz's Camouflage)
- Talk TO the viewer, not AT them
- Fast pacing — new visual or info every 3-5 seconds
- Text overlays reinforce key points
- CTA feels like a natural next step

### Script Structure (15-60 seconds)

**0-2 sec: Hook**
Visual pattern interrupt + verbal hook. "POV: You just found out [surprising thing]"
or "Stop scrolling if you [qualify audience]"

**2-10 sec: Problem/Setup**
Quickly establish the relatable situation or pain point. Use their language.

**10-25 sec: Solution/Mechanism**
Show or explain what makes this different. Keep it fast, visual, engaging.

**25-40 sec: Proof**
Quick result, testimonial clip, before/after, or demonstration

**40-60 sec: CTA**
"Link in bio" / "Comment [word] for details" / "Click the link below"

## 8. YouTube Ad Scripts

> *See Section 3 above for current YouTube ad specs (bumper, skippable, non-skippable).
> See `frameworks.md` Section 1 (Core Copy Frameworks) for PAS and AIDA structures that
> compress into the 30-60 second YouTube ad format. See `voice-and-craft.md` Section 4
> (The Art of Transitions and Flow) for maintaining momentum across a video script.*

### Pre-Roll (Skippable — 5 Second Rule)
The first 5 seconds must earn the rest. After 5 seconds, they can skip. Your hook must
work in this window.

**5-Second Hook Framework:**
"If you're a [specific audience] who [specific situation], do NOT skip this ad."
"[Shocking statement or statistic] — and here's what it means for you."
"This [product/method] just [impressive result]. Here's what happened..."

### Structure for 30-60 Second YouTube Ads
1. **0-5 sec:** Hook that earns the skip
2. **5-15 sec:** Problem agitation (relatable, specific)
3. **15-30 sec:** Mechanism/solution (what makes this different)
4. **30-45 sec:** Proof (one powerful result or testimonial)
5. **45-60 sec:** CTA with urgency

### Long-Form YouTube Ads (2-5 minutes)
Follow the VSL structure in compressed form. These work for cold traffic where the
product requires education. Lead with story, educate through mechanism, prove with
results, close with offer.

## 9. Retargeting Ad Copy

These people have already visited your site, watched your content, or engaged with your ads.
They know who you are. The copy strategy shifts completely.

> *See `psychology.md` Section 1 (Five Levels of Awareness) — retargeting audiences are
> Product Aware or Most Aware. The copy strategy must match. See `psychology.md` Section 8
> (Loss Aversion — Kahneman/Tversky) for why scarcity and urgency work on warm audiences.
> See `frameworks.md` Section 9 (Guarantee / Risk Reversal Frameworks) for objection-handling
> structures that power retargeting ads.*

### Retargeting Principles
- Don't re-introduce yourself. Get to the point.
- Address the likely reason they didn't convert the first time
- Add NEW information: new proof, new angle, new offer, new urgency
- Shorter copy is often better — they have context already
- Use the awareness level: they're now Product Aware or Most Aware

### Retargeting Ad Angles
- **Objection handling:** "Worried about [common objection]? Here's why you shouldn't be..."
- **Social proof:** "See why [number] people chose [product] this month alone"
- **Urgency/scarcity:** "The offer you saw earlier? It expires in 24 hours."
- **Testimonial:** Feature a specific, relatable success story
- **FAQ:** Address the #1 question people have before buying
- **Direct offer:** Special pricing or bonus for returning visitors
- **The "breakup":** "This is the last time we'll show you this. If [product] isn't for you,
  no hard feelings. But if [pain point] is still bothering you..."

## 10. Advertorial Copy

The advertorial is Schwartz's Camouflage technique in its purest form — copy that looks,
reads, and feels like editorial content rather than advertising.

> *See `psychology.md` Section 2 (Mass Desire — Schwartz) for the full Camouflage technique.
> See `voice-and-craft.md` Section 9 (Market-Specific Craft Adjustments) for matching editorial
> tone by vertical. See `frameworks.md` Section 2 (Lead Types — Great Leads) — advertorials
> typically use Story, Secret, or Proclamation lead types.*

### Principles
- Match the look and feel of the publication (digital or print)
- Lead with education, insight, or story — not with the product
- Use the format of a news article, research report, or feature story
- The product enters naturally as part of the story, not as a hard pivot
- Include genuine value — the reader should learn something real even if they don't buy
- Comply with advertising disclosure requirements (label as sponsored/advertorial)

### Structure
1. **Headline:** News-style or curiosity-driven (not ad-style)
2. **Lead:** Story or surprising finding that hooks the reader
3. **Body:** Education, research, case study — genuine value
4. **Bridge:** Natural transition from the topic to the product
5. **Product section:** How it works, proof, results
6. **CTA:** Soft or embedded ("Learn more about [product] here")

### When to Use Advertorials
- Cold traffic in sophisticated markets (Stage 4-5)
- Complex products that require education before selling
- Audiences that are ad-blind or ad-resistant
- When you need to shift beliefs before presenting the offer
- As an intermediate step between an ad and a sales page (ad → advertorial → sales page)

## 11. Ad Performance Benchmarks by Platform

Numbers without context are meaningless. "We got a 2% CTR" means nothing until you know
that's 3x the platform median for cold traffic in your vertical. These benchmarks give you
the context to evaluate performance honestly.

> *See `data-analysis` skill → `references/metrics-frameworks.md` for OMTM selection and
> guardrail metric design that prevents optimizing CTR at the expense of conversion quality.
> See `data-analysis` skill → `references/experimentation.md` for statistical significance
> thresholds — do not call a winner until you have 95%+ confidence.*

### How to Read These Tables
- **Good** = performing above the platform median. You're not wasting money.
- **Great** = top 25% of advertisers. Your copy and targeting are working.
- **Elite** = top 5-10% of advertisers. Your entire system (copy + creative + targeting + landing page + offer) is dialed in.
- All ranges assume standard campaign structures with proper pixel/tracking setup.
- Ranges vary by geography, seasonality, and offer price point. Use these as calibration, not gospel.

### Facebook & Instagram Benchmarks

| Vertical | CTR (Good / Great / Elite) | CPC (Good / Great / Elite) | CVR (Good / Great / Elite) |
|---|---|---|---|
| SaaS (B2B) | 0.8-1.2% / 1.2-2.0% / 2.0%+ | $2.50-4.00 / $1.50-2.50 / <$1.50 | 2-4% / 4-7% / 7%+ |
| SaaS (B2C) | 1.0-1.5% / 1.5-2.5% / 2.5%+ | $1.50-3.00 / $0.80-1.50 / <$0.80 | 3-5% / 5-9% / 9%+ |

| Local services | 0.8-1.3% / 1.3-2.0% / 2.0%+ | $1.50-3.50 / $0.80-1.50 / <$0.80 | 3-6% / 6-10% / 10%+ |

Never write an ad without a brief. Filling this out forces strategic clarity and prevents the
#1 amateur mistake: writing copy before understanding who you're talking to, where they are
in the awareness journey, and what you're actually trying to get them to do.

### Brief Usage Rules

1. **No blank fields.** If you don't know the answer, you haven't done enough research.

2. **Awareness level drives everything.** If you get this wrong, every word you write will
   miss. When in doubt, assume LOWER awareness than you think — it's safer to over-educate
   than to assume knowledge the prospect doesn't have.
3. **Message match is non-negotiable.** An elite ad driving traffic to a mismatched landing
   page is worse than a mediocre ad driving to a matched page. Check the alignment section
   honestly.
4. **Fill out one brief per audience temperature.** Cold, warm, and hot traffic need separate
   briefs, separate ads, separate landing pages. If you're writing one ad for all three,
   you're doing it wrong.

## 13. AI-Assisted Ad Production Workflow

AI doesn't replace the strategist — it replaces the blank page. The workflow below uses Claude
to produce ad variants at scale while maintaining the strategic precision this reference demands.
The key insight: AI is excellent at generating volume and variation within tight strategic
constraints. It is terrible at setting those constraints itself.

> *See `ai-prompting-workflows` skill → `references/workflow-playbooks.md` Section 3 (Content
> Generation Pipeline) for the full technical architecture, cost breakdowns, and system prompts.
> See `ai-prompting-workflows` skill → `references/prompt-craft.md` for prompt structure principles
> that make Claude's ad output dramatically better.
> See `ai-prompting-workflows` skill → `references/evaluation-testing.md` for building eval
> rubrics that score ad variants programmatically.*

### The Five-Step AI Ad Production Process

**Step 1: Human — Complete the Ad Copy Brief (Section 12)**

This is the non-negotiable human step. The brief IS the prompt constraint. Without it, you're
asking AI to guess at strategy, and it will produce generic, awareness-mismatched, platform-
inappropriate copy. Fill out every field. The brief becomes the core of your Claude prompt.

**Step 2: Human → Claude — Generate the Strategy Layer**

Before generating ad copy, ask Claude to output the strategic frame. This catches misalignment
before you waste tokens on bad copy.

Prompt pattern:
```
Given this ad brief [paste completed brief], generate a strategic analysis:

1. Confirm the awareness level and recommend the appropriate lead type
   (per Schwartz's framework)
2. Identify the 3 strongest hook angles for this audience/platform
   combination — rank by likely scroll-stopping power
3. Identify the primary emotional trigger (per Sugarman's 31 triggers)
   that aligns with this audience's desire/pain
4. Flag any message-match risks between the ad strategy and the
   landing page awareness level
5. Recommend ad length (short/medium/long-form) for this platform
   and audience temperature

Do NOT write ad copy yet. Strategy only.
```

Review the strategy output. Correct anything misaligned. This 60-second review prevents
hours of wasted variant generation.

> *See `psychology.md` Section 6 (Sugarman's 31 Psychological Triggers) for the trigger
> taxonomy Claude should reference. See `frameworks.md` Section 2 (Lead Types) for lead type
> selection criteria.*

**Step 3: Claude — Generate Variant Batch**

Now generate the volume. The goal is 8-12 variants per hook angle, across 2-3 hook angles.
That gives you 16-36 total variants to select from.

Prompt pattern:
```
Using the approved strategy [paste strategy output from Step 2] and
the ad brief [paste brief], generate ad copy variants.

Platform: [platform]
Format: [format from brief]
Character limits: Primary text [X], Headline [X], Description [X]

Generate exactly [N] variants using this structure:
- Hook angle: [angle from strategy]
- Each variant must use a DIFFERENT opening line
- Each variant must include: hook, body, CTA
- Stay within character limits — count characters precisely
- Match the voice calibration: [reference voice-and-craft.md tone]
- Awareness level: [level] — do NOT assume knowledge the prospect
  doesn't have at this level

For each variant, output:
1. Primary text (with character count)
2. Headline (with character count)
3. Description (with character count)
4. Hook type used (from the hook categories in Section 2)
5. Emotional trigger deployed
```

**Variant volume guide:**
- Testing new campaign (no control): Generate 24-36 variants across 3 hook angles.
  Select the top 6-8 to launch.
- Optimizing existing control: Generate 12-16 variants of the winning hook angle
  with body/CTA variations. Select 4-6 challengers.
- Fighting creative fatigue: Generate 16-24 variants across 2 new hook angles.
  These are fresh angles, not rewrites of the fatigued creative.

> *See `voice-and-craft.md` Section 1 (The Five Master Voices) for voice calibration
> instructions to include in your prompt. The voice constraint is the difference between
> AI copy that reads like AI and AI copy that reads like a specific, intentional voice.*

**Step 4: Human — Select, Edit, and Approve**

AI generates. Humans curate. This is where craft happens.

Review process:
1. **First pass — kill the obvious failures.** Anything that misses the awareness level,
   violates platform norms, or reads generically gets cut. This should eliminate 40-60%
   of variants.
2. **Second pass — rank survivors.** Score each remaining variant on: hook strength (1-5),
   message-market match (1-5), clarity of CTA (1-5), voice authenticity (1-5). Total
   score out of 20.
3. **Third pass — edit the top performers.** Take the top 6-8 variants and apply human
   craft. Sharpen the hook. Tighten the rhythm. Add specificity from your VOC research.
   Replace generic proof with real proof. This is where the copywriter earns their fee —
   the AI gave you the clay, you sculpt.
4. **Final check — message match.** Read each finalist ad, then immediately look at the
   landing page. Does the promise match? Does the tone match? Does the awareness level
   match? If not, fix the ad or fix the page. Launching a mismatched pair is worse than
   not launching at all.

**When to reject ALL AI output and write from scratch:**
- When every variant feels generically "AI-ish" despite good prompting — the brief was
  probably too vague. Go back to Step 1.
- When the product/offer is genuinely novel and has no close analogues in training data.
- When the target audience has a highly specific voice/dialect that AI can't replicate
  from a prompt alone (use VOC mining output from `research-market-analysis` skill as
  direct input to fix this).

**Step 5: Structure the A/B Test**

> *See `data-analysis` skill → `references/experimentation.md` for full statistical rigor
> on test design, sample size calculations, and OEC selection.*

The variants you approved in Step 4 become your test matrix. Structure it correctly:

**Test hierarchy (most to least impact — test in this order):**
1. Hook angle vs. hook angle (different angles from Step 2)
2. Hook wording within the winning angle (different opening lines)
3. Body copy variations (proof type, length, structure)
4. CTA variations (wording, urgency, offer framing)
5. Creative variations (image vs. video, different visuals)

**Test structure per round:**
- Run 2-4 variants simultaneously (not more — you need statistical volume per variant)
- Minimum spend per variant before making a call: $50-100 for Facebook/TikTok,
  $100-200 for LinkedIn, $30-50 per ad group for Google
- Minimum timeframe: 3-5 days (captures day-of-week variation)
- Winner criteria: Define BEFORE launching. Usually: lowest CPA at acceptable volume.
  Not CTR alone. Not CPC alone. CPA or ROAS.
- When a winner emerges: it becomes the control. Generate new challengers using Step 3
  with the winning hook angle as the constraint. Repeat indefinitely.

### AI vs. Human Decision Matrix

| Task | AI | Human | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| Filling out the ad brief | No | Yes | Strategy requires market knowledge AI doesn't have |
| VOC research / language mining | Assist | Lead | AI can process volume; human identifies signal in noise |
| Hook angle ideation | Yes | Review | AI generates breadth; human judges market fit |
| Variant generation | Yes | Edit | AI handles volume; human adds craft and specificity |
| Character count compliance | Yes | Verify | AI counts; human spots truncation edge cases |
| Selecting test winners | No | Yes | Requires contextual judgment about business goals |
| Analyzing test results | Assist | Decide | AI can crunch numbers; human interprets business meaning |
| Writing the "breakthrough" ad | No | Yes | The ad that 10x's performance comes from human insight, not AI iteration |

### Cost Per Variant (using Claude)

Using the pipeline from `ai-prompting-workflows` → `workflow-playbooks.md` Section 3:

| Step | Model | Cost per batch (24 variants) |
|---|---|---|
| Strategy generation | Sonnet + extended thinking | $0.04-0.08 |
| Variant generation | Sonnet (cached system prompt) | $0.06-0.12 |
| Quality scoring (automated) | Haiku | $0.01-0.02 |
| Revision pass (40% of variants) | Sonnet | $0.03-0.06 |
| **Total per batch** | | **$0.14-0.28** |

At $0.14-0.28 per batch of 24 variants, AI-assisted production costs less than 2 cents per
variant. A human copywriter producing 24 quality variants manually would take 4-8 hours.
The economics are obvious — but only if the human strategy layer (Steps 1, 4, 5) is done
properly. Garbage strategy in, garbage variants out, no matter how cheap.

## 14. Testing & Iteration (Hopkins Method)

> *See `data-analysis` skill → `references/experimentation.md` for statistically rigorous
> test design (sample sizes, significance thresholds, OEC definition). See `frameworks.md`
> Section 18 (Hopkins' Rules of Scientific Advertising) for the foundational testing philosophy.
> See Section 13 (AI-Assisted Ad Production Workflow) above for how to generate the variant
> volume that testing demands.*

The best copywriters test relentlessly. Hopkins: "Almost any question can be answered,
cheaply, quickly and finally, by a test campaign. And that's the way to answer them —
not by arguments around a table. Go to the court of last resort — the buyers of your
product."

### What to Test (in order of impact)
1. **Hook / Headline** — this has the biggest impact on performance
2. **Offer** — what you're selling and how you frame it
3. **Audience / targeting** — who sees it
4. **CTA** — wording, placement, urgency level
5. **Body copy** — different angles, lengths, proof elements
6. **Creative** — image vs. video, different visuals, native vs. polished

### Testing Rules
- Test ONE variable at a time (Hopkins' foundational rule)
- Let tests run long enough for statistical significance
- The market's response is truth — not your opinion or the client's opinion
- Keep a swipe file of winners and losers with notes on WHY they performed
- Caples: "The only way to know if your advertising works is to test it."
- Test big changes first (new hook, new angle, new offer), then refine small changes
  (word choice, formatting, creative variations)

### The Control System
- Your best-performing ad is the "control"
- Every new ad is tested AGAINST the control
- When a new ad beats the control, it becomes the new control
- This creates a continuous improvement cycle
- Never stop testing — markets shift, audiences change, creative fatigues

## 15. Common Failures and Fixes

| Failure | Fix |
|---|---|
| Low click-through rate | Hook isn't stopping the scroll. Test 5+ new hooks. Use the hook categories above. |
| Clicks but no conversions | Ad-to-landing-page disconnect. Ensure message match (same promise, same language, same visual tone). |
| High cost per click | Ad isn't relevant enough to audience. Tighten targeting or improve hook relevance. |
| Ad fatigue (declining performance) | Rotate creatives every 2-4 weeks. Test new angles, not just new creatives. |
| Reads like an ad | Make it feel like content. Native language, conversational, value-first. Use Camouflage. |
| Too clever, not clear | Clarity beats cleverness. State the benefit plainly. Hopkins: "Don't try to be amusing." |
| No social proof in ad | Even one specific result ("helped [company] increase leads by 34%") dramatically improves credibility. |
| CTA is vague | "Learn more" is weak. "Get your free [specific thing]" is strong. Be specific about what happens when they click. |
| Only targeting the 3% | Use education and curiosity-based ads to reach the 90% who aren't ready to buy yet (Holmes' Buyer's Pyramid). |
| Same angle for all audiences | Cold, warm, and hot traffic need completely different hooks, angles, and CTAs. Segment your copy strategy. |
