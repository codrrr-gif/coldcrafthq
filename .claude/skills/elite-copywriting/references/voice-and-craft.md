> **Cross-references:** This file is the craft layer — it tells you HOW to write. For the
> psychological engines behind WHY these techniques work, see `psychology.md`. For the
> structural formulas and frameworks that determine WHAT to write, see `frameworks.md`.

The other reference files tell you WHAT to write. This one tells you HOW to write it.

Strategy without craft is a skeleton without flesh. The frameworks give you structure —
awareness levels, lead types, belief gates, offer stacks. But the reader doesn't experience
your framework. They experience your WORDS. Your rhythm. Your voice. The way your sentences
feel in their chest.

This file is the craft layer. The sentence-level moves, the voice calibration, the writing
techniques that separate copy that follows the right strategy but reads flat from copy that
follows the right strategy and makes people feel something so strongly they have to act.

## 1. The Five Master Voices — How to Calibrate Tone

Every piece of copy needs a voice. Not just "conversational" — that's too vague.
Voice is the specific personality behind the words. It determines word choice, sentence
length, rhythm, humor, intensity, and the reader's emotional experience.

Before writing, choose a voice register — or blend elements from multiple. The voice
should match the product's personality (Sugarman's Axiom 10), the audience's expectations,
and the brand's character.

### The Halbert Voice — The Conspiratorial Friend
**Sounds like:** A smart, slightly reckless friend who's had two drinks and is leaning
across the table to tell you something he probably shouldn't.

**Characteristics:**
- Irreverent, self-deprecating, conspiratorial
- Uses slang, fragments, dashes, ellipses
- Breaks "rules" of grammar constantly and on purpose
- Addresses the reader like a buddy, not a customer
- Self-deprecation disarms — "I'm just a desperate nerd from Ohio"
- Profanity or near-profanity when the audience allows it
- Long paragraphs that feel like a friend rambling but are actually tightly structured

**When to use:** Info products, personality-driven brands, markets that are jaded and
ad-resistant, long-form sales letters where you need to hold attention through personality.

**How to write it:**
- Start sentences with "Look," "Here's the thing," "Listen," "Now,"
- Use parenthetical asides (like this one) to create intimacy
- Admit flaws, mistakes, and embarrassing moments before making claims
- Write like you're talking, not like you're writing
- Use "you" and "I" constantly — it's a conversation between two people
- Let your real opinions show. Don't be balanced. Be opinionated.

**Example:**
"Look, I'm going to be straight with you. Most of what you've been told about
building an email list is garbage. I know because I believed it too — for three
years I followed the 'experts' and my list grew by about 12 people a month.
Twelve. My mom was three of them."

### The Sugarman Voice — The Curious Storyteller
**Sounds like:** Someone who's genuinely fascinated by what they're describing and can't
help but pull you into their fascination.

**Characteristics:**
- Meandering, personal, unhurried
- Conversational but more polished than Halbert
- Draws you in through genuine curiosity and wonder
- Uses sensory details lavishly
- Creates the "slippery slide" through sheer readability
- Never seems to be selling — seems to be sharing an experience
- Tangents that seem random but always circle back to the point

**When to use:** Product-focused copy where the product itself is interesting, consumer
products, technology, anything where you can create sensory mental ownership.

**How to write it:**
- Describe your personal experience with the product in real-time
- Use sensory language: what you saw, felt, heard, tasted, smelled
- Take your time. Don't rush to the pitch. The journey IS the pitch.
- Use "I" a lot — this is personal storytelling
- Let the reader experience the product through your narration
- Embed selling points inside the story so they don't feel like selling points

**Example:**
"I put on the sunglasses and looked out at the pool. The first thing I noticed was
the sky. It wasn't just blue — it was a blue I'd never seen before. Deep, rich,
almost electric. The clouds had edges so crisp they looked painted. I turned to look
at the palm trees and the green was so vivid it almost hurt. I took the glasses off.
The world went flat again. I put them back on. There it was — that world."

### The Ogilvy Voice — The Authoritative Expert
**Sounds like:** A brilliant, well-read person who respects your intelligence too much to
exaggerate or hype.

**Characteristics:**
- Factual, understated, precise
- Lets specificity do the persuasion work
- Never raises its voice — quiet confidence
- Uses longer, well-constructed sentences
- Avoids superlatives and hype language entirely
- Conveys authority through knowledge, not volume
- Elegance and restraint signal quality

**When to use:** Luxury brands, premium products, B2B, sophisticated audiences,
professional services, anything where restraint signals quality.

**How to write it:**
- State facts. Let them speak for themselves.
- Use specific numbers and details instead of adjectives
- Never use "amazing," "incredible," "revolutionary" — show, don't inflate
- Write in complete, polished sentences (no fragments)
- One vivid detail is worth more than ten adjectives
- Understate rather than overstate — the reader's imagination fills the gap
- Trust the reader to draw the right conclusion

**Example:**
"At 60 miles an hour the loudest noise in this new Rolls-Royce comes from the
electric clock. The coachwork is given five coats of primer paint and hand-rubbed
between each coat. The finished car is inspected by 2,000 separate quality checks.
Every engine is run for seven hours at full throttle before installation."

### The Carlton Voice — The Vivid Provocateur
**Sounds like:** A carnival barker who actually delivers, crossed with a pulp fiction
writer who knows how to sell.

**Characteristics:**
- Punchy, vivid, slightly over-the-top but earned
- Uses the most extreme, specific, visual image possible
- Short sentences. Staccato rhythm. Then a longer one that builds.
- Headlines that are physically impossible to ignore
- Fascination bullets that create almost painful curiosity
- Shieldbreaker technique: names your skepticism before you think it
- Unapologetically bold

**When to use:** Direct response, health/fitness, self-improvement, any market where
boldness is rewarded, fascination-heavy copy, markets that respond to vivid imagery.

**How to write it:**
- Find the most unusual, specific, visual detail about the product or result
- Make it the headline. Make it impossible to skip.
- Use physical, concrete language — never abstract
- Write short paragraphs. One sentence is fine. Two is normal.
- Punch the reader in the gut with specificity, then pull them close with a story
- Anticipate objections and demolish them INSIDE the narrative
- Never be boring. If a sentence is boring, cut it or make it vivid.

**Example:**
"Amazing Secret Discovered by One-Legged Golfer Adds 50 Yards to Your Drives,
Eliminates Hooks and Slices... And Can Slash Up to 10 Strokes From Your Game
Almost Overnight!"

### The Settle Voice — The Polarizing Entertainer
**Sounds like:** A friend who emails you every day with stories, opinions, and a link to
something he's selling — and you love it because he's never boring.

**Characteristics:**
- Opinionated, polarizing, story-driven
- Every email opens with a random story that bridges to a selling point
- Unapologetically sells in every communication
- Short emails (300-500 words typically)
- The personality IS the value — you read for the voice, not the info
- Takes stands that repel some and magnetically attract others
- Humor that's dry, sometimes cutting, always authentic

**When to use:** Daily email, ongoing relationship marketing, personality-driven brands,
subscription models, info products, coaching, any context where YOU are the brand.

**How to write it:**
- Start with whatever happened to you today (a movie, a conversation, an annoyance)
- Find the analogy to your market, your product, or a lesson
- Bridge to the pitch naturally — one sentence is enough
- End with a link and a P.S.
- Have opinions. Strong ones. Share them. Let the wrong people leave.
- Write fast. 15-30 minutes per email. Speed produces authenticity.
- Don't worry about "providing value" — being interesting IS value

**Example:**
"Yesterday I watched my neighbor spend 45 minutes trying to parallel park his
brand new BMW. Back and forth. Back and forth. Almost hit the fire hydrant twice.
His wife was shouting directions from the sidewalk. The whole block was watching.

Here's the thing: that BMW has a self-parking feature. Built in. Paid for. Ready
to use.

He just never read the manual.

This is exactly what happens when people buy my [product] and don't go through
Module 1. They have the tool. They just don't know how to use it.

Don't be my neighbor.

[Link]

P.S. — His wife is still not speaking to him."

### Blending Voices
Most great copy blends elements. You might use:
- Halbert's intimacy + Ogilvy's specificity
- Sugarman's storytelling + Carlton's vivid imagery
- Settle's opinions + Halbert's self-deprecation

The key is intentionality. Choose the blend BEFORE you write. Don't drift into a voice
by default — select it based on the audience, the product, and the awareness level.

**General rule:**
- Cold audiences / low awareness → Sugarman or Halbert (story-driven, indirect)
- Warm audiences / solution aware → Carlton or blended (mechanism-driven, vivid)
- Hot audiences / most aware → Ogilvy or direct (factual, concise)
- Daily relationship copy → Settle (personality-driven)
- Premium / professional → Ogilvy (understated authority)

## 2. Voice Selection Decision Matrix

The Five Master Voices above give you the palette. This matrix tells you which color to reach
for. Before writing, run the brief through this decision matrix to select the right voice
register — or the right blend — for the specific situation.

> **Cross-reference:** The Brand Voice skill (`brand-voice/references/voice-identity.md`)
> covers systematic brand-level voice architecture — archetypes, verbal identity systems,
> and voice/tone matrices. Use this matrix for campaign-level and piece-level voice selection.
> Use the Brand Voice skill when building a durable voice system for an entire brand.

### The Matrix

| Voice Register | Best For (Product / Context) | Price Point | Audience Profile | Awareness Level Match | Platform Match |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Halbert** — Conspiratorial Friend | Info products, coaching, personality brands, "guru" markets, markets jaded by polished marketing | $47–$2,000 | Skeptical, ad-resistant, internet-savvy, values authenticity over polish | Unaware → Problem Aware (story-driven indirection works best when you need to earn trust before selling) | Long-form sales letters, email sequences, advertorials, direct mail, VSLs |
| **Sugarman** — Curious Storyteller | Consumer products, technology, physical goods, SaaS with a compelling UX, anything where the product experience itself is interesting | $20–$500 (consumer); any price if product is genuinely fascinating | Curious, experience-oriented, appreciates craft and detail, moderate sophistication | Solution Aware → Problem Aware (product experience creates desire without direct claims) | Magazine-style ads, long-form landing pages, product pages, advertorials, email sequences |
| **Ogilvy** — Authoritative Expert | Luxury brands, premium services, B2B/SaaS, professional services, financial services, anything where restraint signals quality | $500+ (or premium positioning at any price) | Educated, high-income, analytical, appreciates intelligence and substance over flash | Product Aware → Most Aware (factual specificity converts ready buyers; understated authority justifies premium) | Print ads, B2B landing pages, LinkedIn content, white papers, proposal copy, brand campaigns |
| **Carlton** — Vivid Provocateur | Health/fitness, golf/sports, self-improvement, direct response offers, any market that rewards bold specificity and fascination | $37–$297 (sweet spot for impulse-meets-value) | Results-driven, action-oriented, responds to vivid imagery and specific promises, values proof over polish | Solution Aware → Problem Aware (mechanism-driven, fascination-heavy copy breaks through noise) | Sales letters, Facebook/Instagram ads, VSLs, fascination bullet sections, headlines, email subject lines |
| **Settle** — Polarizing Entertainer | Daily email, newsletters, subscription models, coaching, info products, any business where YOU are the brand and ongoing relationship is the asset | Any (the relationship drives LTV regardless of individual price point) | Personality-seekers, loyal followers, values entertainment and opinion, wants to feel like an insider | Most Aware → Product Aware (relationship already established; daily touchpoints maintain it) | Email (daily/frequent), social media posts, short-form content, SMS, community posts |

### How to Use the Matrix

**Step 1: Identify the brief's constraints.** What's the product type, price point, audience
profile, and awareness level? Check `psychology.md` Section 1 for Schwartz's awareness
spectrum if you need to diagnose awareness.

**Step 2: Find the row that matches the most constraints.** Perfect matches are rare —
look for the row with the strongest overlap across all columns.

**Step 3: Consider blending.** If two rows match equally well, blend those voices. Common
high-performance blends:
- **Halbert + Ogilvy:** Intimate and specific. Works for premium info products and high-ticket
  coaching where you need personality AND credibility.
- **Sugarman + Carlton:** Vivid storytelling meets punchy fascination. Works for physical
  products in competitive markets where you need to paint the experience AND create urgency.
- **Settle + Halbert:** Pure personality-driven selling. Works for daily email where you need
  to be entertaining AND conspiratorial. The default blend for email-first businesses.
- **Ogilvy + Sugarman:** Authoritative storytelling. Works for premium consumer products
  where you need factual credibility AND sensory engagement. Think high-end tech, luxury
  consumer goods.

**Step 4: Validate against the awareness level.** The awareness column is the tiebreaker.
If two voices seem equally appropriate but serve different awareness levels, choose the one
that matches where your prospect actually IS — not where you wish they were. See
`psychology.md` Section 1 for the full awareness spectrum and `frameworks.md` Section 2
for how lead types map to awareness levels.

**Step 5: Write a voice brief.** Before drafting, write 2-3 sentences describing the
specific voice you're targeting. Example: "Halbert warmth with Ogilvy specificity. Intimate,
self-deprecating opening that earns trust, then shifts to precise, fact-heavy proof. Reads
like a smart friend who's done the homework." This voice brief becomes your anchor — return
to it whenever the draft starts drifting.

> **Brand Voice integration:** When writing for an established brand, the Brand Voice skill's
> archetype and verbal identity system takes precedence for the brand's VOICE (the consistent
> personality). This matrix then helps you calibrate the TONE for the specific piece — which
> craft register to dial up or down within the brand's established voice. Think of the Brand
> Voice skill as the constitution; this matrix as the tactical field manual.

## 3. The Writing Moves Library — 30 Sentence-Level Techniques

These are specific craft moves you can deploy at any point in your copy. Each one is
a tool in the belt. Knowing WHEN to use each is as important as knowing how.

> **Cross-reference:** See `case-studies.md` for these techniques demonstrated in legendary
> promotions — the Specificity Bomb in Ogilvy's Rolls-Royce ad, the Honesty Disarm in
> Karbo's "Lazy Man" letter, Scenario Painting in Hopkins' Pepsodent campaign. See
> `psychology.md` Section 6 for Sugarman's 31 Psychological Triggers that underpin many
> of these moves.

### Momentum Moves (Keep Them Reading)
> *See `case-studies.md` Section 4 (BluBlocker) for the Slippery Slide in action, and
> Section 1 (WSJ) for how seeds of curiosity sustain a 2-billion-dollar letter.*

**1. The Cliffhanger Cut**
End a paragraph or section in the middle of a thought, forcing the reader forward.
"I stared at the screen. The number kept climbing. And then I saw something that
made my stomach drop—"
(New paragraph or section continues.)

**2. The Whisper**
After building intensity, drop suddenly to a quiet, intimate, confessional tone.
The contrast is magnetic.
"And here's the part nobody talks about."
"Can I tell you something honestly?"
"But there's a quieter truth underneath all of this."

**3. The Seed of Curiosity (Sugarman)**
Plant a teaser that creates a micro-loop of curiosity bridging to the next section.
"But that's not even the best part."
"I'll show you how in a moment — but first, you need to understand something."
"The reason will surprise you."

**4. The One-Word Paragraph**
A single word. Standing alone. Creates rhythm and emphasis.
"Wrong."
"Everything."
"Finally."

**5. The Cascade**
A series of short, parallel phrases that build momentum like waves:
"More clients. More revenue. More freedom. Less stress. Less guessing. Less wondering
if this is ever going to work."

**6. The Long Roll**
One long, flowing sentence that builds and builds, layering clause upon clause, pulling
the reader forward through rhythm and momentum until the sentence itself becomes a
kind of persuasion — because by the time they reach the period, they've been carried
so far they can't turn back.
Use sparingly. One per page maximum. It should feel like a wave cresting.

### Credibility Moves (Make Them Believe)
> *See `psychology.md` Section 4 (Cialdini) for the persuasion principles behind these
> moves, and `case-studies.md` Section 6 (Rolls-Royce) for the Specificity Bomb executed
> at the highest level.*

**7. The Specificity Bomb**
Insert one hyper-specific detail into a general passage. It makes everything around
it feel more real and trustworthy.
"Our clients typically see results within the first two weeks. Mark — a 43-year-old
accountant in Denver who signed up on a Tuesday afternoon — saw his first lead come
in before dinner."

**8. The Honesty Disarm (Sugarman)**
Admit a weakness, flaw, or limitation before making a positive claim. The admission
makes everything after it more believable.
"This isn't the prettiest interface you've ever seen. We put every dollar into the
engine, not the paint job. Which is why it outperforms tools that cost 10x more."

**9. The Reverse Testimonial**
Instead of a glowing endorsement, use a testimonial that starts with skepticism:
"I almost didn't buy this. I'd been burned by three other programs. But something
about the guarantee made me think — what do I have to lose? Two weeks later..."

**10. The "I'll Prove It" Challenge**
State a bold claim, then immediately challenge yourself to prove it. The challenge
itself signals confidence.
"That's a big claim. Let me back it up with three numbers..."

**11. The Borrowed Authority**
Reference someone the reader already respects to lend credibility to your claim.
Not name-dropping — genuine connection to a respected framework, study, or expert.
"It's the same principle Warren Buffett uses when he says..."

### Emotional Moves (Make Them Feel)
> *See `psychology.md` Section 13 (Blair Warren) for the psychological foundations of the
> Enemy Frame and Permission Grant, and Section 8 (Loss Aversion) for why the Status Quo
> Slap works.*

**12. The Status Quo Slap**
A single vivid sentence that makes their current situation suddenly feel intolerable.
"You're going to spend the next year doing exactly what you did last year. And
you already know what that got you."

**13. Scenario Painting**
Drop the reader into a vivid scene from their own life. Second person, present tense,
sensory details.
"It's 3am. You're staring at the ceiling again. The numbers are running through
your head and they don't add up. Your spouse rolls over and asks if you're okay.
You say yes. You're lying."

**14. The Future Flash**
Project the reader forward in time to see what their life looks like AFTER the
transformation. Sensory. Specific. Emotional.
"Imagine this: It's six months from now. You open your laptop and check your
dashboard. The number staring back at you makes you laugh — not because it's
funny, but because six months ago you wouldn't have believed it was possible."

**15. The Empathy Mirror**
Show the reader you understand their situation with such precision that they feel
known. Not "I understand your frustration" — that's generic. Instead:
"You've tried the apps. You've read the books. You've set the goals on January 1st
and watched them dissolve by February. And every time, there's that little voice
that says maybe you're just not cut out for this."

**16. The Enemy Frame (Blair Warren)**
Name a shared enemy — not a person, but a system, belief, or force — and throw
rocks at it together.
"The reason you're struggling isn't discipline. It's the fact that every piece of
advice you've been given was designed by people who've never been in your situation."

**17. The Permission Grant**
Give the reader permission to want what they want, feel what they feel, or believe
what they believe. This is Blair Warren's "justify their failures" in craft form.
"It's not your fault. And wanting more doesn't make you greedy — it makes you human."

### Structural Moves (Shape the Experience)

**18. The Pattern Interrupt**
Break the expected pattern with something jarring, unexpected, or visually different.
Mid-page: a single bold line. A question that seems off-topic. A one-sentence
paragraph that contradicts what came before.
"Forget everything I just said."

**19. The Callback**
Reference something from earlier in the copy. Creates a sense of cohesion and
rewards attentive readers.
"Remember the film on your teeth I mentioned earlier? That same principle applies to..."

**20. The Objection Judo**
State the reader's objection STRONGER than they would state it themselves, then
flip it into a selling point.
"You're thinking: 'This sounds too good to be true. I've heard this kind of thing
before and it never works.' Good. That skepticism is exactly why this IS going to
work for you — because it means you won't half-ass the implementation."

**21. The List Break**
After a dense paragraph, break into a rapid-fire list. The visual variety creates
rhythm and makes the page scannable.

**22. The Parenthetical Aside**
Use parentheses to create a voice-within-the-voice — a more intimate, off-the-record
commentary that feels like a private thought shared between friends.
"She doubled her revenue in four months (and honestly, she almost didn't sign up —
her husband thought it was a scam)."

### Closing Moves (Make Them Act)
> *See `frameworks.md` Section 10 for CTA frameworks and Section 9 for guarantee/risk
> reversal frameworks that pair with these closing moves. See `case-studies.md` Section 1
> (WSJ) for the Two Paths executed masterfully.*

**23. The Two Paths (Kennedy)**
Paint two vivid futures — one where they act, one where they don't. Make the contrast
visceral and specific.
"Path A: You close this page. Nothing changes. Next month looks like last month.
Path B: You click the button below. In 48 hours, you have a plan. In 30 days,
you have your first result. In 90 days, you barely recognize your business."

**24. The Velvet Close**
Ask for the sale so gently that saying no feels like the harder option.
"If any of this sounds like what you've been looking for, the next step is simple.
Click below, fill in your details, and I'll show you everything."

**25. The Assumed Close**
Write as if they've already decided. Skip the persuasion and move straight to
logistics.
"When you log in for the first time, the first thing you'll see is..."

**26. The Risk Flip**
Reframe the purchase as the LESS risky option compared to doing nothing.
"The real risk isn't trying this and it not working — you're covered by the
guarantee. The real risk is doing nothing and being in this exact same spot
a year from now."

**27. The Quiet Confidence Close**
No hype. No urgency. Just a calm, direct statement of what's available.
"This is what I have. This is what it does. This is what it costs. If it's right
for you, I'd love to help."

**28. The P.S. Hook**
The P.S. either restates the strongest benefit, adds one final proof element,
or opens a new curiosity loop.
"P.S. — I didn't mention the bonus. It's the one thing three clients told me
was worth the entire price on its own. You'll see it on the next page."

**29. The Deadline Whisper**
Instead of shouting urgency, state it quietly. Understatement can signal more
seriousness than exclamation marks.
"The doors close Friday. I won't extend it. If you're in, be in by then."

**30. The After-CTA Reassurance**
Immediately after the CTA button, add one sentence that reduces the anxiety of clicking.
"You'll be taken to a secure checkout page. Nothing is charged until you confirm.
And you can change your mind anytime within 60 days."

## 4. The Art of the First Line

Sugarman said the first sentence should be so short and easy to read that the reader is
"almost sucked into" the copy. The first line is a gravity well. Get it right and
everything after it flows. Get it wrong and nothing else matters.

> **Cross-reference:** See `frameworks.md` Section 3 for Masterson's 12-step lead writing
> process (steps 6-7 are the first-line discipline). See `case-studies.md` for first lines
> in action — Caples' "They laughed when I sat down at the piano" (Section 3) is the
> archetype of the Story Hook first line.

### Rules for First Lines
- **Short.** Under 10 words is ideal. Under 7 is better.
- **Unexpected.** It should make the reader's brain go "wait, what?"
- **Incomplete.** It should raise a question that demands the second sentence.
- **Personal.** "You" or "I" in the first line creates immediate intimacy.
- **Present tense.** Creates immediacy and urgency.

### First Line Archetypes

**The Confession:** "I almost didn't send this."
**The Contradiction:** "Everything you know about X is wrong."
**The Scene:** "It was 3am and I was sitting in my car in the parking lot."
**The Question:** "What would you do with an extra $10,000 next month?"
**The Bold Claim:** "This is the most important email I'll ever send you."
**The Story Hook:** "The phone rang. I almost didn't answer."
**The Challenge:** "I dare you to try this for 7 days."
**The Weird Detail:** "It started with a napkin and a red pen."
**The Direct Address:** "You've been lied to."
**The Admission:** "I'm going to tell you something that might make you unsubscribe."

## 5. The Art of Transitions and Flow

The "slippery slide" isn't just about each sentence being interesting — it's about the
CONNECTIONS between sentences and sections being invisible and frictionless.

### Transition Techniques

**The Bridge Phrase:** Short phrases that connect one idea to the next:
"Which brings me to..." / "Here's why that matters..." / "But there's a catch..."
/ "Now, here's where it gets interesting..." / "And that's not all..."

**The Question Bridge:** End a section with a question, then answer it in the next:
"So why doesn't this work for most people? [new section] Because they're missing
one critical piece..."

**The Callback Bridge:** Connect a new section to something mentioned earlier:
"Remember when I said [thing]? Here's why that matters now..."

**The Contrast Bridge:** Create a sharp turn that re-engages attention:
"Everything I just told you is true. But it's not the whole story."

**The "Chapter End" Bridge:** Treat each section break like the end of a book chapter —
end with a hook that makes starting the next section irresistible:
"That was the easy part. What I'm about to tell you next is where everything changed."

### Flow Rules
- Never end a page or screen with a completed thought. End mid-thought.
- Vary sentence length: short, short, long. Short. Medium, long, long, short. The rhythm
  should feel like music, not a metronome.
- Vary paragraph length: a one-sentence paragraph after a dense paragraph creates emphasis.
- Use white space deliberately. Cramped copy feels hard. Spacious copy feels easy.
- Read aloud. If you stumble, the reader will stumble. Fix the stumble.

## 6. The Art of Fascination Bullets

Fascination bullets are miniature headlines — each one should be so compelling that it
alone could justify reading the rest of the copy or buying the product. They create
curiosity and desire simultaneously.

> **Cross-reference:** See `frameworks.md` Section 4 for headline formulas — fascination
> bullets follow the same structural DNA as headlines. See `case-studies.md` Section 14
> for Carlton's One-Legged Golfer — the headline IS a fascination bullet, expanded. See
> `psychology.md` Section 12 (Heath Brothers' SUCCESs) for why Unexpected + Concrete +
> knowledge gaps make bullets irresistible.

### The Anatomy of a Perfect Bullet
Every great bullet has three elements:
1. **A specific, intriguing claim** (creates curiosity)
2. **An implied benefit** (creates desire)
3. **Withheld information** (creates tension that demands resolution)

### Bullet Formulas

**The "What" Bullet:**
"What [unexpected person/thing] can teach you about [desired outcome]"
"What never to [do/eat/say] when [common situation]"

**The "Why" Bullet:**
"Why [common practice] is actually [negative consequence] — and what to do instead"
"Why [counterintuitive thing] actually [desirable result]"

**The "How" Bullet:**
"How to [achieve result] without [common sacrifice/effort]"
"How a [unexpected person] [achieved impressive result] in [short timeframe]"

**The "The" Bullet:**
"The #1 [thing] that separates [winners] from [losers] (it's not what you think)"
"The [adjective] [thing] that [specific result] — page [X]"
"The one [thing] every [audience] should know about [topic] before [action]"

**The "Secret" Bullet:**
"The little-known [thing] that [specific result] (hint: it has nothing to do with [obvious thing])"
"A closely-guarded secret used by [impressive group] to [result]"

**The "Warning" Bullet:**
"Warning: [common action] may be [negative consequence]"
"The #1 mistake that [audience] make when [activity] (you're probably doing it right now)"

### Bullet Writing Rules
- Be specific about the PROBLEM; be vague about the SOLUTION (creates need to buy)
- Each bullet should stand alone — compelling even without context
- Use numbers when possible — they create specificity and credibility
- Write 3x more bullets than you need, then cut the weakest ones
- If a bullet doesn't make you think "I need to know that," cut it

## 7. The Art of the Close

The close is where most copy falls apart. You've done the work — built desire, shifted
beliefs, stacked proof — and then you fumble the ask. The close must feel like the natural,
inevitable conclusion of everything that came before.

### Types of Closes

**The Emotional Close:**
Return to the Golden Thread. Connect the CTA to their deepest desire, not the product
features. "This isn't about [product]. This is about [what they really want]. And today,
you can take the first step."

**The Logical Close:**
Summarize the math. Make it undeniable.
"You've seen the results. You know the guarantee. The question isn't whether this works —
it's whether you can afford NOT to try it."

**The Story Close:**
End with a brief story — a client who almost didn't buy, a moment that captures the
transformation. Let the story do the closing.

**The Quiet Close:**
No hype. Simple, direct, human.
"I've shared everything I can in this letter. If it resonates, the next step is below.
If not, no hard feelings."

### Close Rules
- Never introduce new information in the close. Everything should have been established.
- The CTA should be physically easy to complete (one click, one step).
- Restate the guarantee near the close — it removes the final barrier.
- After the main close, add a P.S. for skimmers who jumped to the bottom.
- The best close feels like relief, not pressure. The reader has been carrying the
  tension of the problem through the whole piece — the CTA is the release valve.

## 8. Generative Swipe Prompts — Writing Like the Masters

These are instructions for producing copy in the style of specific legendary promotions.
Not for copying — for internalizing the structural DNA and generating new work.

> **Cross-reference:** See `case-studies.md` for the full breakdown of each of these
> promotions — what they did, why they worked, and what to steal. Each swipe prompt below
> corresponds to a case study. See the **AI Prompting & Workflows** skill for how to turn
> these swipe prompts into reusable AI system prompts that generate voice-consistent first
> drafts in the style of any master. The swipe prompt becomes the voice directive in your
> prompt chain.

### To Write Like "The Two Young Men" (WSJ)
Start with two characters who share a starting point but end in different places. Open at
the point of divergence. Never state what caused the difference — let the comparison create
the conclusion. Use 6th-grade reading level. Make every sentence advance the contrast.
Mention the product only after the story has created the question it answers.

### To Write Like the Coat of Arms Letter (Halbert)
Find the one thing about the reader that's unique to THEM. Make it the opening. Keep it
personal, brief, and flattering. The offer should feel like something discovered FOR them,
not something sold TO them. Under 400 words. Hand-addressed feel, even in digital.

### To Write Like "They Laughed When I..." (Caples)
Start at the moment of highest emotional tension. Build the underdog narrative:
mockery → performance → triumph. Let the audience's reaction tell the story of the
result. Withhold the "how" until the reader is emotionally invested in the "what."
The product is the method that made the miracle possible, not the star of the ad.

### To Write Like the BluBlocker Ad (Sugarman)
Write in first person. Describe your personal encounter with the product in real time.
Use extreme sensory detail — what you saw, felt, noticed. Meander. Let the reader walk
through the experience with you. The product reveal should feel like you're sharing a
personal discovery, not making a sales pitch. Take your time. The length doesn't
matter if every sentence pulls toward the next.

### To Write Like the One-Legged Golfer (Carlton)
Find the most unlikely success story you can — the person with the MOST disadvantages
who still got the result. Make them the headline. Use extreme specificity in the
numbers (50 yards, 10 strokes). The implicit logic: if they can do it with those
limitations, you can definitely do it. Load every sentence with vivid, physical,
concrete imagery.

### To Write Like "The End of America" (Palmer)
Start with a fear the audience already has but can't articulate. Confirm it with
data and evidence. Build to a prediction that feels urgent and inevitable. Position
yourself as the person who sees what's coming. Your product is the lifeboat for
people smart enough to prepare. Documentary tone, not sales tone. The format itself
(long-form video, editorial style) is part of the persuasion.

### To Write Like Settle's Daily Email
Start with something completely unrelated to your product — a movie, a neighbor, a news
story, an argument, a childhood memory. Find the unexpected connection to a principle
your audience cares about. Bridge to your offer in one sentence. Close with a link
and a P.S. Total: under 500 words. Write in under 30 minutes. If it's polished,
it's overworked.

## 9. The Revision Protocol — What to Do When Something Fails

The revision checklist (in SKILL.md) tells you what to check. This section tells you
what to DO when something doesn't pass.

> **Cross-reference:** For the underlying frameworks referenced in each fix below, see
> `frameworks.md` (Schwartz verbalization techniques in Section 5, Hormozi Value Equation
> in Section 12, Bencivenga's belief gates in Section 11 RMBC method). For the psychology
> behind why these fixes work, see `psychology.md` (awareness levels in Section 1, loss
> aversion in Section 8, Blair Warren in Section 13).

### If the Headline Falls Flat
- **Diagnosis:** It's not stopping the right person or creating enough pull.
- **Fix 1:** Check the awareness level. A direct headline for an unaware audience will
  always fail. Move to a more indirect approach.
- **Fix 2:** Pick a DIFFERENT Schwartz verbalization technique. If you used a benefit
  claim, try a question. If you tried curiosity, try a specific result.
- **Fix 3:** Go back to VOC research. Find the exact phrase the prospect uses to describe
  their pain. Put it in the headline.
- **Fix 4:** Write 25 more headlines. Fast. No editing. The best one is usually in the
  second half of the list, after your brain exhausts the obvious options.

### If the Lead Loses Momentum
- **Diagnosis:** The reader starts but doesn't continue.
- **Fix 1:** Your first sentence is too long. Cut it to under 10 words.
- **Fix 2:** You're starting at the wrong point. If using a story lead, start at the moment
  of highest tension, not the beginning. If using a problem lead, start with the most
  visceral detail, not a general statement.
- **Fix 3:** Try one level MORE indirect. If the big idea lead isn't working, try a story
  lead. If the promise lead isn't working, try a secret lead.
- **Fix 4:** Add a seed of curiosity in the first 3 sentences. Something that can only
  be resolved by reading the next paragraph.

### If the Body Feels Flat
- **Diagnosis:** The strategy is right but the writing is boring.
- **Fix 1:** You're telling instead of showing. Replace abstract statements with scenario
  painting, specific details, and sensory language.
- **Fix 2:** Add stories. One story is worth a thousand assertions.
- **Fix 3:** Check the voice. Is it conversational? Read it aloud. If it sounds like a
  textbook, rewrite it as if you're explaining to a friend at dinner.
- **Fix 4:** Add open loops and seeds of curiosity at every natural stopping point.
- **Fix 5:** Vary the rhythm. Too many sentences of similar length creates monotony.
  Mix short punches with long rolls.

### If the Proof Feels Weak
- **Diagnosis:** Claims aren't landing. The reader doesn't believe.
- **Fix 1:** You're using only one TYPE of proof. Stack multiple types: testimonials +
  data + demonstrations + credentials + case studies.
- **Fix 2:** Your proof isn't specific enough. "Great results" means nothing. "Added
  $47,000 in revenue in 31 days" means everything.
- **Fix 3:** Place proof IMMEDIATELY after each claim, not in a separate section.
- **Fix 4:** Use a reverse testimonial — someone who was skeptical and was won over.
  This mirrors the reader's current mental state.

### If the Offer Doesn't Convert
- **Diagnosis:** They read everything but don't buy.
- **Fix 1:** Run the Hormozi Value Equation diagnostic. Which lever is weak? Dream
  Outcome unclear? Perceived Likelihood too low? Time Delay too long? Effort too high?
- **Fix 2:** Check Bencivenga's belief gates. Which belief is missing? They may believe
  the problem is real but not believe it will work for THEM specifically. Or they
  believe in the product but don't feel urgency.
- **Fix 3:** Strengthen the guarantee. Name it. Make it bold. Make it the climax of
  the offer, not an afterthought.
- **Fix 4:** Add price justification. Anchor, compare, break down into daily cost.

### If the Close Feels Pushy
- **Diagnosis:** The transition from content to sale feels jarring.
- **Fix 1:** Your body copy isn't doing enough pre-selling. The close should feel like
  a natural conclusion, not a pivot. Work backward — the body should already have the
  reader 80% convinced before the close begins.
- **Fix 2:** Rewrite using the Romantic Metaphor (Masterson). You're not conquering,
  you're inviting. You're not closing, you're opening a door.
- **Fix 3:** Use the Velvet Close or Quiet Confidence Close. Drop the hype.
- **Fix 4:** Frame the CTA as service, not selling. "Here's what I recommend..." feels
  different from "BUY NOW BEFORE IT'S TOO LATE."

## 10. Market-Specific Craft Adjustments

The core principles are universal. But HOW you apply them shifts based on the market.

> **Cross-reference:** For market-level research and audience profiling before writing, see
> the **Research & Market Analysis** skill. For systematic brand voice architecture that
> persists across campaigns within a market, see the **Brand Voice** skill. For how these
> market-specific adjustments map to awareness levels and sophistication stages, see
> `psychology.md` Section 1 (awareness) and `frameworks.md` Section 7 (unique mechanism —
> critical for sophisticated markets).

### Health / Supplements / Wellness
- **Voice:** Authority + empathy. Ogilvy meets empathy.
- **Lead with:** Mechanism. This market is sophisticated (Stage 4+). They've heard every
  claim. The mechanism — WHY your approach works differently — is everything.
- **Proof emphasis:** Studies, doctor endorsements, specific physiological explanations.
- **Watch out for:** Regulatory compliance. You often can't make direct health claims.
  Use the "structure-function" workaround: describe what the product does mechanistically
  rather than claiming it cures/treats/prevents anything.
- **Craft note:** Scenario painting is extremely powerful here. Put the reader in the scene
  of how their body feels at 3am, in the mirror, at the doctor's office.

### B2B / SaaS / Professional Services
- **Voice:** Ogilvy voice. Understated, factual, respectful of intelligence.
- **Lead with:** Results and ROI. "We helped [company] achieve [specific metric]."
- **Proof emphasis:** Case studies with named companies, specific numbers, and timelines.
- **Watch out for:** Multiple decision-makers. Your copy may need to convince the user
  AND the person who signs the check. Include both emotional benefits (for the user)
  and ROI/logic (for the CFO).
- **Craft note:** Subtherland's insight applies heavily here: people buy to avoid criticism.
  "Nobody ever got fired for choosing [established option]." Address the implicit fear
  of making the wrong decision for their company.

### Info Products / Coaching / Courses
- **Voice:** Halbert or Settle. Personality-heavy. The person IS the product.
- **Lead with:** Transformation story. Your story or a client's story.
- **Proof emphasis:** Student results, transformation stories, specific outcomes.
- **Watch out for:** Market is extremely saturated (Stage 5). You MUST differentiate
  through personality, mechanism, or a fresh Big Idea.
- **Craft note:** The Epiphany Bridge (Brunson) is critical here. You're selling a
  belief shift, not a product. The copy must take the reader across the bridge from
  their current belief to the new belief that makes buying the natural conclusion.

### E-Commerce / Physical Products
- **Voice:** Sugarman. Sensory, product-focused, experiential.
- **Lead with:** The product experience. What it feels like to use/wear/hold it.
- **Proof emphasis:** Photos, videos, reviews, UGC, before/after.
- **Watch out for:** Short attention spans. Get to the product fast. Use visuals heavily.
  Fascination bullets work extremely well for product descriptions.
- **Craft note:** Mental ownership (Sugarman's #1 trigger) is everything. Get the reader
  to imagine holding it, wearing it, using it. Sensory language creates possession.

### Financial / Investment
- **Voice:** Palmer/Stansberry style. Authority + fear + identity.
- **Lead with:** A prediction, a warning, or insider knowledge.
- **Proof emphasis:** Data, charts, track record, named recommendations with outcomes.
- **Watch out for:** Compliance requirements. Financial disclaimers are mandatory.
  Work within them — the constraint often forces more creative copy.
- **Craft note:** Blair Warren's "confirm their suspicions" and "throw rocks at enemies"
  are extremely powerful in financial markets. The reader already suspects things are
  rigged. Confirm it with evidence. Then position your product as the antidote.

### Local / Service Businesses
- **Voice:** Warm, personal, community-focused.
- **Lead with:** Trust signals. Reviews, years in business, local references.
- **Proof emphasis:** Google reviews, before/after photos, specific local results.
- **Watch out for:** These prospects are often Most Aware or Product Aware — they know
  they need a plumber/dentist/lawyer. They're comparing options. Lead with proof and
  differentiators, not education.
- **Craft note:** The guarantee is often the #1 conversion lever for local services.
  "On time or your service is free." "Not satisfied? Don't pay." Named, bold guarantees
  dominate in local markets.

## 11. Craft Benchmarks — Readability, Rhythm & Format Targets

Craft is subjective — but it has measurable dimensions. These benchmarks give you concrete
targets to aim for during drafting and revision. They are guidelines, not laws. A piece that
hits every benchmark but reads flat is worse than a piece that breaks the rules but sings.
But when something feels "off" and you can't pinpoint why, these numbers help you diagnose.

> **Cross-reference:** For how to use AI to evaluate copy against these benchmarks at scale,
> see the **AI Prompting & Workflows** skill. You can build automated evaluation pipelines
> that score drafts against these targets before human review — dramatically reducing revision
> cycles. See also `case-studies.md` for what these benchmarks look like in legendary copy
> (the WSJ letter reads at a 6th-grade level; Ogilvy's Rolls-Royce ad sits at 8th-grade).

### Readability Targets by Format (Flesch-Kincaid Grade Level)

| Format | Target Grade Level | Flesch Reading Ease | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Long-form sales letter** | 5th–7th grade | 70–80 | Halbert and Schwartz both wrote at a 6th-grade level. Simple language sells because it's frictionless — System 1 processing (see `psychology.md` Section 9). The reader's energy goes to the argument, not the decoding. |
| **VSL script** | 4th–6th grade | 75–85 | Spoken word demands even simpler language. If you stumble reading it aloud, it's too complex. Palmer's "End of America" VSL used conversational, 5th-grade language despite covering complex economics. |
| **Email (sales/nurture)** | 5th–7th grade | 70–80 | Emails are read on phones, half-distracted, between other tasks. Simplicity is survival. Settle's daily emails read at a 5th-grade level. |
| **Cold email / outbound** | 5th–6th grade | 75–85 | You have 3 seconds. Every syllable is a tax on attention. The shorter and simpler, the higher the reply rate. |
| **Facebook/Instagram ad** | 4th–6th grade | 80–90 | Competing with cat videos and vacation photos. Your copy needs to be processed instantly. Short words, short sentences, zero friction. |
| **Google ad (search)** | 6th–8th grade | 65–75 | The prospect is actively looking — you can be slightly more direct and specific. But still, simpler beats clever. |
| **LinkedIn ad / post** | 7th–9th grade | 60–70 | Professional context allows slightly more sophisticated language, but don't confuse "professional" with "dense." Ogilvy-register language works here. |
| **B2B landing page** | 7th–9th grade | 60–70 | Decision-makers are busy. Clear, specific, and factual beats jargon-heavy and verbose. See `case-studies.md` Section 6 for how Ogilvy's factual specificity at ~8th-grade level tripled Rolls-Royce sales. |
| **Premium/luxury copy** | 8th–10th grade | 55–65 | Restraint and precision signal quality. Slightly more sophisticated vocabulary is appropriate — but never sacrificing clarity. Complexity should come from precision, not obscurity. |
| **Advertorial** | 6th–8th grade | 65–75 | Must feel like editorial content. Match the reading level of the publication or platform it appears in. Camouflage (see `psychology.md` Section 2 on Schwartz's techniques) requires matching the host environment. |

**The rule:** When in doubt, go simpler. Nobody ever lost a sale because the copy was too
easy to read. Plenty of sales have been lost because it was too hard. Hopkins: "The wisest
men in advertising use the simplest language."

### Sentence Length Distribution Targets

Great copy has RHYTHM. Rhythm comes from variation — not from uniformly short or uniformly
long sentences. Here are the distribution targets:

| Format | Short (1–8 words) | Medium (9–20 words) | Long (21+ words) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Sales letter / VSL** | 30–40% | 40–50% | 10–20% |
| **Email (sales/nurture)** | 35–45% | 40–50% | 10–15% |
| **Cold email / outbound** | 40–50% | 40–50% | 5–10% |
| **Facebook/Instagram ad** | 45–55% | 35–45% | 5–10% |
| **B2B landing page** | 25–35% | 45–55% | 15–25% |
| **Premium/luxury copy** | 20–30% | 45–55% | 20–30% |

**How to use this:** After drafting, count a representative sample of 20-30 sentences. If
your distribution skews too far in any direction, rewrite. Too many long sentences = the
reader drowns. Too many short sentences = the copy feels choppy and breathless. The mix
creates music.

**The Carlton pattern:** Short, short, long. Short. Medium, long, short. This is the
rhythm that creates the "punchy but flowing" feel Carlton's copy is known for. See
`case-studies.md` Section 14 for this rhythm in action.

**The Sugarman pattern:** Medium, long, medium, long, short. Medium, long. This is the
"meandering but compelling" feel of Sugarman's narratives — the slippery slide. See
`case-studies.md` Section 4 for the BluBlocker ad's sentence rhythm.

### Paragraph Length Targets by Format

| Format | Target Paragraph Length | Max Paragraph Length | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Sales letter** | 1–4 sentences | 6 sentences | Mix aggressively. Follow a 4-sentence paragraph with a 1-sentence paragraph for emphasis. |
| **VSL script** | 1–3 sentences | 4 sentences | Every paragraph is a single thought. Shorter = more conversational when spoken. |
| **Email** | 1–3 sentences | 4 sentences | On mobile, a 4-sentence paragraph looks like a wall. 1-2 sentence paragraphs are the sweet spot. |
| **Cold email** | 1–2 sentences | 3 sentences | Brevity is respect. Each paragraph should be scannable in under 2 seconds. |
| **Facebook/Instagram ad** | 1–2 sentences | 3 sentences | Visual whitespace matters as much as the words. Every line break is a micro-rest for the eye. |
| **B2B landing page** | 2–4 sentences | 5 sentences | Slightly denser is acceptable — the reader expects substance. But still break up walls of text. |
| **Premium/luxury copy** | 2–5 sentences | 6 sentences | Longer, more polished paragraphs signal care and sophistication. But never sacrifice readability for elegance. |

### Reading Time Targets by Format and Price Point

How long should the reader spend with your copy? This depends on the price point and the
awareness level. Higher price = more time needed to build desire and overcome objections.
Lower awareness = more time needed to educate and shift beliefs.

| Format | Low Ticket ($7–$47) | Mid Ticket ($47–$497) | High Ticket ($497–$5,000+) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Sales page** | 5–10 min (1,500–3,000 words) | 15–30 min (4,000–8,000 words) | 30–60 min (8,000–15,000+ words) |
| **VSL** | 8–15 min | 20–45 min | 45–90 min |
| **Email sequence (total)** | 3–5 emails, 200–400 words each | 5–10 emails, 300–600 words each | 10–20+ emails, 400–800 words each |
| **Single nurture email** | 1–2 min (200–400 words) | 2–4 min (300–600 words) | 3–5 min (400–800 words) |
| **Cold email** | 30 sec–1 min (50–125 words) | 30 sec–1 min (75–150 words) | 1–2 min (100–200 words) |
| **Facebook/Instagram ad** | 30 sec (50–150 words) | 1–2 min (150–400 words) | 2–3 min (300–600 words) |
| **Landing page (opt-in)** | 1–3 min (300–800 words) | 3–5 min (800–1,500 words) | 5–10 min (1,500–3,000 words) |

**The Halbert rule:** "Long copy outsells short copy, every time." This is true WHEN the
copy is good. Length is a function of how much you need to say to close the deal — which
is a function of price and awareness. A $7 impulse buy from a hot list needs 500 words.
A $2,000 course from a cold audience might need 12,000.

**The Sugarman rule:** "Your ad should be long enough to tell the story and short enough
to remain interesting." Never pad for length. Every sentence must earn its place.

## 12. The Final Test — Reading Aloud

This is not optional. This is the single most important editing technique in copywriting.

Read your copy aloud. Every word. As if you're saying it to one person sitting across
from you.

### What You're Listening For
- **Stumbles:** If your mouth trips on a phrase, the reader's brain will trip on it too. Rewrite it.
- **Boredom:** If YOU get bored reading it, the reader bailed five sentences earlier. Cut or rewrite.
- **Stiffness:** If it sounds like a press release or a textbook, it's dead. Make it human.
- **Rhythm:** Can you hear the music? Short, short, long. Punch, punch, flow. Vary it.
- **Authenticity:** Does it sound like a person? Or does it sound like "marketing"?
- **The "Would I Say This?" Test:** At every sentence, ask: "Would I actually say this to
  someone face-to-face?" If not, it doesn't belong in the copy.

### The Halbert Test
Gary Halbert would read his copy to strangers — in line at the grocery store, at the bar,
to his barber — and watch their reactions. If their eyes glazed over, he rewrote. If they
leaned in, he kept it. You can't do that every time. But you CAN read it aloud and feel
it in your own body: does it land? Does it move? Does it pull?

### The Hopkins Gut Check
Hopkins said: "The best ads ask no one to buy. They offer wanted information and describe
advantages. The ad is based entirely on service." After reading your copy aloud, ask:
does this feel like a service to the reader, or an imposition on them? The answer
determines whether you've succeeded.
