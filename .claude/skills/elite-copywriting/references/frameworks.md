## 0. Framework Selection Decision Matrix

The hardest question isn't "how do I use PAS?" — it's "should I use PAS at all, or
something else entirely?" This matrix answers that question. Match your situation (format,
awareness level, market sophistication) to the right framework BEFORE you start writing.

> **See psychology.md Section 1** for the full five-level awareness system that drives the
> "Awareness Level Match" column below. **See psychology.md Section 2** for understanding
> mass desire dimensions that inform which framework will channel desire most effectively.

### The Decision Matrix

| Framework | Best For (Format / Situation) | Awareness Level Match | Sophistication Stage Match | Complexity Level |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **PAS** (Problem, Agitate, Solve) | Short-form copy, cold emails, social ads, Facebook/Instagram ads, email subject lines + body. Any situation where you need to move fast and hit hard. | Problem Aware, Solution Aware | Stages 1-3. Loses power in Stage 4-5 unless combined with a unique mechanism. | Low — the simplest framework. Master this first. |
| **AIDA** (Attention, Interest, Desire, Action) | Sales pages, email sequences, full-funnel copy, landing pages, VSLs. The Swiss Army knife — works across most formats and lengths. | All levels (adjust the Attention element to match awareness). Works best Product Aware through Solution Aware. | Stages 1-4. At Stage 5, supplement with heavy identification and story elements. | Low-Medium — straightforward but each stage requires real craft. |
| **RMBC** (Research, Mechanism, Brief, Copy) | Long-form sales letters, VSLs, high-ticket sales pages, advertorials, any copy over 1,000 words where the sale depends on a persuasive argument. The professional's production system. | Solution Aware and Problem Aware primarily. The mechanism component makes it ideal when the prospect has tried other solutions. | Stages 3-5. This is the framework built for sophisticated markets where mechanism differentiation wins. | High — this is a full production system, not a quick formula. Requires deep research. See the Research & Market Analysis skill for the research phase. |
| **4 P's** (Promise, Picture, Proof, Push) | Landing pages, webinar registration pages, opt-in pages, short sales pages. Excellent for offers with strong proof elements and a clear, bold promise. | Product Aware, Solution Aware. The prospect needs to be far enough along to appreciate a direct promise. | Stages 1-3. At higher stages, the Promise must be mechanism-backed. | Low — four clean steps. Good for fast execution with a proven offer. |
| **Star-Chain-Hook** (Gary Halbert) | Advertorials, long-form sales letters, story-driven markets, info products, coaching/consulting. Any market where narrative bypasses skepticism better than direct claims. | Unaware, Problem Aware. Especially powerful when the prospect doesn't know they need what you sell. | Stages 3-5. Story leads are the go-to for exhausted markets. | Medium — the Star must be compelling and the Chain must build genuine momentum. See case-studies.md Section 7 for Halbert's "Desperate Nerd from Ohio." |
| **QUEST** (Qualify, Understand, Educate, Stimulate, Transition) | Webinar scripts, long-form content, email sequences, LinkedIn posts, educational sales content. Ideal when you need to qualify the audience AND educate them before selling. | Unaware through Solution Aware. The Qualify step filters the audience; the Educate step moves them up the awareness ladder. | Stages 2-4. The education component repositions the conversation — useful when the market is tired of direct claims. | Medium — the Educate stage requires genuine insight, not just product features. |
| **Before-After-Bridge** (BAB) | Short emails, social media posts, simple ad copy, cold outreach opening lines, SMS. The fastest emotional framework — works in under 100 words. | Problem Aware, Solution Aware. The Before state must resonate instantly. | Stages 1-3. Too simple for highly sophisticated markets unless paired with a strong mechanism in the Bridge. | Very Low — the simplest framework after PAS. Three moves and you're done. |
| **FAB Chain** (Feature, Advantage, Benefit) | Product descriptions, comparison pages, email bullets, sales page body copy (within a larger framework), Amazon listings. Best as a component inside a larger piece, not a standalone structure. | Product Aware, Most Aware. The prospect already knows the category — now they need specifics. | Stages 1-3. At higher stages, wrap each FAB chain in a mechanism explanation. | Very Low — but stacking multiple FAB chains requires strategic ordering. See the Funnel Architecture skill for offer stacking principles. |
| **StoryBrand** (Donald Miller) | Website copy, brand messaging, about pages, email sequences, pitch decks, webinar structures. Any copy where the brand narrative needs to be clear and the customer must feel like the hero. | All levels — the 7-part structure adapts to any awareness level by adjusting which element you lead with. | Stages 1-4. At Stage 5, combine with aggressive mechanism differentiation. | Medium — requires clear identification of the customer's external, internal, and philosophical problems. |
| **PASTOR** (Ray Edwards) | Sales pages, VSLs, webinar closing sequences, high-ticket offer presentations. A modern PAS expansion built for long-form selling with story and offer stacking. | Problem Aware through Solution Aware. The Story element makes it work for less aware audiences than PAS alone. | Stages 2-4. The Story + Transformation combination handles moderate sophistication well. | Medium — six stages require pacing discipline. The Story must be genuine and the Transformation must be vivid. |
| **Schwartz Big Idea** | Any format — but most critical for headlines, leads, and the organizing principle of major campaigns. This is not a structural framework like PAS; it's the strategic foundation that any framework sits on top of. | All levels — the Big Idea adapts its expression to the awareness level. | Stages 3-5. At lower stages, strong claims alone can win. At higher stages, the Big Idea IS the differentiator. | Very High — finding the Big Idea requires deep market understanding, creative thinking, and the ability to distill a complex argument into one compelling concept. See the Research & Market Analysis skill for the discovery process. |

### How to Use the Matrix

**Step 1: Diagnose the situation.** Before choosing a framework, answer three questions:
- What awareness level is my prospect? (See psychology.md Section 1)
- What sophistication stage is my market? (See psychology.md Section 2 for desire dimensions;
  use the Research & Market Analysis skill for a full diagnosis)
- What format am I writing? (Ad, email, sales page, VSL, landing page, etc.)

**Step 2: Match to the matrix.** Find the row(s) that match all three answers. If multiple
frameworks fit, choose based on:
- Your strongest available proof (heavy proof → 4 P's or AIDA)
- Your strongest available story (compelling narrative → Star-Chain-Hook or StoryBrand)
- Your strongest available mechanism (unique mechanism → RMBC)
- Time available (fast turnaround → PAS, BAB, or FAB chain)

**Step 3: Layer frameworks.** Elite copy often combines frameworks. Common combinations:
- **RMBC + PAS lead** — Use RMBC as the production system, open with PAS to hook
- **StoryBrand + AIDA** — StoryBrand for the narrative arc, AIDA for the pacing within each section
- **Big Idea + any framework** — The Big Idea is the strategic foundation; the framework is the structural execution
- **QUEST + FAB chain body** — QUEST for the overall flow, FAB chains for the product detail section
- **PASTOR + Hormozi Value Equation** — PASTOR for the argument structure, Value Equation for the offer section

> **See the Funnel Architecture skill** for how framework selection changes based on where
> the copy sits in the funnel (frontend vs. core vs. backend). A cold traffic Facebook ad
> uses a different framework than a retargeting sales page, even for the same product.

## 1. Core Copy Frameworks

> **See psychology.md Section 1** for the awareness levels referenced throughout this section.
> Every framework works differently depending on where the prospect sits on the Schwartz
> awareness spectrum. **See psychology.md Section 13** (Blair Warren's One Sentence Persuasion)
> for the five emotional drivers that power every framework below.

### PAS — Problem, Agitate, Solve (Dan Kennedy)
Kennedy called this "the most reliable sales formula ever invented."
- **Problem** — Name the specific pain the reader is experiencing. Be precise. Use their
  language, not yours.
- **Agitate** — Twist the knife. Show consequences. Make it visceral. Paint what happens
  if they do nothing. Use sensory language — what does it FEEL like to live with this
  problem? Future-pace the pain: "In six months, where will you be if nothing changes?"
- **Solve** — Present your product/service as the bridge from pain to relief. The relief
  should feel proportional to the agitation.

Best for: short-form copy, cold emails, social ads, problem-aware audiences.
See case-studies.md Section 15 for cross-cutting lessons on how PAS appears in the greatest
ads ever written. See psychology.md Section 8 (Loss Aversion) for why the Agitate step
works — losses feel 2x as painful as equivalent gains.

### AIDA — Attention, Interest, Desire, Action
The oldest formula in advertising (E. St. Elmo Lewis, 1898). Still the backbone of most
funnels and sequences.
- **Attention** — Stop them with a headline, hook, or pattern interrupt.
- **Interest** — Engage with relevant, unexpected, or counterintuitive information.
- **Desire** — Build the want. Benefits, transformation, social proof, emotional pull.
- **Action** — Tell them exactly what to do, remove friction, add urgency.

Best for: sales pages, email sequences, full-funnel copy. See the Funnel Architecture skill
for how AIDA maps to each stage of a multi-step funnel. See case-studies.md Section 1 (Wall
Street Journal) for one of the most successful AIDA-structured letters ever mailed.

### BAB — Before, After, Bridge
- **Before** — Here's your world right now (pain state). Make it vivid and specific.
- **After** — Here's what your world looks like after (desired state). Make it sensory.
- **Bridge** — Here's how to get there (your product/solution).

Best for: short emails, social posts, simple ad copy. Very fast and emotionally potent.

### PASTOR — Problem, Amplify, Story, Transformation, Offer, Response (Ray Edwards)
A modern expansion of PAS designed for sales pages and VSLs:
- **Problem** — Identify and call out the problem
- **Amplify** — Show consequences of not solving it (agitation + future-pacing)
- **Story** — Tell a story of transformation (yours, a client's, the product's origin)
- **Transformation** — Paint the after-state in vivid detail
- **Offer** — Present the solution, stack the value
- **Response** — Clear CTA with urgency

### The 4 P's — Promise, Picture, Proof, Push (Henry Hoke Sr.)
- **Promise** — Bold, specific promise in the headline
- **Picture** — Help the reader visualize the outcome with sensory language
- **Proof** — Back the promise with evidence
- **Push** — Create urgency and ask for the action

### Star, Story, Solution (Gary Halbert)
- **Star** — Introduce a character (often the customer or the product creator)
- **Story** — Tell their story of struggle and discovery
- **Solution** — Reveal the product as what made the difference

Best for: advertorials, long-form sales letters, story-driven markets. See case-studies.md
Section 7 (Halbert's "Desperate Nerd from Ohio") and Section 2 (Coat of Arms Letter) for
Star-Story-Solution in action. See psychology.md Section 6 (Sugarman's Trigger #25:
Storytelling) for why narrative bypasses analytical resistance.

### QUEST — Qualify, Understand, Educate, Stimulate, Transition
- **Qualify** — Call out your specific audience ("If you're a...")
- **Understand** — Show you know their situation deeply (empathy, mirroring)
- **Educate** — Provide new insight or reframe (belief shifting)
- **Stimulate** — Create desire through benefits and proof
- **Transition** — Bridge to the offer/CTA

### The Schwartz Big Idea Framework
Schwartz argued that every great ad is built around ONE Big Idea — a single, powerful
concept that captures the imagination and carries the entire sales message. The Big Idea is:
- Fresh (the market hasn't heard it before)
- Easily understood (can be stated in one sentence)
- Inherently intriguing (creates curiosity)
- Emotionally compelling (connects to a mass desire)
- Believable (supported by proof or logic)

The Big Idea becomes the headline, the lead, and the organizing principle of the entire
piece. Everything supports it. Nothing competes with it.

## 2. Lead Types (Masterson & Forde — Great Leads)

> **See psychology.md Section 1** for the full awareness level system that determines which
> lead type to use. **See psychology.md Section 3** (Core Emotional Complex) for how to
> identify the Golden Thread that your lead must connect to. **See case-studies.md Sections
> 1-14** for examples of each lead type deployed in legendary campaigns.

The lead is the opening of your copy — headlines through the first few paragraphs.
It does the heaviest lifting. Choose based on awareness level and market sophistication.
Masterson identified six lead types, arranged on a spectrum from most direct to most
indirect:

### Direct Lead (Most Direct)
Leads with the offer, benefit, or promise straight up.
Use when: Most Aware or Product Aware. Low-sophistication markets. Hot retargeting lists.
Example approach: "Get [specific result] in [specific timeframe] — guaranteed."
Strength: Fastest conversion for ready buyers.
Risk: Repels cold or sophisticated prospects.

### Promise Lead
Leads with a powerful benefit promise but delays the offer.
Use when: Product Aware to Solution Aware. Markets that respond to bold claims.
Example approach: "How to retire 10 years early — on your current salary."
Strength: Captures desire-driven readers.

### Secret / Discovery Lead
Teases hidden, insider, or counterintuitive knowledge.
Use when: Solution Aware or Problem Aware markets that love exclusivity and novelty.
Example approach: "A small group of [type of people] have been quietly using this method..."
Strength: Creates irresistible curiosity while implying authority.

### Big Idea / Proclamation Lead
Opens with a bold, sweeping claim or contrarian insight.
Use when: Solution Aware markets that need a new frame. Sophisticated markets.
Example approach: "Everything you've been told about [topic] is wrong. Here's why..."
Strength: Reframes the conversation and positions you as the authority.

### Story Lead
Opens with a narrative — a person, a struggle, a turning point.
Use when: Unaware or Problem Aware. Especially effective in sophisticated markets
where the reader has heard every direct claim before.
Strength: Bypasses skepticism entirely because people don't resist stories.

### Invitation Lead (Most Indirect)
Invites the reader into an exclusive group, experience, or worldview.
Use when: Unaware audiences. The most indirect approach.
Example approach: "You've been selected to join..."
Strength: Creates belonging and exclusivity before any selling occurs.

### The Rule: Match Indirection to Awareness
- Most Aware / Product Aware → Direct leads
- Solution Aware → Promise or Secret leads
- Problem Aware → Big Idea or Story leads
- Unaware → Story or Invitation leads

## 3. Masterson's 12-Step Lead Writing Process

From *The Architecture of Persuasion* — a systematic process for crafting powerful leads:

1. **Define the Core Emotional Complex** — What is the bundle of feelings, thoughts, and
   desires driving this market? What is the Golden Thread that connects them all?
2. **Identify the awareness level** — Where is the prospect on the Schwartz spectrum?
3. **Select the lead type** — Based on awareness and sophistication (see above).
4. **Write the headline** — Express the Big Idea in terms that match the awareness level.
5. **Write the deck/subheadline** — Expand, qualify, or add credibility to the headline.
6. **Write the opening line** — Make it impossibly short and compelling. Sugarman: the first
   sentence should be so short and easy to read that the reader is "sucked in."
7. **Establish rapport and identification** — Show you understand their world.
8. **Introduce the problem or opportunity** — Named in their language, not yours.
9. **Agitate or intensify** — Make the problem vivid, urgent, and personal.
10. **Plant the first seed of curiosity** — Tease what's coming. Open a loop.
11. **Transition to the body** — Bridge from problem/story to solution/mechanism.
12. **Test the lead** — Read it aloud. Does it flow? Does it create momentum? Would YOU
    keep reading? If not, rewrite from step 6.

## 4. Headline Formulas & Systems

> **See psychology.md Section 1** for how awareness level determines which headline type to
> use. **See psychology.md Section 9** (System 1 vs System 2) for why headlines must target
> System 1 processing — cognitive ease, emotional resonance, and pattern interruption.
> **See case-studies.md Section 3** ("They Laughed When I Sat Down at the Piano") for the
> most analyzed headline in advertising history.

Headlines do 80% of the work (Ogilvy). Spend disproportionate time here.
Write 20-50 headline variations before picking the best one.

### The Caples Headline Framework
John Caples identified four qualities of great headlines. The best ones have multiple:
1. **Self-interest** — promises a benefit the reader wants
2. **News** — announces something new or timely
3. **Curiosity** — creates an information gap that compels reading
4. **Quick, easy way** — suggests speed, simplicity, or convenience

Caples also identified the weakest category: headlines that are merely clever or funny
without any of the above four qualities.

### Victor Schwab's 100 Greatest Headlines Analysis
Schwab studied the 100 most successful headlines and found they fell into categories:
- Offering useful information ("How to...")
- Addressing a specific audience ("To the man who...")
- Promising a specific result ("Lose 10 pounds in...")
- Arousing curiosity ("The secret of...")
- Offering something free or valuable ("Free book tells...")
- Making a surprising claim ("They laughed when...")

### Proven Headline Structures

**How-to headlines:**
"How to [achieve desired outcome] without [common objection]"
"How [specific person/group] [achieved specific result] in [timeframe]"

**Number headlines:**
"[Number] ways to [achieve outcome] (even if [objection])"
"The [number] biggest mistakes [audience] make when [activity]"

**Question headlines:**
"Do you make these [number] mistakes in [area]?"
"What happens when [scenario]?"
"Are you [specific situation the reader identifies with]?"

**Command headlines:**
"Stop [painful action] and start [desired action]"
"Give me [small commitment] and I'll give you [big benefit]"

**Testimonial / third-person headlines:**
"They laughed when I [did something]... but when I [result]!" (Caples classic)
"How a [relatable person] [achieved result] using [method]"

**News/announcement headlines:**
"Introducing the first [mechanism] that [benefit]"
"New [discovery/study/method] reveals [surprising finding]"

**Warning/fear headlines:**
"Warning: [thing they're doing] may be [negative consequence]"
"The hidden danger of [common practice]"

**Fascination headlines (bullet-style hooks):**
"The one [thing] every [audience] should know about [topic] (page X)"
"Why [surprising thing] actually [counterintuitive result]"
"The [adjective] [mechanism] that [specific result] (hint: it's not [obvious thing])"

**Reason-why headlines:**
"[Number] reasons why [claim] (and what to do about it)"
"Why [counterintuitive thing] (and how to use it to [benefit])"

**Challenge headlines:**
"Can you pass this [topic] test?"
"I dare you to [simple action] for [timeframe] and NOT see [result]"

## 5. Schwartz's 38 Headline Verbalization Techniques

From *Breakthrough Advertising* — 38 specific ways to verbalize your headline claim once
you've identified the desire and the market's sophistication level. These are not formulas —
they are WAYS OF EXPRESSING the same underlying promise:

### Intensification Techniques (Making the Claim Stronger)
1. **Measure the size of the claim** — quantify: "Lose 20 pounds," "$47K in 30 days"
2. **Measure the speed of the claim** — add timeframe: "In just 14 days," "Overnight"
3. **Compare the claim** — contrast with alternatives: "3x faster than," "Half the cost of"
4. **Metaphorize the claim** — use vivid imagery: "A tsunami of leads," "Money magnet"
5. **Sensitize the claim** — add sensory detail: "Feel the difference the moment you..."
6. **Demonstrate the claim** — show it in action: "Watch as...", "See for yourself..."
7. **Turn it into a dramatic event** — "The day I discovered...", "The moment everything changed"
8. **Project the claim into the future** — future pace: "Imagine yourself 90 days from now..."
9. **Show consequences of the claim** — "Which means you'll never have to..."
10. **Limit the claim** — paradoxically, limiting makes it more believable:
    "Not for everyone — only for those who..."

### Identification Techniques (Making the Reader See Themselves)
11. **Name the audience** — "Attention: freelancers earning under $100K"
12. **Write them into the claim** — use "you" and "your": "Your first $10K month"
13. **Show their role** — "As a [role], you know..."
14. **Mirror their current state** — "If you're still struggling with..."
15. **Project their desired identity** — "Join the top 1% of..."
16. **Reference their tribe** — use in-group language they identify with

### Gradualization Techniques (Building Belief Step by Step)
17. **Start with the universally accepted** — begin where everyone agrees
18. **Use inclusion questions** — "Have you ever noticed...?" (forces a yes)
19. **Use "if-then" logic** — "If [accepted premise], then [new conclusion]"
20. **Chain of agreements** — build momentum through sequential micro-yeses
21. **The "of course" frame** — present your claim as common sense: "Of course, you already know..."

### Redefinition Techniques (Changing How They See Something)
22. **Simplify the complex** — make the overwhelming feel manageable
23. **Escalate the trivial** — make the overlooked feel critical
24. **Minimize the price** — reframe cost: "Less than your daily coffee"
25. **Maximize the value** — reframe worth: "The same advice that costs $500/hour"
26. **Turn a liability into an asset** — "Yes, it's expensive. Here's why that matters..."
27. **Reframe the problem** — give the problem a new name or new cause

### Mechanization Techniques (Introducing the How)
28. **Name the mechanism** — give your method a proprietary name
29. **Describe the mechanism** — explain how it works in simple terms
30. **Feature the mechanism as the headline** — the mechanism IS the promise
31. **Make the mechanism proprietary** — "Our patented [name] system"

### Concentration Techniques (Positioning Against Alternatives)
32. **Point out what's wrong with alternatives** — without naming competitors directly
33. **Show what's missing from other solutions** — the gap only you fill
34. **Demonstrate why other approaches fail** — the root cause they miss

### Camouflage Techniques (Borrowing Believability)
35. **Editorial format** — look like news, not advertising
36. **Report language** — "A new study reveals...", "Researchers found..."
37. **Understated authority** — let facts speak with deadly sincerity
38. **Testimonial format** — let a customer's voice carry the claim

## 6. Sugarman's 15 Axioms of Copy

From *The Adweek Copywriting Handbook* — 15 axioms that govern how copy works. These are
laws, not suggestions:

1. **Copywriting is a mental process** — the successful execution of which reflects the
   sum total of all your experiences, your specific knowledge, and your ability to mentally
   process that information and transfer it onto paper for the purpose of selling.
2. **All the elements of an ad are designed to do one thing:** get you to read the first
   sentence of the copy.
3. **The sole purpose of the first sentence is to get you to read the second sentence.**
   (The Slippery Slide begins here.)
4. **Your ad layout and the first few paragraphs must create the buying environment**
   most conducive to the sale. (Pre-suasion through design and context.)
5. **Get the reader to say yes** and harmonize with your accurate and truthful statements.
   Build a chain of agreements.
6. **Your copy should be a slippery slide:** every element must be so compelling that the
   reader cannot stop reading until they reach the end.
7. **When you try to resolve curiosity too soon, you lose the reader.** Plant seeds of
   curiosity and delay resolution to maintain momentum.
8. **Copy should be long enough to cause the reader to take the action you request.**
   Not longer. Not shorter.
9. **Every communication should be personal:** from one human being to another, using
   first person, conversational tone.
10. **The ideas presented in copy should flow** in a logical sequence, anticipating
    questions and answering them as if in a conversation.
11. **In the editing process, refine to express what you want with the fewest words.**
    Every word must earn its place.
12. **The more the mind must work to reach the conclusion, the more positive the feeling**
    about the conclusion. (Let them connect the dots; self-generated conclusions are more
    persuasive than stated ones.)
13. **Every element of copy should sell.** The headline sells the first sentence. The first
    sentence sells the second. Every component has a job.
14. **Never sell a product or service. Sell a concept.** Don't sell a thermostat; sell
    comfort, savings, and peace of mind. Find the Big Idea behind the product.
15. **The incubation process is the power of your subconscious mind.** Write the first
    draft, then step away. Let your subconscious process it. The best edits come after
    incubation, not during grinding sessions.

## 7. The Power of the Unique Mechanism

> **See psychology.md Section 2** (Schwartz's Mass Desire) for understanding the desire
> dimensions the mechanism must connect to. **See case-studies.md Section 11** ("The End of
> America" — Stansberry Research) for a masterclass in mechanism-driven copy at the highest
> sophistication level. **See the Research & Market Analysis skill** for how to discover and
> validate mechanisms through competitive audits and VOC mining.

In sophisticated markets (Level 3+), you cannot win with claims alone. You need a
unique mechanism — a proprietary explanation of WHY your solution works differently.

### What a Unique Mechanism Does
- Explains the underlying cause of the problem in a new way
- Introduces a new method, process, ingredient, or system
- Differentiates you from every other claim in the market
- Gives the prospect a reason to believe THIS TIME will be different
- Shifts the conversation from "does this work?" to "how does this work?"

### How to Build One
1. Identify the real reason existing solutions fail (the root cause everyone else misses)
2. Name your approach (proprietary name adds perceived value and memorability)
3. Explain the mechanism in simple, visual terms (use analogies)
4. Show why this mechanism addresses the root cause, not symptoms
5. Support with proof (studies, data, demonstrations, expert endorsement)

Example: Instead of "our supplement helps you sleep," try: "The REM-Reset Protocol
targets the 3 specific neurotransmitter imbalances that cause your brain to stay in
'alert mode' long after your body is exhausted."

### Schwartz's Mechanization Progression
As market sophistication increases, the mechanism evolves:
- **Stage 1-2:** No mechanism needed. Bold claims work.
- **Stage 3:** Name the mechanism. It enters the copy.
- **Stage 4:** The mechanism moves into the headline. It becomes the primary differentiator.
  Elaborate it. Make it proprietary. Name it. Explain how it overcomes prior limitations.
- **Stage 5:** Mechanism alone isn't enough. Combine with identification, story, and
  emotional resonance — or find an untapped sub-market and restart the cycle.

## 8. Offer Architecture

> **See the Funnel Architecture skill** for the complete offer stacking and Value Ladder
> system — this section covers the copy-level architecture; Funnel Architecture covers the
> strategic multi-offer system. **See psychology.md Section 8** (Loss Aversion and the
> Contrast Principle) for why price anchoring works. **See psychology.md Section 10**
> (Decoy Effect, Zero Risk Bias) for cognitive biases that power offer presentation.

A great offer makes the price feel irrelevant. Structure matters.

### The Irresistible Offer Stack
1. **Core offer** — the main product/service and its primary transformation
2. **Bonus stack** — 2-5 additional items that increase perceived value (each with its own
   mini-headline and "value" price). Each bonus should solve an adjacent problem or
   remove a common obstacle on the path to the main result.
3. **Price anchor** — show the total value of everything before revealing the actual price
4. **Price reveal** — the actual investment, positioned against the anchor
5. **Payment options** — installments lower perceived barrier
6. **Guarantee** — risk reversal (see below)
7. **Urgency/scarcity** — deadline, limited spots, disappearing bonuses

### Value Stacking Language
"That's a total value of $X,XXX. But you're not going to pay anywhere near that today.
In fact, when you act now, your total investment is just $XXX — that's less than $X per day."

### Hormozi's Naming Convention for Offer Components
Turn boring deliverables into compelling names:
- "Module 3: Email Templates" → "The 7-Figure Email Arsenal: 47 Proven Templates That
  Generated $2.3M in the Last 12 Months"
- "Weekly Q&A Call" → "The 'Unstuck' Hotline: Direct Access to Get Your Specific
  Questions Answered Within 48 Hours"

The name should communicate the outcome, not the format.

## 9. Guarantee / Risk Reversal Frameworks

The guarantee is not an afterthought — it's a conversion lever. The stronger the guarantee,
the higher the conversion rate and (counterintuitively) the lower the refund rate.
Sugarman proved this through decades of testing.

### Types of Guarantees
- **Money-back guarantee** — standard, 30/60/90 day
- **Double your money back** — extreme confidence signal
- **Conditional guarantee** — "If you [do the work] and don't see [result], we'll [guarantee]"
- **Keep everything guarantee** — "If it's not for you, keep the bonuses and get a full refund"
- **Results guarantee** — "We'll work with you until you [achieve result]"
- **Better-than-risk-free guarantee** — "If you don't love it, we'll refund your money AND
  give you [additional compensation for your time]"

### Positioning the Guarantee
Don't bury it. Name it. Make it bold. It's not a legal formality — it's a sales argument.
"Our 'Sleep Like a Baby or Pay Nothing' 90-Day Guarantee" is more powerful than
"30-day money-back guarantee."

The guarantee serves three purposes simultaneously:
1. Removes risk for the buyer (obvious purpose)
2. Signals supreme confidence in the product (implied proof)
3. Creates a named entity that becomes part of the offer's identity

## 10. CTA Frameworks

Weak CTAs kill conversions. Every piece of copy ends with a CTA — make it unmistakable.

### The CTA Must:
1. Be a clear, specific instruction (not "learn more" — tell them exactly what happens next)
2. Reinforce the key benefit ("Click below to start getting [result] today")
3. Reduce perceived risk ("Try it risk-free for 60 days")
4. Create urgency when appropriate ("Only [X] spots remaining")
5. Tell them what happens AFTER they click (reduces uncertainty)

### CTA Formulas:
- "Click below to [get specific result] — risk free"
- "[Action verb] now and [benefit] in [timeframe]"
- "Yes! I want [specific desired outcome] →"
- "Join [number] [type of people] who already [achieved result]"
- "Get instant access before [deadline/scarcity element]"

### Post-CTA Reinforcement
After the main CTA, add:
- A brief recap of the guarantee
- A "what happens next" description (reduces uncertainty: "Step 1: Click. Step 2: Fill in
  your details. Step 3: Check your email for instant access.")
- One final piece of social proof or urgency

### Multiple CTAs
Place CTAs at multiple points throughout long-form copy:
1. After the solution reveal (for fast, decisive buyers)
2. After the offer stack (the main CTA)
3. After the guarantee
4. At the very bottom of the page (for readers who scrolled to the end)

## 11. The RMBC Method — Systematic Copy Production (Stefan Georgi)

> **See the Research & Market Analysis skill** for a deep-dive on the Research phase —
> VOC mining, avatar profiling, and competitive audits that feed the RMBC research document.
> **See psychology.md Section 3** (Core Emotional Complex) for identifying the emotional
> drivers that inform the Mechanism phase. **See psychology.md Section 11** (Belief Shifting)
> for the six belief gates the Brief must address.

Stefan Georgi has generated over $1 billion in sales through direct-response copy. His
RMBC Method turns copywriting from an art into a repeatable system. He hits an 80% success
rate on cold traffic — most copywriters hit 30-40%. The method is:

### R — Research
Research is where all the magic happens. Most copywriters skip this or rush through it.
Georgi spends more time on research than on writing. Research is the foundation that
makes everything else possible.

**What to research:**
- **Voice of customer data** — Go to forums, Reddit, Amazon reviews, Facebook groups,
  support tickets. Find the exact language your prospects use to describe their pain,
  desires, and frustrations. These become your headline words and body copy phrases.
- **Failed solutions** — What has the prospect already tried? Why did it fail? This
  becomes the foundation for your unique mechanism argument.
- **Emotional drivers** — What keeps them up at night? What are they embarrassed about?
  What do they secretly dream of? What are the feelings, thoughts, and desires that form
  their Core Emotional Complex?
- **Demographic specifics** — Age, situation, sophistication level, platform behavior.
- **Competing claims** — What are competitors saying? What sophistication stage is the market?
- **Scientific/factual backing** — Studies, data, expert opinions that support the mechanism.

**The research output:** A document full of raw quotes, pain points, desire statements,
objections, and language patterns. This is your copy's raw material.

### M — Mechanism
The unique mechanism answers: "Why hasn't anything worked for them before, and why will
THIS work now?" Georgi popularized this as a formal technique and made it the centerpiece
of his method.

**Building the mechanism:**
1. Identify the ROOT CAUSE of the prospect's problem (not the symptoms)
2. Explain why existing solutions only address symptoms (this justifies their failures —
   Blair Warren's second driver)
3. Introduce YOUR explanation of the real cause (name it — proprietary names add power)
4. Show how your product/mechanism addresses the root cause directly
5. This becomes the backbone of your entire sales argument

The mechanism is what makes your copy different from every other ad in the market.
Without it, you're just making claims. With it, you're making an argument.

### B — Brief
Before writing the actual copy, create a brief — a structured outline that pre-sells the
offer. The brief breaks the sales argument into modular blocks:

- The hook / big idea
- The problem articulation (using research language)
- The mechanism explanation
- The solution reveal
- Key proof points (mapped to specific claims)
- The offer stack
- Objection handling sequence
- The close

Writing the brief first means the actual copy becomes a "fill in the blanks" exercise.
The strategic thinking is done. The creative writing is just execution.

### C — Copy
Now you write — and it goes fast because the research, mechanism, and brief did the
heavy lifting. Georgi produced 12 long-form sales letters per month using this system.

**Key Georgi principles during the copy phase:**
- Write the first draft fast. Don't edit while writing. Momentum matters.
- Use your research document's exact language — prospects' own words sell better than yours
- Every section of the copy should map to a block in the brief
- Read aloud. If it doesn't flow conversationally, rewrite.
- Edit ruthlessly in a separate pass — every word must earn its place
- The first 10% of your copy (the lead) determines 80% of the result

## 12. The Value Equation — Grand Slam Offers (Alex Hormozi)

From *$100M Offers*: the mathematical framework for making offers so good people feel
stupid saying no. This belongs in every piece of copy that presents an offer.

### The Value Equation

**Value = (Dream Outcome × Perceived Likelihood of Achievement) / (Time Delay × Effort & Sacrifice)**

The top of the equation creates value. The bottom destroys it.
Your job in copy: maximize the top, minimize the bottom.

### The Four Levers

**Dream Outcome (increase this)**
The vivid, specific future the customer most desires. Not "better health" but "wake up
at 6am with energy, play with your kids without getting winded, and feel proud when you
look in the mirror." Paint it in sensory, emotional detail. The bigger and more specific
the dream outcome, the more valuable the offer feels.

**Perceived Likelihood of Achievement (increase this)**
The customer's belief that they'll actually GET the result. This is why proof matters so
much — testimonials, case studies, demonstrations, guarantees all increase perceived
likelihood. The unique mechanism also increases this: it gives them a logical reason to
believe this time will be different.

Ways to increase in copy:
- Stack specific, relatable testimonials
- Show the methodology (why it works)
- Reduce perceived complexity ("just 3 steps")
- Address their specific identity ("designed for busy parents who...")
- Remove conditional effort ("works even if you have no experience")

**Time Delay (decrease this)**
How long until they see results? Faster = more valuable. "See results in 48 hours" is
worth dramatically more than "see results in 6 months" even if the end result is identical.

Ways to decrease in copy:
- Highlight quick wins ("You'll notice X within the first week")
- Show the speed of the mechanism
- Compress the timeline with bonuses (quick-start guides, templates)
- Contrast with alternatives ("Instead of 6 months of trial and error...")

**Effort & Sacrifice (decrease this)**
How hard is it? What do they have to give up? Easier = more valuable. "No gym required,
no meal prep, no willpower needed" removes effort. "Keep eating the foods you love"
removes sacrifice.

Ways to decrease in copy:
- Done-for-you > done-with-you > do-it-yourself (each level is more valuable)
- Templates, swipe files, plug-and-play tools
- "We handle the [hard part] so you can focus on [easy/fun part]"
- Emphasize what they DON'T have to do or give up

### Hormozi's Offer Construction Process
1. List every problem the prospect faces on the way to their dream outcome
2. For each problem, list every possible solution
3. Select the solutions that are high value but low cost to deliver
4. Bundle them into a "Grand Slam Offer" — core product + bonuses that solve adjacent problems
5. Name each component with a benefit-driven name (not a description)
6. Assign perceived value to each component
7. Stack the total value, then reveal the price against the anchor

## 13. The StoryBrand Framework (Donald Miller)

> **See the Brand Voice skill** for the full StoryBrand BrandScript, one-liner formula,
> and how StoryBrand integrates with broader brand positioning. **See psychology.md Section
> 12** (SUCCESs Framework — Stories) for why story structure bypasses analytical resistance.
> **See psychology.md Section 8** (Loss Aversion) for why Step 6 (Avoid Failure) is
> psychologically more powerful than Step 7 (Ends in Success).

From *Building a StoryBrand*: a 7-part framework based on universal story structure.
The core insight: your customer is the HERO. Your brand is the GUIDE. Most companies
get this backward — they position themselves as the hero, which makes the customer a
spectator instead of the protagonist.

"If you confuse, you lose." — Miller

### The 7-Part StoryBrand Framework

**1. A Character (the customer)** — Define what the customer wants. One clear desire.
Not what YOU want to sell. What THEY want to achieve. This must be stated simply enough
that it could fit on a billboard.

**2. Has a Problem** — The villain in the story. Problems exist on three levels:
- **External problem** — The tangible, surface-level issue (e.g., "my website doesn't convert")
- **Internal problem** — How it makes them FEEL (e.g., "I feel like I'm wasting money and
  time, and I'm embarrassed that my competitors are beating me")
- **Philosophical problem** — Why it's WRONG (e.g., "Great products deserve to be seen.
  Hard-working business owners shouldn't be punished by bad marketing.")

Most copy only addresses the external problem. Elite copy addresses all three — especially
the internal. People buy solutions to internal problems, not external ones.

**3. Meets a Guide (your brand)** — The guide has two qualities:
- **Empathy** — "I understand how frustrating this is because I've been there"
- **Authority** — "And I have the expertise/results to help you solve it"

Lead with empathy. Prove with authority. Never lead with authority alone — it feels
arrogant. The guide earns trust by showing they understand BEFORE showing credentials.

**4. Who Gives Them a Plan** — Reduce confusion and fear by showing a clear path:
- **Process plan** — "Step 1: Book a call. Step 2: We build your strategy. Step 3: Watch
  your revenue grow." (3 steps is ideal — simple enough to remember)
- **Agreement plan** — Commitments that address fears ("We guarantee X" / "No long-term
  contracts" / "Cancel anytime")

Plans reduce the perceived effort and risk (ties directly to Hormozi's Value Equation).

**5. And Calls Them to Action** — Direct CTA ("Buy Now," "Schedule a Call") and
transitional CTAs ("Download the Free Guide") for those not ready to buy. Every page,
email, and ad needs a clear, visible CTA. Don't be shy about asking.

**6. That Helps Them Avoid Failure** — Paint the stakes. What happens if they DON'T act?
What does their life look like in 6 months if nothing changes? The threat of loss
motivates more than the promise of gain (Kahneman's loss aversion). You don't need to
be heavy-handed — just clear about the cost of inaction.

**7. And Ends in Success** — Paint the transformation. Show the "after" state in vivid,
specific, emotional detail. This is the dream outcome. Close the loop of the story:
the hero (customer) succeeds because the guide (you) showed them the way.

### Applying StoryBrand to Copy
- **Sales pages:** Use the 7 parts as your structural outline
- **Headlines:** Express #1 (character's desire) or #2 (the problem)
- **About pages:** Position yourself as the guide (#3), not the hero
- **Email sequences:** Walk the prospect through steps 1-7 across multiple emails
- **Ads:** Hook with #2 (problem), bridge with #3 (guide credibility), CTA with #5

## 14. The Attractive Character & Funnel Architecture (Russell Brunson)

> **See the Funnel Architecture skill** for the complete funnel system — Value Ladder,
> 7 funnel types, Money Model, and traffic strategy. This section covers the copy-level
> elements; Funnel Architecture covers the strategic system design. **See the Email Marketing
> skill** for how Soap Opera and Seinfeld sequences integrate with campaign architecture.

From *DotCom Secrets* and *Expert Secrets*: frameworks for building an audience-brand
relationship and structuring copy within multi-step funnels.

### The Attractive Character
Every brand needs an Attractive Character — a persona that the audience bonds with.

**Four Identity Types** (choose one that fits):
- **The Leader** — "Follow me, I know the way" (authority and vision)
- **The Adventurer/Crusader** — "Let's go on this journey together" (shared exploration)
- **The Reporter/Evangelist** — "I've researched everything so you don't have to" (curation)
- **The Reluctant Hero** — "I didn't want to share this, but..." (humble expertise)

**Elements of the Attractive Character:**
- **Backstory** — Share your origin story. Vulnerability builds connection.
- **Parables** — Teach through stories from your life, not lectures.
- **Character flaws** — Imperfections make you relatable and trustworthy.
- **Polarity** — Take stands. Don't be vanilla. Repelling some people attracts others harder.

### The Epiphany Bridge (Expert Secrets)
The most powerful selling tool is not a logical argument — it's an "epiphany story."
People don't buy because of logic. They buy because they experience an emotional
epiphany — an "aha moment" that shifts their belief. Your job is to take the prospect
across the Epiphany Bridge: from their current belief to the new belief that makes
buying the natural conclusion.

Structure: backstory → journey → epiphany moment → new belief → product as vehicle

### The Soap Opera Sequence (Brunson)
A 5-email welcome sequence modeled on TV soap operas:

**Email 1: Set the stage** — Welcome. Set expectations. Open a loop.
**Email 2: High drama / backstory** — Origin story at highest tension. Build connection.
**Email 3: Epiphany** — The turning point. Introduce your framework or product.
**Email 4: Hidden benefits** — Unexpected results and secondary proof.
**Email 5: Urgency / CTA** — Create urgency. Close the loops.

### The Seinfeld Sequence (Brunson)
After the Soap Opera Sequence, switch to daily "Seinfeld" emails — emails "about nothing"
that entertain, tell stories, and sell daily. Like a TV show that's always running. Maps
to Ben Settle's daily email philosophy (see email-copy.md).

### Funnel Context for Copy
Copy never exists in isolation. It lives inside a funnel:

**Frontend (cold traffic → lead):** Ad → Landing page → Lead magnet → Welcome sequence
- Copy goal: capture attention, build trust fast, deliver quick value

**Core (lead → customer):** Sales page or VSL → Order form → Upsell → Downsell
- Copy goal: shift beliefs, present the offer, close the sale

**Backend (customer → repeat buyer):** Email nurture → New offers → High-ticket
- Copy goal: deepen relationship, increase lifetime value

Understanding WHERE your copy sits in the funnel determines everything: length, tone,
awareness level, aggressiveness of the CTA.

## 15. Voice of Customer Mining (Joanna Wiebe / Copy Hackers)

> **See the Research & Market Analysis skill** for the complete VOC mining system — this
> section covers the copy-level application; Research & Market Analysis covers the full
> research methodology, avatar profiling, and competitive audit process. **See psychology.md
> Section 4** (Cialdini's Liking Principle) for why mirroring the reader's language builds
> trust. **See psychology.md Section 9** (System 1) for why familiar, concrete language
> activates cognitive ease and increases believability.

The core insight: the best copy isn't written — it's assembled from the exact words
your customers already use.

### The VOC Research Process

**Step 1: Mine Customer Language**
Go where your customers talk about their problems and desires:
- Amazon reviews of competing products (1-star and 5-star reviews are gold)
- Reddit threads, Quora questions, niche forums
- Customer support tickets and chat logs
- Survey responses (open-ended questions)
- Sales call transcripts and recordings
- Social media comments on competitor content
- Testimonial requests (ask existing customers WHY they bought)

**Step 2: Identify Patterns**
Look for:
- **Repeated phrases** — If 15 people use the same words to describe a pain point,
  those words belong in your headline
- **Emotional language** — The specific adjectives they use (not your marketing jargon)
- **Before/after descriptions** — How they describe their life before vs after
- **Objections** — What almost stopped them from buying
- **Unexpected benefits** — Results they got that they didn't expect

**Step 3: Use Their Words, Not Yours**
The highest-converting headlines are often near-verbatim quotes from customer research.
This works because:
- It creates instant "that's exactly how I feel!" recognition
- It signals you deeply understand their world
- It uses language their brain already associates with the problem
- It bypasses the "this sounds like marketing" filter

### The Conversion Copywriting Hierarchy (Wiebe)
When optimizing existing copy, change these in order of impact:
1. **Messaging** — Are you saying the right things? (biggest impact)
2. **Structure** — Are you saying them in the right order?
3. **Design/layout** — Is it easy to read and navigate?
4. **Copy** — Are you saying them in the most compelling way? (smallest impact of the four)

Always start by validating that you're addressing the right pain point with the right
promise before polishing the prose.

## 16. Collier's Six Essentials of Successful Sales Letters

Robert Collier identified six elements every successful sales letter must have. From
*The Robert Collier Letter Book*:

1. **The Opening** — Enter the conversation already happening in the reader's mind. Collier:
   "Always enter the conversation already taking place in the customer's mind." This is the
   Collier Principle — your copy must start where the reader already IS, not where you want
   them to be. What are they thinking about RIGHT NOW? Start there.

2. **The Description or Explanation** — Not of your product, but of what your product DOES
   for the reader. Paint the picture of the result. Make it vivid.

3. **The Motive or Reason Why** — WHY should they act? WHY now? Give a compelling reason
   that makes the action feel logical and justified.

4. **The Proof or Guarantee** — Evidence that your claims are real. Collier emphasized that
   proof should feel natural, not bolted on.

5. **The Snapper or Penalty** — What do they lose by NOT acting? The cost of inaction.
   Scarcity, urgency, or a vivid picture of what continues if they don't respond.

6. **The Close** — A clear, simple, direct request for action. Make it easy. Remove friction.

### The Collier Principle in Practice
The most important of these is the opening — entering the reader's existing conversation.
Before writing any copy, ask: "What is my prospect thinking and feeling RIGHT NOW, in the
moment they encounter this copy?" Match that. Then redirect.

## 17. Chet Holmes' Buyer's Pyramid & Stadium Pitch

> **See psychology.md Section 1** (Awareness Levels) for how Holmes' Buyer's Pyramid maps
> directly to Schwartz's awareness spectrum. **See the Funnel Architecture skill** for how
> to design funnels that capture all five tiers of the pyramid, not just the top 3%.

From *The Ultimate Sales Machine*:

### The Buyer's Pyramid
At any given time, your market breaks down as follows:
- **3% Buying Now** — ready to purchase, actively looking
- **7% Open to It** — aware of the problem, open to solutions
- **30% Not Thinking About It** — have the problem but aren't focused on it
- **30% Don't Think They're Interested** — would say "no" if asked
- **30% Definitely Not Interested** — firm no

Most marketing targets only the top 3%. But 90% of the market is in the bottom three
tiers. Education-based marketing (Holmes' "Stadium Pitch") allows you to reach ALL tiers
by leading with valuable information instead of a sales pitch.

### The Stadium Pitch Strategy
Imagine you could get your entire market into a stadium. What would you say? NOT a sales
pitch — but a presentation so valuable that everyone would want to stay and listen,
regardless of where they are on the pyramid.

Structure:
1. Lead with industry data and insights (not about your product)
2. Reveal problems they didn't know they had
3. Educate on the real causes and real solutions
4. Position your product as the natural conclusion of the education

This is Schwartz's Indirection strategy applied to content marketing: by the time you
reveal the product, the prospect has already accepted the premises that make buying
logical.

## 18. Hopkins' Rules of Scientific Advertising

> **See case-studies.md Section 10** (Hopkins' Pepsodent Campaign) and **Section 12**
> (Schlitz Beer Campaign) for Hopkins' principles in action. **See the Data Analysis skill**
> for modern measurement and testing systems that extend Hopkins' "test everything" philosophy.

From Claude Hopkins' *Scientific Advertising* — the principles that founded modern
direct-response advertising:

1. **Advertising is salesmanship in print.** It follows the same rules as face-to-face
   selling. If it wouldn't work in person, it won't work in copy.
2. **The only purpose of advertising is to sell.** Not to entertain, not to win awards,
   not to be clever. To sell.
3. **Be specific.** "Lose 3.5 inches" not "lose weight." Specificity IS proof.
4. **Offer service.** The best ads ask no one to buy. They offer wanted information.
   They describe advantages. They site users to tell what the product has done for them.
5. **Headlines are the selectors.** They select the right audience from the mass of
   readers. A headline about "acid indigestion" selects only the people with that problem.
6. **Test everything.** Almost any question can be answered cheaply and quickly by a test.
   The thousands decide what the millions will do.
7. **Use samples and demonstrations.** Let people experience the product. The product
   itself is its best salesperson.
8. **Be a humble servant, not a clever performer.** Hopkins' most quoted line: "Don't try
   to be amusing. Money-spending is a serious matter."
9. **Track and measure.** Hopkins invented the coupon code (keyed ads). Every claim should
   be measured against results.
10. **Study your audience more than your product.** Hopkins spent weeks studying prospects
    before writing a word of copy.
